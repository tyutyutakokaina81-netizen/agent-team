Who this is for

Keyword research tools serve different people:

  • Bloggers and solo creators who want to find topics real people search for.
  • In-house marketers who need to track rankings and report to a boss.
  • Agencies and freelancers managing several client sites at once.
  • Operators like us who feed keyword data into content pipelines and want an export-friendly, API-capable tool.

Semrush is aimed mostly at the middle-to-upper end of that list. It's a broad SEO suite rather than a single-purpose keyword tool, which is both its strength and its cost.

What Semrush actually does

Based on its public documentation, Semrush covers a wide range of tasks:

  • Keyword research — the Keyword Magic Tool surfaces related terms, questions, and long-tail variants, with search volume, difficulty scores, and intent labels.
  • Competitor analysis — you can look at the keywords a rival domain ranks for and spot gaps.
  • Rank tracking — daily position monitoring for chosen keywords and locations.
  • Site audits — technical crawls that flag on-page issues.
  • Backlink and content tools — link analysis, a writing assistant, and topic research.

For keyword research specifically, the appeal is depth: large keyword databases across many countries, filters for question-based queries, and difficulty estimates that help you judge whether a term is realistically winnable.

How to approach keyword research with a tool like this

Whatever tool you land on, the workflow is similar. Here's a practical sequence:

1. Start with a seed term close to your topic (e.g., "keyword research tool").

2. Expand into clusters using the tool's related-keywords view. Group by intent — informational, commercial, transactional.

3. Filter by difficulty and volume. Low difficulty plus steady volume is usually where small sites should start.

4. Check the SERP for a few target terms manually. Tools estimate difficulty, but the actual results page tells you who you're competing with.

5. Export and organize. A good tool lets you export to CSV or pull data via API so you can build a content calendar without re-typing anything.

Semrush supports this workflow, including CSV export and an API on higher tiers — worth confirming which tier you need, since API access has historically sat behind more expensive plans.

Strengths, honestly

  • Breadth. If you want one subscription that covers keywords, audits, rank tracking, and competitor research, having it in one place saves context-switching.
  • Data volume. Large keyword and backlink databases mean you rarely hit a dead end on suggestions.
  • Mature ecosystem. Lots of tutorials, integrations, and community knowledge exist because the product has been around a long time.

Weaknesses, honestly

  • Price. Semrush sits at the higher end. Advertised plans have typically started around the low-to-mid hundreds of dollars per month (2026 — verify current pricing). For a hobby blog, that's hard to justify.
  • Complexity. The breadth that helps agencies can overwhelm a beginner who just wants ten good keywords.
  • Metric caveats. Search volume and difficulty scores from any tool are *estimates*, modeled from clickstream and other signals. They're useful for ranking priorities, not gospel. Cross-check important decisions.
  • Seat and limit gotchas. Extra users, extra tracked keywords, and historical data can cost more. Read the plan limits carefully before committing.

Who it's for / not for

A reasonable fit if you:

  • Manage SEO professionally or for clients and need more than just keywords.
  • Want competitor and backlink data alongside keyword research.
  • Can use — and will actually use — a broad toolset regularly enough to justify the cost.

Probably not for you if you:

  • Run a single small blog and mainly need topic ideas. A cheaper or free tool (or a limited free trial) may be enough.
  • Are on a tight budget and won't touch the audit, backlink, or rank-tracking features.
  • Want a simple, one-screen keyword tool with a gentle learning curve.

A sensible move is to try a free trial (if one is available at the time you read this), run your real keyword list through it, and see whether the extra features earn their keep before you pay for a full month.

Bottom line

Semrush is a capable, wide-ranging SEO platform, and its keyword research is legitimately strong for people who'll use the rest of the suite. It is not the cheapest or the simplest option, and it isn't the only good tool — the right choice depends on your budget and how much of the platform you'll actually use. Confirm current pricing and limits yourself before deciding, since both shift over time.
