## Who this is for

- L&D and HR teams turning SOPs and policies into watchable onboarding videos.
- Solo creators and small businesses who need consistent talking-head content without filming.
- Localization-heavy orgs that must ship the same course in several languages.

If you need cinematic brand films, emotive acting, or a real spokesperson's face and voice, an AI avatar is usually the wrong tool.

## What Synthesia does well

- **Speed from script to video.** Paste text, choose an avatar and voice, and render. For a documentation-heavy training library, this collapses a multi-day shoot into an afternoon.
- **Large avatar and voice library.** Publicly, Synthesia advertises a wide range of stock avatars and support for many languages, which is genuinely useful for multi-market teams.
- **Editing that feels like slides, not video.** The scene-based editor is approachable for non-editors — closer to building a deck than to using Premiere.
- **Custom / personal avatars.** Higher tiers let you create an avatar of a real person (with consent). Handy if you want a consistent company presenter across dozens of modules.
- **Update without re-shooting.** Change a line of the script and re-render. For training content that goes stale (tool names, policies, prices), this is the strongest practical argument for the whole category.

## Where it falls short

- **It still reads as an avatar.** Quality has improved a lot, but expressive, natural delivery has limits. Viewers can often tell. For internal training that is usually fine; for public-facing brand trust, weigh it carefully.
- **Cost scales with minutes and seats.** Plans are typically metered by video minutes and features gate behind higher tiers. Heavy volume gets expensive — confirm current limits.
- **Not built for complex motion.** Screen-recordings, product demos, and dynamic B-roll are not its strength. Many teams pair it with a separate screen-capture tool.
- **Consent and disclosure overhead.** Custom avatars and AI voices raise real policy questions. You need a process for consent and, in many contexts, for disclosing that content is AI-generated.

## A realistic workflow

Here is the sequence we would use, and the one most training teams converge on:

1. **Write the script first, in plain text.** The avatar is only as good as the words. This is also where an automated setup shines — draft scripts programmatically, review them, then feed the finals in.
2. **Standardize on one avatar and voice** per course series so modules feel consistent.
3. **Build scene by scene**, keeping each scene to one idea. Short scenes render faster and are easier to update later.
4. **Add on-screen text and captions** for accessibility — do not rely on the voice alone.
5. **Render one module, get feedback, then batch the rest.** Do not produce twenty videos before checking that tone and pacing land.

## How to evaluate it honestly before paying

- Run the **free trial or demo** with your actual script, not the vendor's sample text. Sample scripts are chosen to flatter the tool.
- Test your **hardest language** and your **most jargon-heavy paragraph** — that is where AI narration tends to stumble on pronunciation.
- Price it against **one month of real volume**, not the headline plan.
- Check the **terms around custom avatars, voice cloning, and commercial use** for your region.

## Who it's for / not for

**A good fit if you:** produce a steady stream of internal training, onboarding, or explainer content; need multi-language versions of the same material; and value the ability to update a video by editing text.

**Probably not for you if you:** need emotionally resonant, public-facing brand videos; do mostly screen-demo or motion-heavy content; or only make a handful of videos a year, where the subscription is hard to justify.

## Bottom line

For turning written training material into consistent, updatable talking-head videos, Synthesia is a credible, mature option — especially for teams that live in text-first, repeatable workflows. It will not replace a human presenter for high-trust brand work, and you should verify current pricing and avatar/consent terms yourself before committing. Test it on your own worst-case script, and let the result — not the marketing — decide.
