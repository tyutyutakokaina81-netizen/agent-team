Riding the Toyama Trams: Fares, Day Pass, How to Board

I live in the Takaoka area and take trams in and around Toyama most weeks. If you have searched for how the Portram, Centram, and the city's tram lines work — what they cost, whether a day pass pays off, and how to actually get on and off — this is the plain version, written from daily use rather than a brochure.

The lines, in plain terms

Toyama City runs a compact tram network operated by Toyama Chiho Railway. Two names you will see printed on the cars are Centram (the low-floor loop-style cars that run through the central part of the city) and Portram (the light-rail line that heads toward the bay). A few years ago the north and south sides of the network were connected through the main station, so you can often ride across the city without the transfer that older guidebooks describe. Because the exact route names and colors shift from time to time, check the current line map at the station on the day you travel — 要確認.

The trams are street-level for much of the route: you wait at a platform or a marked stop, not inside a big station building. On the newer low-floor cars the step is close to flat, which helps with luggage or a stroller.

Fares: how paying works

Inside the central city zone, most single rides are a flat fare — you pay the same amount whether you go one stop or several within that zone. As of now that flat fare sits around ¥210 for adults, but fares change, so treat that as a guide and confirm the posted amount — 要確認. Children are typically about half.

How you board and pay depends on the car:

  • Board at the rear or marked door, and exit at the front near the driver on many cars. Watch what other passengers do; it is consistent along a given line.
  • Pay when you get off, not when you get on.
  • You can pay with coins into the fare box, or tap a transit IC card. National IC cards (the Suica/ICOCA family) are generally accepted here, but coverage on every car and line is not something I would promise blind — 諸説あります, so carry some coins as backup.
  • If you need change, use the coin changer on the fare box before your stop, not while everyone is waiting to get off.

The day pass, and when it actually pays off

Toyama Chiho Railway sells a one-day pass for its tram lines. The price has been in the neighborhood of ¥650 for adults in recent years, but this is exactly the kind of number that gets revised, so check at the ticket window or on the current official notice — 要確認.

Here is the honest math from how I use it. If a single ride is roughly ¥210, then:

  • Two rides = about ¥420 → the pass does not pay off.
  • Three rides = about ¥630 → roughly break-even.
  • Four or more rides = the pass starts saving money.

So the pass makes sense on a day when you hop on and off several times — say, station → a museum stop → lunch somewhere → the bay line → back. If your plan is just "ride out in the morning, ride back at night," two single fares are cheaper. For a lot of visitors doing one loop, the pass is a convenience, not a saving.

Small practical notes

  • Trams can be crowded at commuter times (early morning, around 6 p.m.). Off-peak is calmer for photos and luggage.
  • Stops are announced, but the display is quicker to read than the audio if your Japanese is limited.
  • Give yourself buffer time — trams run frequently but are subject to street traffic and signals.
