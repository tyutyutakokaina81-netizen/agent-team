Food · Culture · Toyama, Japan

The town that wraps rice balls in kelp, not seaweed

Almost everyone who loves Japanese food knows the onigiri — the triangular rice ball wrapped in a crisp sheet of dark nori seaweed. So here's a small surprise from the Sea of Japan coast: in Toyama, people often skip the nori entirely and wrap their rice balls in soft, fluffy tororo kombu — finely shaved kelp — instead. It looks like the rice ball is wearing a pale, feathery cloud. And it's delicious. Here's the story behind one of Japan's most charming regional food quirks.

Why kelp instead of seaweed?

It comes down to history. Toyama barely harvests any kelp of its own — but for centuries it was a key port on the old kitamae-bune trade routes that carried premium kelp down from Hokkaido. Kelp piled up here, local artisans learned to shave it paper-thin, and a deep kelp culture took root. Today Toyama has one of the highest kelp-consumption rates in all of Japan. Wrapping onigiri in shaved kelp is just one delicious expression of that obsession.

What does it taste like?

Where nori is crisp and faintly smoky, tororo kombu is soft, melting and savoury, with a gentle, building umami. As it sits against the warm rice it half-dissolves, so the kelp flavour seeps into every bite rather than staying on the outside. It's comforting in a way that's hard to describe until you've tried it — the kind of simple thing locals get quietly homesick for.

How to try it at home

It couldn't be easier: shape a warm rice ball (with a salty filling like pickled plum, salmon or bonito flakes), then press a loose handful of tororo kombu gently all around the outside until it's coated. A pinch of salt helps the umami pop. The same kelp also makes an instant soup — just put a pinch in a bowl with a little soy sauce and pour over hot water. Where to taste the real thing

You'll find tororo kombu onigiri and kelp dishes all over Toyama — in shops, markets and home kitchens. It pairs perfectly with the bay's other treasures: white shrimp, kelp-cured sashimi and local sushi. The easiest way to dive into Toyama's food culture is to stay a night and eat your way around the bay.
