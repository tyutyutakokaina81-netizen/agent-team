Toyama, Japan — start here

This guide has grown to nearly a hundred pages, which is wonderful if you already know what you're looking for and a bit much if you don't. So this page is the front door. I live in Takaoka, and I've laid the whole thing out in the order a first-time visitor actually needs it — where Toyama is, how to get here, how long to stay, what to eat, and the handful of things that make the area worth the small detour. Each step links to the detailed page, so read this once, then follow the threads that matter to you.

First: where is this, and is it worth it?

Toyama is a coastal prefecture on the Sea of Japan, tucked between Kanazawa and the Northern Alps. It isn't a headline destination, and honestly that's the appeal — the crowds thin out the moment you step off the main Kanazawa–Kyoto track. Two smaller cities carry most of the character: Takaoka, a 400-year metal-casting town, and Himi, a working fishing port on the bay. If you're weighing whether it earns a slot in a tight itinerary, I've written an honest answer rather than a sales pitch: is Toyama worth visiting? and seven reasons to choose Toyama over the usual route.

Getting here

Most people arrive by train, and it's simpler than the "off the beaten path" label suggests. From Tokyo it's a direct bullet train; from Kansai it's one logical transfer.

How many days?

Two nights is the sweet spot for most people — enough for one mountain day and one coast-and-food day without rushing. If the Alpine Route is open and you want to slow down, three works better. Where to base yourself

For train access and convenience, Toyama City is the easy default — compact, tram-friendly, hotels clustered near the station. If you'd rather trade convenience for sea views and a crab-season dinner, a seaside inn toward Himi is the nicer choice. The full comparison of city versus coast versus craft-town is in where to stay in Toyama.

What to eat

This is the part I'd travel for. Toyama Bay is one of Japan's richest fishing grounds, and the food leans local rather than showy. Start with the overall food guide, then chase whatever's in season:

  • White shrimp (shiroebi) — tiny, delicately sweet, best as sashimi or kakiage tempura.
  • Winter yellowtail (buri) — Himi's cold-season pride, rich and fatty.
  • Red snow crab — sweet, and at its best in the winter months.

None of it is dressed up. A modest counter with fish this fresh will out-eat a fancier place inland.

The manga towns

Here's the quiet surprise most guidebooks skip. Takaoka is the hometown of Doraemon's creator, and Himi is tied to Ninja Hattori-kun — both threads you can actually walk. The overview is in the manga pilgrimage guide, with the two towns detailed separately: Doraemon's Takaoka and Ninja Hattori-kun's Himi. You can even ride the Doraemon tram out toward the port on Takaoka's little Manyosen line. It's a light, low-key thing rather than a theme park, which is exactly why it's charming.

Mountains and coast

The scenery is the other reason to come. In season, the Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route climbs from foothills to a high snow corridor — check when it opens and when the snow wall is best, and how to cross it in a single day. High on the route sits Kurobe Dam with its thundering summer discharge, while lower down the Kurobe Gorge runs a tiny railway deep into a ravine (people mix the two up constantly). Down on the coast, the Amaharashi Coast gives you the region's signature view — snow-capped peaks rising straight out of the sea on a clear winter morning. For a different pace, Gokayama's thatched villages sit quieter than their famous neighbour; you can reach them on the World Heritage Bus from Takaoka and even stay overnight in a thatched farmhouse.

Before you go — the practical bits

A few small things smooth the trip. Time your visit with the season-by-season guide (spring for the snow corridor, winter for the seafood). Sort out cash, cards and IC passes, since some smaller spots still prefer cash, and a SIM or eSIM for maps and trains. If a specific worry is nagging at you, the travel FAQ probably covers it.
