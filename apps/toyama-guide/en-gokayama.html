Travel · Gokayama, Toyama

Gokayama — UNESCO thatched villages, without the crowds

Everyone has seen the photos of Shirakawa-go: steep thatched roofs, like hands pressed together in prayer, dusted with snow. What most travelers don't realize is that the same UNESCO World Heritage site includes two more villages just over the prefectural border in Toyama — Gokayama — and they're smaller, older-feeling, and far, far quieter. If you want the storybook village without the tour-bus crush, this is where a local would send you.

What Gokayama actually is

Gokayama is a remote, mountainous corner of Toyama Prefecture, home to two thatched-roof hamlets — Ainokura and Suganuma — built in the distinctive gassho-zukuri ("praying hands") style. Together with Shirakawa-go, they were inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage list in 1995. These are living villages, not a museum: people still farm here, and some houses take in overnight guests.

Why come here instead of Shirakawa-go

Shirakawa-go is justly famous — and on a busy day, it can feel like it. Gokayama gives you the same architecture and a deeper hush: narrow lanes, the smell of woodsmoke, mountains close on every side, and often just a handful of other visitors. It feels less like a photo stop and more like a place that simply kept living the way it always has.

Things to do

  • Ainokura — the larger hamlet, with a cluster of gassho houses and a viewpoint that takes in the whole village against the mountains.
  • Suganuma — smaller and lower by the river, intimate and quiet.
  • Stay the night — a few thatched farmhouses offer simple lodging and home-style meals; waking up in the village after the day-trippers leave is the real magic.
  • Local craft & song — the area is known for handmade washi paper and old folk songs still performed today.

How to get there

Gokayama sits in the mountains south of Takaoka. The usual approach is by bus from the Shin-Takaoka / Takaoka area, or as a stop between Kanazawa and Shirakawa-go / Takayama. There's no train to the villages themselves, so check bus times carefully and allow a relaxed pace — this is not a place to rush.

Where to stay

You can visit as a day trip, but staying overnight — either in a village farmhouse or back down in Takaoka or Toyama city — lets you see the hamlets at their quietest, early and late in the day. Book farmhouse stays well ahead; there are only a few rooms.

When to go

Winter, with snow on the thatch, is the iconic look (and the area gets serious snow). Spring and autumn are mild and beautiful, and summer is green and cool in the mountains. Whenever you come, mornings and evenings — after the day crowds thin — are the quietest.
