Johana: A Silk-and-Festival Town on the Way to Gokayama

Most people heading into the mountains of southern Toyama have one destination in mind: the thatched-roof villages of Gokayama, a UNESCO World Heritage site. Johana is the town you pass through just before the road starts climbing. I live up the coast in Takaoka, and when I drive friends down to Gokayama I almost always stop here first. It is small, quiet, and easy to skip — which is exactly why it is worth an hour or two of your time.

Why the town exists at all

Johana grew up around silk. For a long stretch of its history the town was a center for silk weaving and dyeing, and you can still feel that in the older streets: narrow lanes, wooden shopfronts, and workshops that have been in the same families for generations. A few places still weave and sell silk textiles today, though it is a much smaller trade than it once was. If textiles interest you, this is a good place to slow down and actually watch how the cloth is made, rather than just buy a souvenir.

The town also sits at a natural threshold. The plain flattens out to the north; to the south the land folds up into the valleys that lead to Gokayama and, eventually, over into Gifu. That "gateway" feeling is real. Johana is where the ordinary rice-country landscape hands you over to the mountains.

Walking the old center

You don't need a plan here. Park once and walk. The core of the old town is compact enough to cover on foot in an hour, and the pleasure is in the small things — a temple gate at the end of a lane, a shop selling local sweets, the sound of a loom if you catch one working. There is a large temple complex near the center that has historically anchored the town, and its grounds are worth a few minutes even if you're not there for anything in particular.

Because Johana isn't a major tourist stop, it stays calm on ordinary days. You will see more locals than visitors. That is part of the appeal, but it also means you should not expect a dense row of cafes or English menus everywhere. Come with a little patience.

The festival

Johana is probably best known outside the region for its float festival, the Johana Hikiyama Matsuri, held in spring. Tall, ornate wooden floats — hikiyama — are pulled through the streets, and there is a tradition of puppet or lantern elements associated with the celebration. It draws a real crowd on those days, and the town feels completely different from its usual quiet.

I want to be honest about the limits of what I can tell you: I know the festival happens in spring and centers on these floats, but I'd check the exact dates and program for the year you're visiting before building a trip around it, since details and timing can shift.

Using it as a Gokayama stop

The practical case for Johana is simple. If you're driving to Gokayama, this is the last comfortable place to stop for food, a coffee, or a walk before the villages, which are beautiful but very small and have limited services. Fill up, stretch your legs, look at some silk, then continue up the valley. On the way back down, it's a gentler place to decompress than pushing straight through to the city.

Fact-check note

  • ✅ The town has a documented history connected to silk weaving and dyeing.
