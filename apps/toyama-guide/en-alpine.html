Travel · Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route

The Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route, from the Toyama side

Imagine a bus inching through a canyon carved out of snow, with white walls rising higher than a four-storey building on either side. That's the Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route in spring — one of Japan's most extraordinary mountain journeys, and a lot easier to reach than most travelers realize. The route climbs from the lowlands up over the Northern Japan Alps using a chain of cable cars, ropeways and buses. Here's how a local would ride it from the Toyama side, and how to pair it with the quiet coast at Takaoka and Himi.

What the Alpine Route actually is

It's not a single train or road — it's a relay. You cross the mountains by switching between mountain railways, a cableway, a long ropeway with no support towers, and buses through the high passes. Private cars can't drive the whole thing, which is part of why the scenery stays so clean. The journey links Toyama Prefecture with Nagano on the far side, climbing to around 2,450m at its highest point, Murodo.

The famous snow corridor (and the other seasons)

SeasonWhat you get
Spring (mid-Apr–Jun)The "snow corridor" (Yuki-no-Otani) — towering walls of snow beside the road at Murodo. The signature sight.
SummerCool alpine air, hiking, wildflowers and ponds high above the heat of the cities.
AutumnFiery mountain colour, some of the earliest in Japan.
WinterThe route is closed; the mountains rest under deep snow.

Opening dates shift slightly each year and sections can pause for weather, so always check the official schedule before you commit.

Riding it from Toyama

From Toyama city (on the Hokuriku Shinkansen, ~2 hours from Tokyo), a local mountain railway carries you to the foot of the route, where the climb begins. You can travel the whole way across to Nagano, or go up to Murodo and come back down the Toyama side — a good option if you're basing yourself on the coast. It's popular, so reserving ahead in peak spring and autumn is wise.

Make a trip of it: mountains + coast

Here's the move most visitors miss. Pair the high mountains with the low coast. After the Alpine Route, drop back down to Toyama and spend a day on the bay — historic metal-craft Takaoka and the fishing port of Himi, where on a clear day those same Tateyama peaks float above the sea. You'll have seen the range from 2,450m up and from sea level in a single trip. See the one-day coast itinerary for how.
