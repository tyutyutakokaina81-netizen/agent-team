Travel · Getting to Shirakawa-go & Gokayama

Shirakawa-go & Gokayama from Takaoka — the World Heritage Bus

Almost every guide sends you to the thatched villages from Kanazawa or from Takayama. Those work — but they're the crowded doors. From here on the Toyama coast, the quiet way in is the World Heritage Bus, which runs up from the Takaoka area into the mountains, threading through Gokayama and on to Shirakawa-go. If you're already in Toyama, you don't need to backtrack to Kanazawa to see the villages. This is how a local would actually do it.

What the World Heritage Bus is

It's a scheduled highway bus that connects the Takaoka side of Toyama with the UNESCO villages. The line climbs south out of the city and into the valleys, stopping at the Gokayama hamlets on the way before reaching Shirakawa-go. Because it starts on the Toyama coast rather than in Kanazawa or Takayama, far fewer overseas visitors know about it — which is exactly its charm. You get the same World Heritage villages with a calmer bus and, often, a calmer arrival.

Where it starts and roughly how long it takes

The bus is designed to connect with the Shinkansen at the main station on the Takaoka side, so it fits neatly if you're arriving by fast train from Tokyo or Kanazawa. From that station up to the Gokayama hamlets is a mountain ride of well under an hour in normal conditions, and on to Shirakawa-go is a bit more — but I won't hand you exact minutes here, because road and season shift them, and timetables get revised. Treat it as "a comfortable morning ride up, an afternoon among the villages, an evening back." For the exact departure times and journey lengths, check the current operator timetable before you commit a day to it. [times: confirm on the official site]

Tickets — what to know before you board

There are a few ticket types on this corridor: plain single fares between points, and value tickets that bundle round trips or let you break the journey at Gokayama and Shirakawa-go on the same day. The bundled options are usually the sensible buy if you want to see both a Gokayama hamlet and Shirakawa-go in one outing, rather than paying leg by leg. Some seats are reservation-based in busy seasons, so it's worth sorting your seat ahead in autumn foliage and the winter light-up periods.

I'm deliberately not quoting yen figures or the exact number of ticket variants, because the fare table and product names are set by the operator and are revised periodically — quoting a stale price helps no one. Why start from Takaoka rather than Kanazawa

Two honest reasons. First, if you're already based on the Toyama coast, going via Kanazawa means doubling back — the Takaoka approach saves you that. Second, the Gokayama hamlets sit on this route, so starting here makes it natural to see the quieter Toyama-side villages, not just the famous one. Most day-trippers skip Gokayama entirely; coming up from Takaoka, you almost trip over it. If you only have time for one village and want fewer crowds, that's a point in this route's favour — I've laid out the trade-off in Gokayama vs Shirakawa-go.

Fitting it into a Toyama trip

A clean version: fast train into the Takaoka-side station in the morning, World Heritage Bus up, a couple of unhurried hours in the villages, bus back to the coast by evening for dinner by the bay. If you'd rather wake up among the thatch, ride up and stay — that's a different and, honestly, better experience, covered in the overnight farmhouse guide.
