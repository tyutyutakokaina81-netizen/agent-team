Takaoka & Toyama to Shirakawa-go and Gokayama by bus

Most guides send you to Shirakawa-go from Kanazawa or from Takayama, so if you are staying on the Toyama side you might assume you have to double back. You do not. There is a bus that runs from the Takaoka area straight up into the mountains, and it is the one I would point a visiting friend to. This page is about that route, written from here rather than from a route map.

What the World Heritage Bus is

The World Heritage Bus (Sekai Isan Bus, run by the local operator Kaetsuno) links the Takaoka area with the Gokayama villages and continues on to Shirakawa-go. It leaves from near Takaoka Station, which is the part that gets left out of the usual write-ups. If you are based in Takaoka or Toyama city, that means you can reach both World Heritage areas without first travelling out to Kanazawa or over to Takayama just to catch a connecting service.

That is really the whole reason this route is worth knowing. It saves the backtrack. For a lot of travellers the day otherwise turns into "go west to go east," and the local bus quietly removes that.

The order things come in

Coming from the Takaoka side the geography is simple, and this is one thing I can tell you without a timetable in hand. Gokayama comes first. The bus climbs south into the Shokawa valley, passes the Gokayama gassho-zukuri villages, and Shirakawa-go sits further on beyond them, across the prefectural line into Gifu.

It is worth sitting with that for a second, because it changes how you'd plan the day. Gokayama is the quieter, smaller pair of hamlets and it is the one you reach sooner; Shirakawa-go is larger, busier and further. If you only have the appetite for one, you pass the calmer one on the way to the crowded one — which is exactly the trade-off I try to lay out in how the two villages compare.

Planning it honestly

Here is the part I would be doing a disservice to skip. This is a mountain bus, not a city one, so there are a limited number of runs through the day. In practice that means your return time decides your visit more than your arrival does — work out which bus you need to be back on before you commit to wandering. It is an easy thing to lose track of once you are standing among the farmhouses.

In busier seasons a reservation may be advisable on some services; I would not state that as a fixed rule, because it is the operator's to set, so treat it as something to check rather than assume. And winter matters up there. Snow is heavy in the Shokawa valley and it can affect how the buses run, so a January plan is not a July plan.

For how the bus fits with trains and the rest of the region, I keep the wider picture in getting around Toyama.

Fares and timetables — check, don't trust me

I have deliberately not printed times or prices on this page. World Heritage Bus fares and timetables are revised periodically, and anything I quoted today could be wrong by the time you read it. Please confirm the current schedule, fares and any reservation details on the official operator (Kaetsuno / World Heritage Bus) information before you travel. A guide that is honest about what it does not know is more use to you than one that sounds certain.

Consider staying the night

If there is one thing that unlocks this route, it is not being ruled by the last bus. The villages change completely once the day-trippers leave — the light drops, the valley goes quiet, and you get the place more or less to yourself. Booking a night in a gassho farmhouse takes all the timetable anxiety out of the trip; I have written about that in staying overnight in a gassho farmhouse. Even one night turns a rushed connection into an actual visit.
