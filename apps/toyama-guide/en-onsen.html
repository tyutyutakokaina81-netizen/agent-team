Toyama hot springs (onsen)

After a day on the mountains or eating your way around the bay, there's no better way to end it in Japan than sinking into a hot spring. Toyama is quietly well-stocked with them — from a famous gorge-side resort to small mountain and seaside baths where you'll often have the view almost to yourself. Here's a local's guide to soaking in Toyama, plus the etiquette basics if it's your first time.

Where to soak

  • Unazuki Onsen — Toyama's best-known hot-spring town, at the mouth of the dramatic Kurobe Gorge. Clear water, riverside inns, and the little gorge railway right there. A classic onsen base.
  • Seaside baths near the bay — soak with views over Toyama Bay; on clear days, the Tateyama mountains across the water. Pairs perfectly with a crab or yellowtail dinner near Himi.
  • Quiet mountain & countryside springs — smaller, calmer baths scattered around the prefecture for travelers who want to escape the crowds entirely.

Onsen etiquette (the short version)

  • Wash first. Rinse and wash thoroughly at the shower stations before entering the bath.
  • No swimsuits; baths are usually nude and separated by gender. A small towel is for washing, not for the water.
  • Tattoos: some places still restrict them — check ahead, or choose an inn with a private (kashikiri) bath.
  • Hydrate and keep soaks moderate, especially after travel or alcohol.

How to plan it

The easiest way to enjoy a Toyama onsen is to stay a night at a hot-spring inn (ryokan), which usually includes dinner and breakfast — often featuring the local seafood. Pair it with the Alpine Route, the Kurobe Gorge in summer, or simply a relaxed end to a food-focused trip. See when to visit to time it.
