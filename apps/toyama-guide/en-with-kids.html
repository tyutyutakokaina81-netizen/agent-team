Toyama with kids

Toyama is one of the easier parts of rural Japan to travel with children. The distances are short, the trains and trams are simple and step-free, there's a genuinely big park that's free to enter, and a rainy afternoon doesn't ruin the day because several of the best spots have indoor options. Here's how a local would plan a family trip — what actually keeps small kids happy, and the practical bits (trams, food, where to base yourself) that make the difference.

Why Toyama works with kids

  • Short hops, not long days. The city, the coast and the craft town are all close together, so most days are a simple out-and-back rather than hours in transit. See getting around Toyama.
  • Easy trams. The city's trams use low-floor, step-free carriages — easy with a stroller — and the ride itself is part of the fun for younger kids.
  • Indoor backups. Several family spots have an indoor hall or museum, so a wet day still works.
  • Food kids actually eat. Toyama Bay seafood, but also plain rice bowls, pressed trout sushi and a famously dark local ramen — easy crowd-pleasers.

A big free park (with a rainy-day option)

If you're travelling with children, the easiest win is a large family park in Takaoka — around 11 hectares of lawns, a river running through it, long slides and big climbing equipment. There's an indoor hall with a trampoline dome and a ball pool, so a sudden shower doesn't end the visit, and the open grass is made for letting kids run and picnic. The park is free to enter (some indoor facilities keep set hours), and it leans into the fact that Takaoka is the hometown of the manga artist behind Japan's most famous blue robot cat — there's a small, photo-friendly corner themed around the comic that families queue up for. Photography there is free. It's an easy half-day, and a good first stop if you arrive tired from the trains.

A glass museum that's surprisingly kid-friendly

Back in the city, the Toyama Glass Art Museum sounds like an adults-only stop, but it works well with children. The building (designed by architect Kengo Kuma) also houses the city library, including a large children's section that runs storytelling and workshops, so one parent can browse glass while the kids settle into picture books. Admission to the museum is free for high-school age and younger, and it's an easy tram ride from Toyama Station — get off near the city-centre stops and it's a short walk. The glass installations are colourful and genuinely eye-catching for little ones.

The tram ride is the activity

Don't overthink entertainment in the city — the trams do a lot of the work. They're cheap (a flat fare, with a reduced child fare for under-12s), step-free, and frequent, and small children tend to love riding the retro and modern carriages around town. You can string together the glass museum, the canal park and the station area almost entirely by tram, which keeps tired legs out of the equation. More on routes and tickets in getting around Toyama.

The Alps over the sea — an easy nature day

For a dose of big scenery without a hard hike, take the local train out to the coast. On a clear morning the 3,000-metre Tateyama range floats above the bay at the Amaharashi Coast — a genuinely memorable view, and the beach and station area are an easy, low-effort stop for families. The fishing port of Himi is the same short rail hop and a good lunch base. It's the kind of nature day that works with a stroller and doesn't demand stamina from small kids. (The sea-and-mountain view depends on the weather and is never guaranteed.)

Eating out with children

Toyama is a seafood region — winter crab and yellowtail, summer's sweet white shrimp — and many seaside inns build dinner around it. But there's plenty for fussier eaters too: pressed trout sushi (masuzushi) travels well and makes great train food, the local rice is excellent in plain bowls, and "Toyama Black" ramen is a hit with older kids who like bold flavours. Where to base yourself with kids

For families, Toyama City is usually the simplest base: step-free trams, hotels clustered by the station, and short rides to the park, the coast and the craft town. If your trip is mainly about the seafood and the bay view, a Himi seaside inn with a generous dinner is a lovely (if pricier) choice — book early in crab season. So — Toyama with kids?

Yes, comfortably. It won't out-dazzle a theme-park trip, but for a calmer, real-Japan family stop — a huge park, easy trams, big scenery and good food, all within short rides of each other — it's one of the more relaxed places on the west coast to travel with children.
