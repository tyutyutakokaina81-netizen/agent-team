Ninja Hattori-kun's hometown: Himi

In India, an entire generation grew up on Ninja Hattori-kun — the cheerful little ninja has been one of the most-watched cartoons on Indian television for years. What most of those fans don't know is where he comes from. The artist behind Hattori-kun, Fujiko Fujio Ⓐ (Motoo Abiko), was born in Himi, a quiet fishing town on the Sea of Japan in Toyama. If you love the series, Himi is a genuinely special — and refreshingly uncommercial — place to visit. Here's a fan's guide.

The two masters of Toyama

Here's a lovely piece of trivia. The legendary manga duo "Fujiko Fujio" was made up of two childhood friends from Toyama. Fujiko F. Fujio (Hiroshi Fujimoto), creator of Doraemon, was from Takaoka. His partner, Fujiko Fujio Ⓐ (Motoo Abiko), creator of Ninja Hattori-kun, Kaibutsu-kun and The Laughing Salesman, was from neighbouring Himi. Two of the most beloved names in manga history grew up just a short train ride apart, on this same stretch of coast. Visit both towns and you're tracing the roots of a huge slice of Japanese pop culture.

Hattori-kun touches around Himi

Himi celebrates its famous son gently — there's no giant theme park, just warm, characterful details woven into a working fishing town:

  • Manga-themed monuments and statues — you'll find Hattori-kun and other Fujiko Fujio Ⓐ characters as bronze figures and public art around the town and along the riverside.
  • A character-themed clock — Himi has a mechanical "karakuri" clock that performs with the manga's characters at set times; a small, delightful stop for fans.
  • A walkable "manga" stroll — half the fun is simply wandering and spotting the characters as you go, with the sea and the boats as a backdrop.

Exact locations, displays and clock times can change, so check current local information before a special trip if a particular spot is your goal.

Why it's worth the trip

Beyond the nostalgia, Himi is just a wonderful place to be. It's a real fishing port, not a tourist set-piece, and on a clear winter morning the Tateyama mountains rise straight out of the bay — one of the most striking views in Japan. Pairing a Hattori-kun pilgrimage with that scenery, and with some of the best seafood on the coast, makes for a day you'll remember.

What to eat

Himi is famous for its winter cold yellowtail (kanburi), rich and fatty, served as sashimi or in a hot-pot. Year-round, the white fish and local sushi are excellent and far gentler on the wallet than big-city prices. See our Himi guide and Toyama food guide for more.

How to get there

Himi sits on the local JR Himi Line, a short ride from Takaoka — which is itself about 15–40 minutes from Kanazawa, or roughly 2.5–3 hours from Tokyo via the Hokuriku Shinkansen to Shin-Takaoka. It makes an easy day trip; see the one-day itinerary. To visit both Fujiko Fujio hometowns, combine it with Doraemon's Takaoka.

Where to stay

Himi's seaside inns are known for winter crab and yellowtail dinners and bay views — and staying over means you can catch that mountains-over-the-sea sunrise. Toyama city is an easy alternative base. Book ahead in crab season.
