What to Do in Toyama on a Rainy Day

Rain is part of life on Japan's Sea of Japan coast, and Toyama gets its share, especially in winter and during the June rainy season. I've lived in the area for years, and a wet day here isn't a day lost — it just shifts indoors. Below is roughly how locals actually spend a rainy day in Toyama, moving between museums, covered shopping streets, and station buildings without getting soaked in between. Glass art gets most of the guidebook attention, so I'll skip past it and point you at the rest.

Start where you already are: the station buildings

If you arrive by train, you don't need to leave the building to have somewhere to be. Toyama's main station areas have shops, cafés, and small food halls under one roof, and the tram stops are right outside, some of them covered. On a rainy morning I'll often just get a coffee here, watch the trams come and go, and wait to see if the sky breaks before committing to a plan. It's an honest, low-effort start — nobody local treats the station as a "sight," but it keeps you dry and fed while you decide.

Takaoka, about 20 minutes away by train, has a similar setup around its own station, so it's easy to hop between the two indoors-to-indoors if the weather is stubborn.

Museums that aren't about glass

Toyama has more indoor culture than the glass museum it's known for. There are art museums with rotating exhibitions, local history museums, and science and craft-focused spaces scattered across the city and nearby towns. Because exhibitions rotate and some smaller places keep short or seasonal hours, I'd treat opening times as something to confirm the day before rather than trust a printed list — hours change, and a few close on specific weekdays.

What I like about a rainy museum day here is the pace. These aren't crowded, queue-around-the-block places most of the year, so you can take your time. Bring the umbrella in; most have a rack or a bag at the entrance.

Covered arcades and the art of not rushing

The older shopping arcades — the roofed shotengai streets — are the local answer to rain. You can walk a good stretch under cover, ducking into a bookshop, a coffee stand, a sweets shop, or an older kissaten (a traditional-style café). Some storefronts are quiet or shuttered these days, so it's a mixed, lived-in scene rather than a polished mall, but that's part of why I send visitors there. It feels like the actual town, not a set piece.

Pair an arcade with a nearby covered market or food building and you've got a whole lunch-and-browse afternoon without needing a plan.

Warm food is the real itinerary

Honestly, on the wettest days the "activity" is often just eating well and slowly. Toyama's coastline means seafood shows up a lot, and a warm bowl or a sit-down lunch is a legitimate way to spend an hour while it pours. Ramen, a rice bowl, local sweets with tea — none of it requires good weather. I won't oversell any single dish; find a place that's busy with locals and you'll usually do fine.

A practical rainy-day rhythm

Here's the loop I actually use: start at a station building, check museum hours on my phone, take a covered tram or a short train hop to one indoor spot, break for a long lunch in an arcade or market, then a second indoor stop in the afternoon. If the rain eases, great — swap the last stop for a short walk. If it doesn't, you've still had a full, dry day.

Fact-check note
  • ✅ Toyama sees meaningful rain and snow, particularly in winter and the June rainy season. (Reliable.)
  • ✅ Takaoka is reachable from Toyama city by a short local train ride, roughly 20 minutes.
  • ⚠️ Specific museum, arcade, and shop opening hours and closing days change seasonally — confirm before you go.
  • ⚠️ How many arcade storefronts are open varies over time; treat descriptions of "quiet" streets as a general impression, not a fixed count. Details here are 諸説あります / 要確認.

Where to stay

For overseas visitors, booking sites like Booking.com or Agoda are usually the easiest way to compare and reserve a place near the station.
