Uozu: Chasing the Bay Mirage and the Buried Forest

I live over in Takaoka, and Uozu is close enough that I go a few times each year. People from overseas usually arrive with one word in their heads: *shinkiro*, the bay mirage. I want to be honest with you before you plan a whole trip around it — the mirage is shy, and most days it does not show. But Uozu is worth the ride anyway, and this guide is about how to enjoy it whether the horizon cooperates or not.

What the mirage actually is

The Uozu mirage is a *shinkiro* — a superior mirage caused by a layer of warm air sitting over the colder surface of the bay. Distant ships, breakwaters, and the far shoreline get stretched, doubled, or turned upside down along the horizon. It is a quiet, subtle effect, not a wall of floating castles. If you go expecting something dramatic, you may feel let down; if you go expecting a strange shimmer that comes and goes, you will enjoy it more.

When to try, and the honest odds

The stronger season runs roughly from late spring into early summer — locals often point to the April-to-June window. You want a warm, calm, sunny afternoon after a cool morning, with light onshore wind. Early afternoon into late afternoon is when sightings are most often reported, though this varies (要確認 — check current-year local guidance before you go).

Here is the part I would want a friend to tell me: sightings are not common, and a clear one on any given visit is far from guaranteed. The city keeps rough records, and strong displays happen only a handful of times in a season. So treat the mirage as a bonus, not the plan. If you build a trip that only works if the mirage appears, you are setting yourself up for disappointment.

A practical tip: the shoreline park area near the water gives you an open view across the bay, and on a good day you can also see the Tateyama mountains behind it. Even with no mirage, that view of sea and snow-capped peaks together is the real reason to stand there.

The reliable plan B: the Buried Forest Museum

This is where a local would steer you. Uozu has a museum built around a *maibotsurin* — a buried forest of ancient cedar stumps, preserved where they grew thousands of years ago and later uncovered near the river mouth. The stumps are displayed roughly in place, some kept underwater in tanks so they do not dry out and crack. Standing over root systems that old is a genuinely strange, calm experience, and it does not depend on the weather at all.

The same museum also has exhibits explaining the mirage itself — the science, the records, and photos of past displays — so even on a flat, mirage-less day you get to understand what you were hoping to see. That is why I tell visitors to pair the two: chase the horizon for an hour, then walk into the museum and let the day be a success regardless.

Check current opening days and hours before you go, as regional museums here sometimes close midweek and over the New Year period (要確認).

Getting there and making a half-day of it

Uozu sits on the Toyama Bay coast and is reachable by train from the Toyama city area, with a short walk or local ride to the waterfront. A relaxed half-day is plenty: shoreline for the view and the mirage attempt, the buried forest museum, and a seafood lunch — the bay is known locally for firefly squid in spring and for its cold-water fish.
