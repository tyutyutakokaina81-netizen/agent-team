Is Toyama safe?

Short version, from someone who lives here: yes, Toyama is a calm, low-crime place, and it's one of the easier corners of Japan to travel solo or on a first trip. But "safe" doesn't mean "nothing to think about." The real things to plan around here aren't crime — they're nature: heavy winter snow, the occasional earthquake, and bears in the mountains. Here's the honest, practical version, with no scare-mongering and no false promises.

Everyday safety & crime

Japan consistently ranks among the safest countries in the world, with very low levels of violent crime, and as of 2025 it sits at the lowest tier of the U.S. State Department's travel advisory (Level 1: exercise normal precautions). Toyama is a smaller, residential region rather than a big nightlife city, so it's quieter still. Petty theft and street crime are uncommon, though never zero — keep the same everyday habits you'd use anywhere: mind your bag, don't leave a phone or wallet unattended on a café table, and use a hotel safe for valuables.

One genuinely reassuring local habit: lost property tends to come back. If you leave something on a train or in a shop, ask staff or report it at a police box (kōban) — there's a real chance it turns up.

Walking at night

Toyama's towns are generally comfortable to walk in after dark, and many solo travellers — including women — describe Japan as one of the easier places to feel at ease at night. That said, "comfortable" isn't a guarantee, and common sense still applies anywhere in the world: stick to lit main streets, keep to areas with some foot traffic, and trust your instincts if a place feels off. Outside the city centre, streets get dark and very quiet early, so plan your route back before you head out for dinner.

Public transport & getting around

Trains, the city tram and buses are clean, punctual and easy to use, and they're a safe, low-stress way to get around without a car. A few practical notes:

  • Last trains are early. Rural and branch lines can finish surprisingly early in the evening — check the last departure so you're not stranded.
  • Less English than Tokyo or Kyoto. Major stations and signs have English, and a translation app covers the rest, but a screenshot of your destination in Japanese helps.
  • Winter delays happen. Heavy snow can slow or suspend local lines. Build in buffer time in winter and check before you travel.

For the full breakdown of trains, trams and whether you need a car, see getting around Toyama.

Earthquakes

Earthquakes are a normal part of life in Japan and can strike with little warning, so it's worth knowing the basics rather than worrying about them. After the January 2024 Noto Peninsula earthquake, nearby Hokuriku cities including Toyama and Takaoka felt strong shaking but, in Toyama Prefecture, did not see the widespread damage seen closer to the epicentre. Simple, calm preparation goes a long way:

If the ground shakes: protect your head, get under a sturdy table if you can, and stay away from windows. Don't rush outside mid-tremor. When it stops, follow hotel staff instructions and watch for aftershocks. Coastal areas may issue tsunami advisories — if you're near the sea and told to, move to higher ground.

When you check in, take ten seconds to note your hotel's evacuation route, and keep your phone charged — it's your map, translator and alert system in one.

Winter & heavy snow

This is the one most first-time visitors underestimate. The Hokuriku coast, including Toyama, is one of the snowiest inhabited regions on earth, and a serious winter day means real snow — not a dusting. It's beautiful, and the locals are very used to it, but it changes how you travel:

  • Wear genuinely waterproof footwear with grip; pavements get packed and slippery.
  • Allow extra time — trains, buses and flights can be delayed or cancelled in heavy snow.
  • Mountain and rural roads may close; don't attempt them casually, especially in a rental car without winter tyres.
  • Check the forecast and any travel advisories the day before you move between towns.

Spring also brings rapid snowmelt, which can swell rivers — another reason to keep an eye on local advisories if you're heading into the mountains.

Bears in the mountains

If you plan to hike, this is worth taking seriously and not waving away. Japan has seen a sharp rise in bear sightings and encounters in recent years, with most activity in rural and mountainous areas, and bears most active from spring through late autumn. Cities and ordinary tourist routes are not where the risk lies — hiking trails and forest edges are. Sensible precautions:

  • Make noise as you walk (a bear bell, or just conversation) so you don't surprise one.
  • Avoid hiking alone, and avoid early morning and dusk when bears are more active.
  • Check local warnings before any trail — even popular ones can pass through bear habitat, and signs may be posted.
  • If you do see a bear: stay calm, don't scream or run, and back away slowly without turning your back.

None of this should stop you enjoying the mountains — it's the same caution locals take. It just means going prepared rather than assuming.

Health, food & tap water

Tap water is safe to drink, food hygiene standards are high, and pharmacies are easy to find. Carry any prescription medicine in its original packaging, and note that some common Western medications are restricted in Japan — check before you travel if you rely on something specific. Travel insurance is still worth having; ambulances themselves are free, but hospital treatment is not.

Emergency numbers to know

Police — 110
Crime, theft, traffic accidents, or anything needing the police.

Fire & ambulance — 119
Medical emergencies and fires. Ambulance transport to hospital is free.

Coast guard (at sea) — 118
Accidents or distress on the water.

These numbers are free, work 24 hours, and can be dialled from any phone, including public payphones. Operators answer in Japanese; many areas can connect you to an interpreter, but it helps to have your location written down or saved, and to speak slowly and simply.

So — is it safe?

For a solo or first-time traveller, Toyama is one of the more reassuring places in Japan to visit: low crime, easy public transport, helpful locals. The honest caveats are about nature, not people — respect the winter snow, know what to do in an earthquake, and take real care if you head into the mountains.
