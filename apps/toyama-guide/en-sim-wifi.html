SIM, eSIM & Wi-Fi for Toyama (and Japan)

You'll want mobile internet from the moment you land — for maps, train times, translation and looking up where to eat. Toyama is no different from the rest of Japan here: the same four options apply, and the choice comes down to your phone, your group size and how much fuss you want. This is a plain-English rundown, current as of 2026, of how to stay connected on a Toyama trip — what each option is, where to pick one up around the city, and how much data you actually need.

For most solo travellers and couples in 2026 the simplest answer is an eSIM; for a family or a group sharing one connection, pocket Wi-Fi often works out cheaper and easier. Below is each in plain terms.

eSIM — the simplest option for most phones

An eSIM is a digital SIM built into your phone — there's no physical chip. You buy a data plan online, you're sent a QR code by email, and you install it before you fly. Many plans only start counting down once you activate them after landing, so you can set everything up at home and switch it on when you arrive.

Will your phone work? Two things must be true: your phone supports eSIM, and it isn't locked to your home carrier. As a rough guide, iPhones from the iPhone XR onward support eSIM, as do recent Google Pixel and Samsung Galaxy flagship models — but check your own model and your carrier-lock status before buying. One known catch: iPhones sold in mainland China don't support eSIM at all, and some Hong Kong/Macao models are hit-or-miss.

Setting it up (the gist): on an iPhone, go to Settings → Cellular (Mobile Data) → Add eSIM, then scan the QR code from your provider. On Android it's typically Settings → Connections (or Network) → SIM manager → add an eSIM and scan the code. Do the install on Wi-Fi before you leave home; switch the line on after you land. This is where any future eSIM recommendation from this guide would live — for now, just buy from a reputable provider you've researched.

Note on data plans: some "unlimited" eSIMs actually give a generous high-speed allowance per day and then slow down until the next day; fixed plans (say 5GB or 10GB) run until used up. Read the fine print so the word "unlimited" doesn't surprise you.

Physical SIM — for older or specific phones

A physical SIM is the traditional chip you slot into your phone, replacing your home SIM for the trip. It's a solid, stable option, especially if your phone doesn't do eSIM. The trade-offs: your phone must be unlocked, you have to physically swap (and not lose) your home SIM, some SIMs need manual network (APN) settings, and a few prepaid SIMs don't allow hotspot sharing. If your phone supports eSIM, an eSIM usually does the same job with less hassle.

Pocket Wi-Fi — best for families and groups

A pocket Wi-Fi is a small rented router that creates your own Wi-Fi bubble; several phones, tablets or laptops connect to it at once. For a family or a group of friends this is often the cheapest and easiest route — one device, one bill, everyone online — and it keeps your home SIM untouched. The downsides are real but minor: it's an extra gadget to keep charged (batteries last a limited number of hours), and you usually have to return it, typically by dropping it in a prepaid envelope at a postbox or handing it back at an airport counter at the end of your rental.

Free public Wi-Fi — a backup, not a plan

Japan does have free public Wi-Fi — at the airport, major train stations, many convenience stores, cafés and tourist information centres — and apps and services exist that bundle large networks of these hotspots together. It's genuinely useful as a free top-up. But it is hotspot-based: the moment you walk out of the station or café, the connection is gone. That's exactly when you tend to need maps or translation most, so treat free Wi-Fi as a backup and carry your own data as the main plan.

Getting connected around Toyama

The cleanest approach is to sort your internet before you fly — buy and install an eSIM at home, or reserve a pocket Wi-Fi for airport or hotel pickup. Most pocket Wi-Fi and SIM rentals in Japan are set up around the big international gateways (Tokyo's Haneda and Narita, Kansai, Chubu and others), with pickup at the airport or your hotel and a prepaid return envelope.

If you're flying into the region, note that Toyama has its own small airport, and most visitors arrive via the Hokuriku Shinkansen into Toyama Station — so the practical move is to have your eSIM already live, or your pocket Wi-Fi posted to your first hotel, rather than counting on buying something on the spot in a smaller city. Around Toyama City and the station you'll also find free Wi-Fi in the usual places (the station area, convenience stores and tourist information), which makes a fine backup while you finish any setup. We don't list specific shops or counters here, as availability changes — confirm current options with the official airport, station or tourist-information sources when you plan.

How much data do you actually need?

Less than you'd think. A typical tourist day — a couple of hours of maps and navigation, some translation, casual social media and looking up restaurants — runs to roughly 0.5–0.7 GB. As a rough planning rule, a 5 GB plan suits many week-long trips and 10 GB a two-week trip; reported median use across recent trips has been on the order of a few gigabytes total. Streaming a lot of video on the go pushes that up, so if you binge on trains, size up. When you're on free Wi-Fi at the hotel or station, you're not touching your plan at all.

The honest bottom line

If you have a recent, unlocked phone and you're travelling solo or as a couple, an eSIM is the least-fuss choice: set it up at home, switch it on when you land. Travelling as a family or group, or carrying an older phone, pocket Wi-Fi (or a physical SIM) is the friendlier pick. Either way, sort it before you arrive and keep free public Wi-Fi as a backup — and you'll spend your time in Toyama looking at the Alps over the bay, not at a "no signal" icon.
