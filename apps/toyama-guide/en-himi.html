Travel · Himi, Toyama

Himi — where the Japanese Alps rise out of the sea

There is a stretch of coast in Japan where, on a clear winter morning, you can stand on the shore of a calm bay and watch a wall of snow-capped 3,000-metre mountains rise straight out of the water. Almost no foreign tourists have heard of it. The town is called Himi, and it sits on Toyama Bay, about 30 minutes by train past Takaoka. This is a working fishing port, not a theme park — and that is exactly why it's worth the detour.

The view almost nobody photographs

Most people who come to central Japan in winter are chasing snow monkeys or powder in Nagano. They miss this: from the Himi coastline, across the flat water of the bay, the Tateyama mountain range floats on the horizon like a mirage. Locals are so used to it they barely look up. For a first-time visitor, it's the kind of view you remember for years — and you'll likely have the shoreline almost to yourself.

Why come to Himi

Himi isn't polished for tourism, and that's the appeal. You get a real, lived-in Japanese fishing town: early-morning fish auctions, weathered boats, small family-run inns, and seafood that goes from the bay to your plate in hours. It's the Japan that most itineraries never reach — quiet, generous, and completely un-staged.

What to eat

Toyama Bay is one of Japan's richest fishing grounds, and Himi is its kitchen. In winter, the star is kanburi — cold-season yellowtail, thick with fat, served as sashimi or in a hot-pot called buri-shabu that locals swear by. The rest of the year brings white fish, squid, and the famous tiny white shrimp (shiroebi) from deeper in the bay. Even modest counters here serve fish that would cost a fortune in Tokyo.

How to get there (it's easier than you think)

Himi is a short hop from the Hokuriku Shinkansen. From Kanazawa, take the train toward Takaoka and change to the local JR Himi Line — roughly an hour door to door, an easy half-day or overnight trip. From Tokyo, it's about 2.5–3 hours to Shin-Takaoka by bullet train, then a short local ride. If you're already visiting Kanazawa or the Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route, Himi slots in without backtracking.

Where to stay

Himi's seaside inns are known for winter crab-and-yellowtail dinners and bay views; Toyama city makes an easy, well-connected base if you prefer a larger town. Book ahead in crab season (winter) and during the Alpine Route season — rooms in the good inns go fast.

When to go

Winter (December–February) is the headline season: cold yellowtail, crab, and the clearest mountain-over-sea views. Spring brings firefly squid and cherry blossoms; the green months are quiet and easy. If your one priority is that Alps-from-the-sea view, come on a crisp, clear winter morning.
