Visiting Doraemon's hometown: Takaoka

If you grew up anywhere in Asia — or anywhere with a soft spot for a round blue robot cat from the future — this one's for you. Takaoka, a quiet craft city on the Sea of Japan, is the hometown of Fujiko F. Fujio, the artist who created Doraemon. The city wears it lightly: no giant theme park, just gentle, charming nods tucked into everyday streets. For fans, that understatement is exactly the appeal. Here's how to find them, and what else to do while you're here.

Why Takaoka matters to Doraemon fans

Fujiko F. Fujio (one half of the Fujiko Fujio duo) was born in Takaoka, and the city quietly celebrates its most famous son. This isn't a manufactured tourist trap — it's a real, working city that happens to be the cradle of one of the most beloved characters in manga history. Coming here feels less like visiting an attraction and more like visiting the place a story actually came from.

Doraemon touches around town

  • The Doraemon tram — a colourfully wrapped tram themed around the characters runs in the area; riding it is a small, joyful pilgrimage in itself.
  • Public art & characters — you'll find Doraemon and friends as statues and gentle touches in public spaces around the city, including near the station area.
  • Themed details — keep an eye out for character motifs in small civic details; half the fun is spotting them as you walk.

Exhibits, displays and routes can change over time, so check current local information before a special trip if a specific spot is your main goal.

Make a full day of it

Takaoka rewards more than a character hunt. While you're here, see the historic Kanayamachi metal-casters' street and the Great Buddha of Takaoka, both a short walk from the centre — the city has been casting bronze for 400 years. Then ride out to the coast: the fishing port of Himi and the famous sea-and-mountains view at Amaharashi are both close. See the full Takaoka guide for more.

How to get there

Takaoka is easy: about 15–40 minutes from Kanazawa by train, or roughly 2.5–3 hours from Tokyo via the Hokuriku Shinkansen to Shin-Takaoka, then a short local ride. It makes a very doable day trip — see the one-day itinerary from Kanazawa.

Where to stay

You can visit in a day, but staying over lets you pair the Doraemon stroll with an early-morning trip to the coast. Stay in Takaoka itself, by the sea toward Himi, or use Toyama city as a well-connected base.
