A 7-day Central Japan route, based in Toyama

Most Japan Alps loops you'll read about run through Kanazawa or Nagoya, and then treat Toyama as a name you pass through on the train. I live on this side of the mountains, so I'd put it differently. If you pick one base for a week and make day trips out from it, Toyama tends to be the quieter, often cheaper choice — and it still puts Kanazawa, Takayama, the Alpine Route and the World Heritage villages within an easy reach. Here's how I'd shape the seven days.

Why base in Toyama, not Kanazawa

Kanazawa is the obvious base, and it's a lovely city — but it's the one everyone books, so lodging there tends to fill early and price up, especially in the busy seasons. Toyama sits a little off that main tourist channel, so rooms here are often cheaper and the evenings calmer, without you giving anything up: Kanazawa is a short, direct hop away when you want it. Basing east of it also means you're already on the doorstep of the Alpine Route and closer to Takayama, so you spend less of the week doubling back. I'm not going to quote you figures — prices move by season and by operator — but the general tendency is real. Compare it yourself in Toyama vs Kanazawa as a base.

The 7-day shape

Treat this as a frame, not a timetable. Each day is a focus, not a fixed clock.

DayFocus
1Arrive and settle in Toyama. Ease in with the bay food — the local seafood is the reason to come hungry.
2Takaoka and the World Heritage villages of Gokayama and Shirakawa-go — old casting town, then the thatched-roof valleys.
3Kanazawa as a day trip — gardens, the old quarters, the market — then back to a quiet room.
4The Alpine Route across the mountains. A full, weather-dependent day (seasonal — see below).
5Takayama — the old streets and morning market in the hills.
6The Himi coast for the sea and, in clear winter weather, the mountains across the bay — or just a slow day.
7Depart, west toward Kanazawa or east toward Nagoya and Tokyo.

How to flex it

Seven days isn't sacred. If you only have four or five, keep Days 1–3 and pick one of the Alpine Route or Takayama — you can't rush either well. The Alpine Route is the piece to watch: it's seasonal and closed in deep winter, and it can be shut by weather even when it's meant to be open, so hold Day 6 as a spare and be ready to swap. If cloud sits on the mountains, trade the high days for the coast and the villages, which read fine in most weather. For a shorter, city-first version see the 2–3 day itinerary, and if you're still sizing the trip, how many days you need lays out the trade-offs plainly.

Getting between the anchors

Trains and buses link all of these anchors, and from a Toyama base most of the day trips are genuinely doable and back by evening. Kanazawa is a short rail hop; Takayama, the Alpine Route and the villages are reached by a mix of train and bus. I'm deliberately not giving times or fares here because they change with the season and the operator — please confirm current official timetables and prices before you commit a day to any leg. The dedicated pieces go deeper: the Alpine Route, the World Heritage Bus, and, if you'd rather bend the standard tourist line eastward, the golden-route detour.
