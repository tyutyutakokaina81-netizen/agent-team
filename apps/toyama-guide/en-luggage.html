Sending Your Bags Across the Alpine Route

The Tateyama Kurobe Alpine Route is a one-way traverse. You enter from one side — usually the Toyama side in the west or the Nagano side in the east — and you come out the other. That geography is the whole appeal: cable cars, a trolley bus, a highland bus, the giant snow walls in spring, and a walk across the Kurobe Dam. But it also creates a problem nobody warns you about until you are standing at the first station with a 25 kg suitcase: there is nowhere sensible to put it, and you will not be coming back to where you started.

I live in Takaoka, on the Toyama side, and I have watched a lot of visitors learn this the hard way. Here is how locals actually deal with it.

Why you can't just carry it

Several legs of the route are cable cars and buses with tight boarding times and limited space. Large suitcases are awkward on the steps and in the aisles, and at peak season the vehicles are full. You are also gaining and losing serious altitude, sometimes in cold or wet weather, and much of the interesting part is meant to be walked. Dragging a big case across the dam is miserable. So the goal is simple: travel the route with a daypack, and have the big bag meet you on the far side.

Takkyubin (luggage forwarding) — the real solution

Japan's courier services, generically called takkyubin, will send a suitcase from a hotel on one side to a hotel (or a station-area pickup point) on the other. This is the method most locals would suggest.

The catch is timing. Same-day delivery across the mountains is not something I would count on. In my experience you should plan for the bag to arrive the next day, so the practical move is:

  • Stay one night on your entry side.
  • Hand your suitcase to the hotel front desk (or a convenience-store counter that handles the courier) the day before, or early on the morning you set off.
  • Have it sent to your accommodation on the exit side, timed to arrive the following afternoon.

Ask your hotel to help fill out the slip — the form is in Japanese, and staff at tourist-facing hotels are used to this request. Keep the receipt and the tracking number. Whether a given hotel will hold an incoming bag before check-in varies, so confirm that directly rather than assuming.

Coin lockers and holding — don't rely on them

People ask about just stuffing everything in a coin locker at the entrance station and grabbing it on the way back. That works for a same-day round trip, not for a one-way traverse — you would have to return to the same station. Locker availability at the mountain stations is limited, and large lockers fill early in high season. I would not build a plan around finding one free. Some stations and nearby facilities offer paid luggage holding, but hours and capacity change, so treat that as "check on the day," not a guarantee.

A workable sample plan

Sending west-to-east as an example: overnight on the Toyama side, forward the suitcase from that hotel to your Nagano-side accommodation, cross the route with a small pack and a warm layer, and reunite with your bag the next day. Reverse it if you're going the other direction. The principle is the same: sleep near your entry point, ship ahead, walk light.

Fact-check note
  • ✅ The Alpine Route is a one-way traverse across the mountains, and several legs use cable cars and buses with limited space for large luggage. Locals routinely forward bags rather than carry them.
  • ✅ Takkyubin forwarding from a hotel to another accommodation is a standard, widely-used service in Japan.
  • ⚠️ Delivery time across the mountains: I would plan for next-day, not same-day. Confirm the cut-off time and expected arrival with the sending hotel — this varies by operator and season. 要確認.
  • ⚠️ Coin locker and paid luggage-holding availability at mountain stations changes with the season and time of day. Do not assume a locker will be free. 諸説あります／要確認.
  • ⚠️ Whether your destination hotel will accept and hold an incoming bag before check-in depends on the hotel. Ask first.
