Kaiwomaru Park and Shinminato Port in Shinko

I live in Takaoka, and when the weather is clear and I want sea air without a long drive, I head to the Shinko harbor area on the coast side of the city. This is where a tall sailing ship called the Kaiwomaru is moored, next to a large red bridge and a small local ferry. It is a place families and older couples come on days off, not a big theme-park kind of spot. Here is how I get there and what I actually do once I arrive.

Getting there by tram and on foot

The easiest car-free way from central Takaoka is the Manyosen tram, a single light-rail line that runs from near Takaoka Station down toward the port area. It is slow and old-fashioned, which is part of the fun. You buy a ticket or tap an IC card on board, and the ride to the port-side stops takes roughly 40 to 50 minutes depending on where you start and get off. Check the current timetable before you go, because trams do not come often and missing one can mean a 20-minute wait.

From the tram stop nearest the harbor, it is a walk of somewhere around 10 to 20 minutes to the ship and the park. The exact time depends on which stop you use, so I would treat that as approximate and confirm on a map. The walk is flat and easy, mostly along quiet roads and the waterfront.

The Kaiwomaru and the park around it

The Kaiwomaru is a retired sail training ship, now kept in the water as a museum piece with a grassy park around it. When its sails are unfurled on certain days it looks striking, but the sails are only set on a limited number of days each year, so if that is your goal you should check the schedule in advance rather than assume. On an ordinary day the ship sits with bare masts, which is still a fine thing to see up close.

Boarding the ship usually costs a small admission fee, and there are open-and-closed days, so verify hours before counting on going inside. The surrounding park is free to walk through. I usually just sit on the grass, watch the boats, and let kids run around. There are benches, some shade, and toilets in the area.

The Shinminato Bridge and the ferry

The big red cable-stayed bridge nearby carries road traffic high over the harbor mouth. What overseas visitors often do not realize is that there is a free pedestrian level under the road deck, reached by elevator, so you can walk across for the view without paying anything. Wind up there can be strong, so hold onto hats.

There is also a small municipal ferry that crosses the harbor and is, as I understand it, free to ride for foot passengers and cyclists. It runs as regular local transport rather than a tourist cruise, so the crossing is short and plain. Schedules and whether it is running on a given day can change, so treat this as something to confirm on the day.

A realistic half day

I usually give the whole area a half day. Combine the ship, a slow walk over or under the bridge, and the little ferry, and you have an easy, low-key afternoon by the water. Bring a drink; options right at the waterfront can be limited, though there are some shops and places to eat in the wider port district.
