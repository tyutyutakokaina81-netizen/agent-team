Walking Iwase: Toyama's Old Canal Merchant Town

I live in Takaoka, one prefecture-neighbor over, and Iwase is the kind of place I send visitors to when they want a half-day that isn't a temple checklist. It's a former shipping district on the coast side of Toyama City, where merchants who ran cargo boats up and down the Japan Sea built solid wooden houses along a single old street. A few of those houses are open to walk through, there's a working sake brewery, and it stays fairly quiet compared to the city center. Here's how I'd actually do it.

Getting there by light rail

The easy way is the tram. Toyama runs a light-rail line north from the main station area out toward the port, and Iwase is near the end of it. From the station it's roughly a 25-minute ride; you buy a flat fare when you board or use an IC card, and you get off near the coast. From that stop the old street is a short, flat walk — think five to ten minutes on foot, well signed enough that you won't need a map for the last stretch. I'd double-check the current timetable before you go, since intervals stretch out midday.

When to go to avoid crowds

Iwase isn't overrun, but the merchant houses and the brewery draw small tour groups and bus day-trippers, and they tend to arrive mid-morning to early afternoon. If you want the street mostly to yourself, take an early tram and be walking by around 9am, or come late afternoon after the groups thin out. Weekdays are calmer than weekends. Note that several of the historic houses keep short hours and some close one day a week, so an early start also protects you from finding doors shut — check opening days in advance rather than trusting a single blog.

A simple walking route

From the tram stop, head toward the old street and just walk its length once, slowly, top to bottom. It's essentially one main run of preserved buildings, so you don't need a clever loop — the whole point is the continuous streetscape of dark wood, latticed fronts, and the occasional deep lot that runs back from the road.

Along the way you can step inside a couple of the former shipping-merchant residences that are open to the public; the ones I've been in show the storehouses and reception rooms and give a sense of how the trade worked. There's a sake brewery on the same street where you can look in and, depending on the day, taste. A handful of cafes and craft shops — glass and ceramics show up a lot in this part of Toyama — fill the gaps between the historic houses. Reaching the far end, you're close to the water, so it's worth walking the last few minutes to the port edge before turning back for the tram.

Budget roughly two to three hours if you go into a house or two and stop for coffee; an hour if you just want the walk.

Practical notes

It's flat and easy on foot, which makes it friendly for a relaxed pace. Cash is still handy for small shops and entry fees, though more places take cards than a few years ago. There's not a huge concentration of lunch spots, so either eat before you come or treat one of the street's cafes as your stop. And it pairs naturally with the tram ride itself — the line runs along the water for part of the way, which is a nice bit of the trip in its own right.
