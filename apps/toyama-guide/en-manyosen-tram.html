Riding the Manyosen tram in Takaoka

The Manyosen is a small, slow light-rail line that runs from central Takaoka out towards the Shinminato port area. Most visitors ride it for one reason only — to catch the Doraemon tram — and then get off again. That is fair enough, but the whole line is a low-key, pleasant trundle past the ordinary edges of the city and out to the water, and the ride itself is the thing worth doing.

What the line actually is

Think of it as a tram rather than a train: a single light-rail line, mostly one or two carriages, rolling along at walking-pace patience through streets and past the backs of houses. It starts near Takaoka Station and heads out toward the coast, ending near the Shinminato waterfront. That end of the line is where the scenery opens up — there's a tall bridge over the harbour and, close by, the Kaiwomaru, a moored sail-training ship that sits in a park by the water. So the line does something quietly useful: it carries you from the middle of a working city out to a genuine port view, without a car and without any fuss.

It isn't a scenic railway in the postcard sense. Much of the ride is everyday townscape. But that's part of the appeal — you're seeing the normal version of Takaoka, not a staged one.

The Doraemon tram

One of the trams on the line is themed around Doraemon — decorated inside and out, seats and windows and all. Takaoka is the home town of the manga's creator, so it belongs here rather than being a bolt-on gimmick; it fits with the wider Doraemon's Takaoka trail through the city.

The important, honest bit: the Doraemon tram runs on a limited schedule that changes, and it is only one of several trams working the line. So the tram that turns up when you're standing on the platform may well be an ordinary one. If seeing the themed tram specifically matters to you, don't just show up and hope — check the official Manyosen site for its current running days before you count on it. Treat catching it as a nice bonus, not a fixed plan.

What to see along the way and at the end

The reward at the far end is the port. From near the waterfront you get the big harbour bridge and the open water, and the Kaiwomaru sitting in its park — a proper tall ship with masts, moored as a preserved vessel you can walk up to. It photographs well in the low afternoon light.

One caution: the park and the ship keep their own opening times and can close on certain days or in bad weather, and boarding the ship is separate from wandering the park. So confirm current opening details before you build an afternoon around going aboard rather than just looking.

Practical notes

A tram day pass exists, and it can be worth it if you intend to get off at the port, look around, and ride back — or make more than a couple of hops. If you're only going one way to see the Doraemon tram and come straight back, paying as you go is simpler. I won't quote a price or a timetable here, because both change; check the current fares and running times on the operator's own information.

The line pairs naturally with a half-day of Doraemon-in-Takaoka: do the city trail, then take the tram out to the water and back. For fitting it into a wider trip, see getting around Toyama, and for what else is worth your time in town, the Takaoka town guide.
