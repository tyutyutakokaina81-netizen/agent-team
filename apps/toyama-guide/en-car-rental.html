Renting a car in Toyama

Here's the honest local view: you don't need a car for Toyama's headline sights — trains, trams and buses reach the city, the coast, Takaoka and the famous mountain routes just fine. But a rental car genuinely opens up the other half of Toyama: the quiet villages, the small fishing ports, the back-road onsen, and the freedom to go on your own schedule. If that's the trip you want, here's exactly when a car is worth it, when it isn't, and how to rent one as a visitor.

When a car is genuinely worth it

  • Gokayama and the rural villages — reaching Gokayama's thatched hamlets by car lets you linger past the last bus and explore the valleys around them at your own pace.
  • The coast and small ports — driving the shore toward Himi and the Amaharashi Coast means you can chase the morning light when the Alps rise over the bay, not a timetable.
  • Onsen and the countryside — the back-road hot springs and rural corners are far easier with your own wheels.
  • Families and heavy luggage — with kids or big bags, a car removes a lot of friction.
  • Winter flexibility — in snow season, a (properly equipped) car lets you adapt around the weather instead of waiting on rural buses.

When to skip the car

  • The Alpine Route can't be driven. The Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route is a chain of mountain transport you ride through — private cars can't cross it, so you'd just leave the car at one end.
  • City-only or famous-route trips. If you're mainly doing Toyama City, Takaoka and the big mountain routes, trains and trams are cheaper and easier — see getting around.
  • Central parking costs. Hotels and city lots often charge for parking, so a car you don't use just sits there.

What you need to drive in Japan

  • An International Driving Permit (IDP) — obtain it in your home country before you arrive; they are not issued in Japan.
  • Exception for some countries: licences from a short list of countries (including Germany, France, Switzerland, Belgium, Slovenia, Monaco and Taiwan) drive on an official Japanese translation of the licence instead of an IDP, generally within one year of entry.
  • Bring to the counter: your original home licence, your IDP (or the official translation), and your passport.

Where to rent

You don't have to fly in to rent — most visitors pick up right where they arrive:

  • Toyama Station — the easiest option, with several major companies (Toyota, Nippon, ORIX, Times, JR Rent-A-Car, and international brands like National, Alamo and Enterprise), most with English-language booking and navigation.
  • Toyama Airport — counters including ORIX and Hertz if you're flying in.

As a rough guide, a small car often starts around ¥6,000–9,000 a day, more for larger vehicles or peak seasons. Book ahead for the spring Alpine Route season and winter holidays.

It adds up — the honest cost

Be aware that the daily rate is only the start. By the time you add it all up, driving in Japan can get genuinely expensive:

  • Expressway tolls are high in Japan — a single longer drive can run several thousand yen each way.
  • Fuel is not cheap, and you usually return the car with a full tank.
  • Insurance / collision waiver (CDW) and options add a daily charge on top of the base rate.
  • Parking at hotels and in the city centre is often paid.

For one or two people doing the main sights, the train is usually the cheaper choice once tolls, fuel, insurance and parking are counted — a car earns its cost mainly when you're a group, have a packed rural itinerary, or value the freedom enough to pay for it. Go in with eyes open on the total, not just the headline daily rate.

Winter driving — read this

Toyama gets serious snow. If you're driving between roughly December and March, you want a car fitted with winter (studless) tyres — in snowy regions these are usually provided, often included in winter, or available for a small daily fee (on the order of ¥1,100/day). Even then, drive slowly, leave extra time, and don't attempt mountain back-roads in heavy snow unless you're experienced. If winter driving makes you nervous, base yourself somewhere connected and let the trains do the work instead.

The local's recommendation

For most people the sweet spot is a hybrid: take the train to your base, see the city and the famous mountain routes by public transport, and rent a car for just the day or two when you want the villages, the coast or the countryside on your own terms.
