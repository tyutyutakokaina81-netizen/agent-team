Is Toyama Worth a Detour from the Golden Route?

If you are planning the usual Tokyo–Hakone–Kyoto loop, Toyama sits off to the side. Getting there is a real detour: from Tokyo it is a couple of hours by shinkansen, and from Kyoto you double back or ride a slower line along the coast. So the honest question is not "is Toyama nice" — it is "is it worth the extra half-day of travel?"

I live in Takaoka, in Toyama. I am not going to sell you a hidden paradise. Here is what I think the detour actually buys you, and what it doesn't.

What you are really paying for: quiet

The Golden Route works because it is dense and easy. It is also crowded, and by your third temple in Kyoto the crowds can start to flatten the experience. What Toyama offers is not a better version of Kyoto. It is a different pace. Trains are shorter, streets are wider, and you can stand in front of something old or something with a mountain view and not be in anyone's way.

That is the whole pitch. If you travel to feel a place breathe rather than to check famous boxes, the detour makes sense. If your trip is short and you want maximum recognizable sights per day, it probably doesn't — and that is a fair choice.

The coast and the mountains in one view

The thing that struck me most, even as a resident, is the geography. On a clear day you can see a wall of tall, snow-topped mountains rising behind a bay. Fishing towns sit along the water. It is the kind of landscape you don't get on the standard route, which is mostly inland cities.

I want to be honest about the catch: the clear view is not guaranteed. Winter and spring mornings tend to be your best chance; summer haze can hide the mountains entirely. Some days you get nothing. Locals check the forecast and accept the gamble, and you should too.

Food is a genuine reason to come

Toyama faces a deep bay, and the seafood is a large part of why people here are quietly proud of the place. Certain small fish and shellfish are strongly associated with the local season, and eating them near where they are landed is a different thing from eating them shipped elsewhere. I would not overclaim — good seafood exists all over Japan — but the freshness and the price-to-quality here are, in my everyday experience, very good.

Seasons matter a lot. What is excellent in one month may simply not be available in another, so treat any specific dish you read about as "check whether it is in season" rather than a promise.

How much time it needs

Realistically, Toyama is a one- or two-night stop, not a day trip. One night lets you see the city and one nearby town. Two nights lets you slow down and reach the coast or the mountain side without rushing. Trying to squeeze it into a single daylight visit tends to undo the one thing you came for, which is calm.

Getting around without a car is workable but slower than a big city. Local trains and buses run, but not frequently, so you plan around timetables rather than just showing up. This is part of the quiet, and part of the friction.

My honest verdict

Worth it if: you have at least a spare night, you like landscapes and seafood, and crowds tire you out. Skip it if: your trip is tight, this is your first time in Japan and you mainly want the icons, or you are traveling in high summer when the mountain views are least reliable.

  • ✅ You can see high mountains behind a bay from parts of the prefecture on clear days. (First-hand; I see it regularly.)
