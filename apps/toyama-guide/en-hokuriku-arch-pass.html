Hokuriku Arch Pass — worth it, and does it cover the Alpine Route?

Two questions come up constantly about the Hokuriku Arch Pass, and I hear the second one from confused visitors more than almost anything else. One: is it worth buying for a Toyama-and-Kanazawa trip? Two: does it cover the Tateyama Kurobe Alpine Route? Short answers first — sometimes, and no. Here's the longer, honest version from someone who lives on the line it runs through.

What the pass is

The Hokuriku Arch Pass is a rail pass aimed at overseas visitors that links the two big gateways — the Tokyo area and the Osaka/Kyoto area — via the Hokuriku coast, so Kanazawa and Toyama fall in the middle. The idea is that you enter Japan at one end, travel across the "arch" through Hokuriku, and exit at the other, without doubling back. For a Tokyo-in, Osaka-out (or the reverse) trip that deliberately spends time on this coast, that shape can fit very neatly.

Is it worth it? The honest test

A rail pass is only worth it if the trips you'd actually take add up to more than the pass costs. The Arch Pass rewards one specific shape of trip: a one-way traverse across the country through Hokuriku over several days. If instead you're doing a Tokyo round-trip, or barely leaving the Kanazawa–Toyama stretch, the maths often doesn't favour it, and a different pass or plain tickets can win.

  • Good fit: Tokyo → Hokuriku (Kanazawa, Toyama) → Osaka/Kyoto in one direction, over roughly a week.
  • Weaker fit: a Tokyo round-trip, or a trip that mostly stays put on the Toyama–Kanazawa coast.
  • Compare before buying: line it up against the nationwide and regional alternatives — see the rail pass overview for how they stack up.

I'm not quoting the price, because pass prices and coverage are revised periodically and a stale number would mislead you. The big myth: it does not cover the Alpine Route

This is the part I most want to be clear about. The Tateyama Kurobe Alpine Route is not a JR railway — it's a chain of cable cars, tunnel buses, a highland bus and a ropeway run by separate mountain operators. No JR rail pass — including the Hokuriku Arch Pass — covers those Alpine Route sections. A rail pass can get you to the foot of the mountains on the regular train network, but from the trailhead station upward you buy the Alpine Route's own tickets. People arrive expecting the pass to sweep them over the mountains for free, and it simply doesn't work that way.

For what the crossing itself actually costs on its own ticketing, see the Alpine Route costs guide, and for how the crossing works end to end, the Alpine Route overview.

My plain take

If your trip is genuinely a one-way arc through Hokuriku, price the pass out and it may well win. If it isn't, don't buy it out of habit — and never buy it expecting the Alpine Route to be included, because that's the assumption that burns people. Whichever way you land, do the crossing budget separately; it's the honest way to avoid a nasty surprise at a mountain ticket window. Base yourself on the Toyama coast and the whole calculation gets simpler — a point I make in Toyama vs Kanazawa as a base.
