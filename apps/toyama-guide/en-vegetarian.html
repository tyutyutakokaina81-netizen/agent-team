Vegetarian and Vegan Eating in Toyama

Toyama sits on the Sea of Japan, and its food culture leans hard on the sea. That is the first thing a vegetarian or vegan visitor should understand before arriving. The regional cooking is built around *dashi* — a stock that, in most kitchens, comes from *katsuobushi* (dried bonito) or *niboshi* (dried sardines). That stock hides inside miso soup, simmered vegetables, dipping sauces, and even some dishes that look plant-based on the plate. As a resident, I want to be honest: eating fully vegan here takes planning, but it is doable, and vegetarians with some flexibility will find plenty.

What you can actually order

Vegetable tempura (yasai tempura). This is your most reliable friend. Battered and fried seasonal vegetables — lotus root, sweet potato, mushroom, pumpkin, shiso leaf — appear on many restaurant menus. Two cautions: the tempura dipping sauce (*tentsuyu*) is usually dashi-based, so ask for salt instead if you avoid fish stock. Also, some kitchens fry vegetables and seafood in the same oil. If that matters to you, ask; staff are generally used to the question.

Shojin ryori (Buddhist temple cuisine). This is the tradition designed to be entirely plant-based, using *kombu* (kelp) and shiitake for stock instead of fish. Toyama and the nearby areas have a Buddhist history, and you can sometimes find shojin-style set meals, particularly around temple-linked establishments. Availability varies and often needs advance booking, so treat it as something to arrange ahead rather than walk into. Details differ by season and place — 諸説あります / please confirm directly.

Cafes. Toyama city has a growing cafe scene, and some spots now offer vegan sweets, oat-milk drinks, and plant-forward lunch plates. These change often — new places open, others close — so I would not name specifics that may be gone by the time you read this. Searching current maps and reviews on the day is more honest than trusting a fixed list.

Soba and vegetables. Cold soba noodles can be plant-based in the noodle itself, but again the dipping broth is typically dashi. Some places use a fish-free version; most do not. Ask.

The hidden animal-derived traps

This is where honesty matters most. Several everyday items look vegetarian but are not:

  • Miso soup — almost always made with fish dashi.
  • Simmered vegetable dishes (nimono) — often cooked in dashi.
  • Dashimaki tamago and many egg dishes — fine for vegetarians, but the dashi is fish-based, so not vegan.
  • Dressings and dipping sauces — *mentsuyu* and *tentsuyu* usually contain bonito or sardine.
  • Katsuobushi flakes — sprinkled on tofu, salads, and vegetables as a topping. Ask for it left off.

A useful phrase: *"Dashi wa sakana nashi desu ka?"* (Is the stock free of fish?). And for vegans, *"Niku, sakana, tamago, nyuseihin nashi de onegaishimasu"* (No meat, fish, egg, or dairy, please). Written on a card, this helps a lot in smaller places where English menus are uncommon.

Practical tips from living here

Convenience stores and supermarkets are quietly helpful. You can assemble a plant-based meal from onigiri (check fillings — plain umeboshi or kombu are usually safe), fruit, nuts, and packaged tofu. Toyama produces rice and has good local vegetables, so fresh produce is easy to buy.

Larger restaurants and hotel dining tend to accommodate requests better than small family-run counters, simply because they have more items to swap. Lunch sets sometimes offer more flexibility than fixed dinner courses.

Set your expectations sensibly: this is a seafood region, not a plant-based hub. But with a few phrases, a willingness to ask, and tempura as a fallback, most visitors eat well.

Fact-check note
  • ✅ Certain: Toyama's regional cuisine relies heavily on seafood, and fish-based dashi is common in miso soup, nimono, and dipping sauces.
  • ✅ Certain: Vegetable tempura is widely available; dipping sauce is usually dashi-based, so salt is a safer request.
  • ✅ Certain: Shojin ryori is designed to be plant-based, using kombu and shiitake stock.
  • ⚠️ Confirm: Specific shojin ryori venues, cafes, and their opening status change over time — verify current availability and booking needs before you go.
  • ⚠️ Confirm: Whether a given kitchen fries vegetables separately from seafood, or offers a fish-free broth — ask on the spot.
