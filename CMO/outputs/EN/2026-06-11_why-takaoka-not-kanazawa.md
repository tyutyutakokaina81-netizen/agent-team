# Why I Always Skip Kanazawa and Go to Takaoka Instead

*— A local's honest take on Hokuriku's overlooked city*

*Persona: Tetsu, a Japanese writer living in Takaoka, Toyama Prefecture*
*Platform: Reddit (r/JapanTravel) + Medium/Substack crosspost*
*Date: 2026-06-11 | Word count: ~1,200*

---

## Reddit Post

**Title:** Why I Always Skip Kanazawa and Go to Takaoka Instead (I live there — here's what you're missing)

---

Look, Kanazawa is a genuinely beautiful city. I'm not going to pretend it isn't. The geisha districts are well-preserved, the market is photogenic, Kenroku-en is... technically one of Japan's three great gardens.

But here's the thing. Every time I walk around Higashi Chaya in peak season, I'm sharing the lane with 300 other people trying to take the exact same photo. The matcha latte costs ¥900. The ryokan I used to love started charging ¥40,000 a night after it showed up on someone's "Hidden Japan" list. The city is lovely, but it's been polished to a point where the edges — the human, slightly-imperfect edges — have been buffed away.

I live thirty minutes from Kanazawa. My city is called Takaoka, and almost nobody from abroad comes here.

That's the thing I want to talk about.

---

### The Buddha Nobody Photographs

There's a large bronze Buddha sitting in the middle of Takaoka. Not on a hillside, not behind a gate — he's just *there*, one block off a shopping street, in a neighborhood where kids bicycle home from school past him every day.

The Takaoka Daibutsu is, by most accounts, one of Japan's three great Buddha statues. It stands about 16 meters tall, cast from the same copper craft tradition that's been running in this city since 1609. The thing is, the other two — Nara's Todai-ji and Kamakura's — have massive tourism infrastructure around them. Takaoka's Buddha has a small shop selling incense.

I walked past him at 7:30 AM last week. A man in work clothes had stopped his bicycle and was pressing his palms together, quietly, before heading to wherever he was going. That's it. That's the whole scene.

No one was photographing it. It was just part of the morning.

---

### Zuiryuji: A National Treasure With No Crowd

If you walk fifteen minutes from the Buddha, you'll reach Zuiryuji, a 17th-century Zen temple that's a designated National Treasure. The main hall, the Buddha hall, the cloister — the spatial geometry is precise in the way that serious Zen architecture is. Not decorative. Intentional.

I've gone there at different times of year. The garden in autumn is the kind of quiet that makes you feel like you've borrowed someone else's solitude for an hour. The moss is thick. A monk was raking gravel in the main courtyard the last time I visited. He didn't look up.

In Kyoto, a temple like this would have a 45-minute queue and a gift shop with refrigerator magnets. Here, you pay ¥500, take off your shoes, and the place is yours.

---

### The Town That Made Doraemon

Takaoka is the hometown of Fujiko F. Fujio — the artist who created Doraemon. If you're reading this and don't know what Doraemon is: think of the most beloved cartoon character in Japan, then multiply that by how much Pikachu means to its generation, then consider that the series has been translated into 45 languages.

The Doraemon Tram runs around the city. There are bronze statues of the characters scattered through town — Doraemon, Nobita, Shizuka, Gian, Suneo — each placed in an ordinary spot that matches a scene from the manga. Not a theme park. Just the city itself, quietly honoring the person who grew up here.

There's something touching about that restraint. The statues aren't giant or brightly lit. They're life-sized, and most of them are at child height. My favorite is near a canal where Nobita sits fishing, looking patient and slightly defeated, which is exactly right.

---

### The Craftsmen Are Still Here

Takaoka produces about 90% of Japan's Buddhist altar fittings and a significant portion of its decorative copperware. The craft has been continuous since the early Edo period — not preserved, not revived, just *ongoing*.

Kanayamachi, the old casting quarter, is a Preservation District. The streets are cobblestone, the machiya townhouses are intact, and inside some of them people are still working — actually forging and casting metal, not performing it for tourists. You can peer through a gate and see someone grinding a piece of bronze by hand.

I don't want to oversell this. It's not Disneyland. There's no curated "artisan experience" with an English guide. It's just a working neighborhood that happens to be beautiful and old, and because almost no one comes here, it's remained exactly that.

---

### The Food Is Stupid Good

Toyama Bay, which sits at Takaoka's doorstep, has a geography that shouldn't work. Deep sea trenches come within a few kilometers of the shore, which means the cold-water fish that usually live far offshore are accessible here. The result is some of the best seafood concentration in Japan.

Shiroebi — white shrimp, translucent and delicate — are basically exclusive to Toyama Bay. You can get a bowl of them raw, sashimi-style, fanned out over rice. The first time I ate it I sat quietly for a minute before saying anything.

Masu-zushi is pressed trout sushi wrapped in bamboo leaves and sold in wooden boxes. You cut it like a pie. You eat it cold. It's been made here for centuries and it's perfect food for a train or a picnic or a 10 AM snack while you're figuring out what to do next.

Kobujime is white fish pressed between sheets of kelp — the kelp pulls moisture from the fish and gives it a slight umami, a texture that's firmer than sashimi but not cooked. If you order omakase at a local izakaya, it'll appear. Just say yes.

### Practical Stuff

**Getting there:** Shin-Takaoka Station is on the Hokuriku Shinkansen — about 2.5 hours from Tokyo on the Kagayaki. From Kanazawa, it's a 30-minute local train. If you're routing through Osaka, the Thunderbird express to Kanazawa connects from there.

**One night, two days (honest itinerary):**

*Day 1 afternoon/evening:* Arrive, drop bags, walk to Takaoka Daibutsu before sunset. Walk to Kanayamachi — have a coffee or beer at one of the refurbished machiya. Find an izakaya that doesn't have an English menu (this is a feature, not a bug — point at what looks good).

*Day 2 morning:* Zuiryuji at opening (8:30 AM). The light at that hour through the cloister columns is worth setting an alarm for. Midmorning: follow the Doraemon street statues along the shotengai. Lunch: masu-zushi from a shop near the station, eaten on a bench. Afternoon train to Kanazawa or onward.

**Budget:** Noticeably cheaper than Kanazawa. A good bowl of shiroebi don is around ¥1,000–1,500. Zuiryuji admission is ¥500. A mid-range guesthouse with breakfast will run ¥8,000–12,000.

---

### Why I'm Writing This

I have complicated feelings about sharing this.

Part of me knows that the reason Takaoka is still like this — calm, local, not-yet-packaged — is precisely because the crowds haven't arrived. The izakaya owner who poured my sake last winter doesn't speak English and doesn't need to. The monks at Zuiryuji aren't accustomed to tourist behavior. The Kanayamachi streets feel lived-in because they are.

And another part of me thinks: if you're the kind of traveler who's searching for something beyond the itinerary, you deserve to know this place exists.

So here it is. Takaoka. Thirty minutes from Kanazawa. Four hundred years of quiet craft. A Buddha that people pray to on their morning commute. Shrimp you won't forget.

Just maybe don't all go at once.
