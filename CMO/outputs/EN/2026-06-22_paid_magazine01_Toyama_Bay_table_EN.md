## Foreword — Why the Toyama Bay table

I did not decide to write about food. I just kept noticing what was already on the table.

I live on Japan's west coast, beside a bay that goes deep almost as soon as it leaves the shore. That single fact — cold water, deep water, close to land — quietly decides most of what a household here eats. The fish is fresh because the fishing ground is near. The preserved food exists because winters were long and the sea's surplus had to be made to last. And the kelp habit, oddly strong for a place that is not where kelp is harvested, is a leftover of an old shipping route that stopped running generations ago.

None of this was built to impress a visitor. It was built to feed a family through a year. That it now tastes like something worth traveling for is, in a way, an accident of necessity.

What follows is thirteen things from that table. Some are showy — a bowl of translucent shrimp, a fish that sits at its own price tier in the case. Most are not: a fermented turnip, a shellfish at the corner of the counter, a tub of salted seaweed. I'll introduce them the way a neighbor would, not the way a guidebook does — here's what I keep in my kitchen, here's what I cook when someone visits, here's the one I only understood after years of eating it.

Each entry has two parts: a short note in a resident's voice, then a block of practical information you can actually use — season, what it is, how to eat it, where to look for it, whether it travels, and one tip. Where the Japanese original gives a number or a claim I can't stand behind, I've kept it vague on purpose. For a reader far away who can't easily check, exaggeration costs more than it's worth.

# 1. Shiraebi — the bay's translucent shrimp

For me this was never a delicacy. It was just what showed up in the shops when the weather turned. It took growing up and looking again to see that the rest of the world treats it as a once-in-a-lifetime bowl while I treated it as a weeknight.

The shrimp is tiny and see-through while alive, taking on a faint pink only in the light. It lives a few hundred meters down — a depth that usually means a long trip offshore. Here the seabed drops away within sight of the harbor, so the boats are back the same day. Locals have long called it the jewel of the bay, and for once a nickname like that feels closer to plain description than to marketing.

**Practical info**
- **Season:** Roughly spring through autumn; on the everyday table during those months.
- **What it is:** A small deep-water shrimp, about 6–8 cm, near-translucent alive, faintly pink in light. Delicate, clean sweetness; an almost melting texture.
- **How to eat it:** The classic is *shiraebi-don* — peeled shrimp packed over a small bowl of rice (often 100–200 of them, each hand-peeled). Also common lightly deep-fried or salt-grilled.
- **Where to find it:** In season, fishmongers and supermarkets in the coastal cities of the prefecture sell fried or grilled packs; sushi places and rice-bowl specialists serve it too.
- **Can you order or ship it?** Peeled shrimp is delicate and labor-intensive, so it tends to be pricey. Whether frozen or fried versions ship by mail order `[to verify]`.
- **Tip:** Eat it where it lands. Freshness is most of the experience, and it falls off fast once the shrimp leaves the coast.

*Fact-check note:* ✅ The bay's steep underwater canyon brings deep-water species unusually close to shore (general, well-known geography). ⚠️ "Lives a few hundred meters deep" — source says 300m+; kept as a range. ⚠️ "Large-scale commercial harvest of this shrimp happens in few places" — stated as a relative observation, not a "only place on earth" claim (A5).

# 2. Nodoguro — the fish at its own price

Walk into a fish shop here and one fish in the case sits apart, at a tier of its own. Small body, red skin, only the inside of the mouth black. We call it nodoguro; its formal name is akamutsu.

It is a lean white fish that somehow carries the fat of a rich one — people sometimes call it the toro of white fish. In our house it is not a weekly fish. It turns up two or three times a year: a birthday, a midsummer gathering, the year-end. Someone announces it before it reaches the table — "tonight is nodoguro." Luxury, I've come to think, is set more by how rarely a thing appears than by how much of it there is.

**Practical info**
- **Season:** Not stated clearly in the source; blackthroat seaperch is generally said to be richest in autumn–winter `[to verify]` for the bay specifically.
- **What it is:** Blackthroat seaperch (akamutsu), a small, fatty white fish from deeper water (roughly 200–400 m). Catch is uneven, which lifts the price.
- **How to eat it:** Sashimi works, but the household favorite is salt-grilled — skin seared hard so the fat under it renders. It also takes simmering, sushi, and shabu-shabu.
- **Where to find it:** In the glass case of fish shops and sushi counters in the prefecture, usually a single fish priced well above its neighbors. Bought whole locally.
- **Can you order or ship it?** Not stated in the source; whether premium fish mail-order carries it, and at what price `[to verify]`.
- **Tip:** If you're here, buying it whole and grilling it at home is far gentler on the wallet than ordering it in a big-city restaurant.

*Fact-check note:* ✅ Nodoguro is the local name for akamutsu; a deep-water white fish with notably high fat content. ⚠️ The price comparison in the Japanese text ("one piece in Tokyo vs. one whole fish locally") swings with season, shop, and size — left out of the English here rather than stated as fact. ⚠️ "Most premium fish of the bay" softened to "sits at its own price tier" (A5).

# 3. Hotaru-ika — the squid that lights the spring sea

There is a small squid that turns the spring sea faintly blue at night, and the strange thing — the thing a visitor notices and a local has stopped noticing — is that the same animal is also just dinner.

It glows pale blue when disturbed. From March into May it comes up into the shallows to spawn, and along parts of the coast the water's edge flickers blue in the dark. Meanwhile the supermarket sells it boiled, in trays, for the price of a side dish. People drive out at midnight to see it; the same evening someone is eating it with vinegar-miso. When the trays vanish from the shelf in late May, that — more than any calendar — is how I know spring is over.

**Practical info**
- **Season:** March to May, ending around late May; fishing and selling cluster in this window.
- **What it is:** Firefly squid, a small squid (about 5–7 cm) that emits blue bioluminescence when disturbed.
- **How to eat it:** Usually sold pre-boiled and eaten with vinegar-miso paste as an ordinary dinner side. The nighttime glow on the shore is a separate, visual pleasure.
- **Where to find it:** Boiled packs at supermarkets across the coastal cities for a few hundred yen in season.
- **Can you order or ship it?** Not stated in the source; whether boiled or marinated versions ship by mail order `[to verify]`.
- **Tip:** If you want both the dish and the spectacle, come in the spawning window — outside it you'll find neither on the shelf nor on the shore.

*Fact-check note:* ✅ Firefly squid spawns into Toyama Bay shallows in spring and is both eaten locally and viewed as a night spectacle. ✅ The same steep bay geography that brings the white shrimp near shore is plausibly tied to the squid's concentration. ⚠️ Exact concentration locations generalized to "parts of the coast" (A4).

# 4. Fukuragi — yellowtail, eaten before it's famous

There's a fish that fills the supermarket case from early summer into autumn here, and it's the same fish that becomes a winter luxury — only younger. We call the young one fukuragi.

Yellowtail is a "promotion fish": it gets a new name at each stage of its life. The grown adult is the celebrated winter catch, rich and expensive. Fukuragi is its leaner, earlier self — a casual, summer-grade fish. Saying "tonight is fukuragi" carries a tone like "a light evening." There's a small, particular pleasure in eating a fish on its way up, before it arrives.

**Practical info**
- **Season:** Early summer into autumn — the household, pre-luxury stage of a fish that peaks in winter.
- **What it is:** The local name for young yellowtail (buri), roughly 30–50 cm, leaner than the adult. One rung on the naming ladder (kozukura → fukuragi → gando → buri); the size cutoffs vary by area.
- **How to eat it:** Usually salt-grilled or teriyaki at home — the lighter fat suits salt, the mild flesh takes a sweet-soy glaze well. None of the heaviness of simmered buri-and-daikon. Sashimi works too.
- **Where to find it:** Sold as fillets in supermarket fish sections across the prefecture. Roughly ¥200–300 a slice `[to verify]` (varies by shop, season, size).
- **Can you order or ship it?** Not stated; this is an everyday local fish, bought in person by default.
- **Tip:** Treat it as a summer fish, not a lesser winter one — that's how locals actually frame it.

*Fact-check note:* ✅ Buri is a "promotion fish"; the Toyama naming sequence is broadly as given, with regional variation. ✅ Fukuragi = young buri. ⚠️ "¥200–300 a slice" and the exact cm cutoffs vary by area and fishery co-op; kept as approximate.

# 5. Beni-zuwai — the everyday crab

When someone says "crab" in a winter here, the first thing in my mind is not the expensive one. The luxury snow crab lands here too, but what we actually buy and eat at home is its more affordable cousin — a feast that doesn't ask you to make a fuss about it.

It's red even before boiling, more vividly so than the premium kind, and lined up at the market that red runs in rows. The meat is delicate, full of moisture, lightly sweet — a different character from the heavier luxury crab. We buy it pre-boiled and eat it plainly: snap a leg, slide the meat out, a touch of crab vinegar or nothing at all. The work of picking meat from shell is itself a small winter ritual.

**Practical info**
- **Season:** Winter. (Exact landing window for red snow crab `[to verify]`.)
- **What it is:** Red snow crab (beni-zuwai), already red before boiling. Delicate, moist, lightly sweet — distinct from the heavier premium snow crab.
- **How to eat it:** Most often bought already boiled and eaten as is, with a little crab vinegar or nothing.
- **Where to find it:** Supermarkets and markets in the prefecture, at an approachable price — easier to reach than the gift-grade premium crab.
- **Can you order or ship it?** Not stated; whether boiled crab ships by mail order `[to verify]`.
- **Tip:** Don't dress it up. Its appeal is that it's a feast you can have on a weekday without ceremony.

*Fact-check note:* ✅ Red snow crab lands in the bay and is a more affordable household crab than premium snow crab; red before boiling is correct. ⚠️ "What we eat at home / affordable" is a general regional tendency; price moves with year and catch. ✅ No shop names, districts, or prices given (A4).

# 6. Baigai — the quiet regular of the simmer pot

At the corner of the fish counter, easy to miss, sits a small spiral shell. It never makes a poster. It isn't luxury and it isn't photogenic. But it's in the case nearly every day, and that, to me, is the more interesting kind of food.

It's a small sea snail, a relative of the whelk. At home it almost always becomes a simmered dish — cooked whole in shell with soy, sake, sugar, and ginger until the shell holds a pocket of concentrated broth. You pull the meat with a toothpick: a firm bite, then a rush of sea umami. It's the second glass of an evening: sashimi first, then this. The foods that aren't flashy are the ones that stay on the table for years.

**Practical info**
- **Season:** Not specified in the source; described as available much of the year, with some variation.
- **What it is:** Baigai — a small spiral sea snail (whelk relative) from the bay. Firm texture, concentrated savory broth. Regionally the name can cover a few related species.
- **How to eat it:** Almost always simmered whole in a sweet-savory soy broth with ginger; meat picked out with a toothpick. Sashimi is possible. A natural match for sake.
- **Where to find it:** Modestly displayed in fish shops around the prefecture, present most days; a household staple side.
- **Can you order or ship it?** Not stated; the simmered form keeps reasonably well, but distribution `[to verify]`.
- **Tip:** Order it as a sake snack, not a centerpiece — that's the role it plays at a local table.

*Fact-check note:* ✅ Baigai is a whelk-type shellfish harvested in the bay; soy-simmered preparation is a common household dish. ⚠️ "In the case nearly every day" varies by shop and season. ⚠️ "Baigai" as a name can refer to a few related species depending on locality.

# 7. Kombu-jime — sashimi rested overnight in kelp

Here there's a quiet habit of taking sashimi and pressing it between sheets of kelp overnight before eating. One simple step, and the fish becomes a different food.

You salt fresh white fish or squid lightly, sandwich it in kelp, and refrigerate it until the next day. The kelp draws out water and gives back glutamate — pure umami. What comes out is denser, glossier, far deeper in flavor; people's first reaction is almost always the same: this isn't sashimi anymore, it's a feast. The reason this region took to it is history, not just taste — an old coastal trade left premium kelp lying around for generations, so people learned to treat it as an ingredient, not only a stock.

**Practical info**
- **Season:** Year-round (a technique, not a seasonal catch). Especially expected at New Year and at memorial gatherings.
- **What it is:** Sashimi (white fish or squid) lightly salted, cured between sheets of kelp overnight or longer. The flesh turns denser and markedly more savory.
- **How to eat it:** As sashimi, straight. The longer it rests, the deeper the umami. Pairs naturally with sake.
- **Where to find it:** Sold normally at markets and supermarkets across this prefecture and the neighboring one — not a rarefied luxury, an ordinary weekday item.
- **Can you order or ship it?** Not stated; it needs refrigeration, so buying it locally is the sure route `[to verify]` for mail order.
- **Tip:** If you study Japanese food, this is one of the clearest things to taste umami in. Buy some locally and eat it where you're staying.

*Fact-check note:* ✅ Kombu-jime is a Toyama/Ishikawa regional curing method; the kelp–water/umami exchange is real food chemistry. ⚠️ The kelp-trade history is widely told but stated here without a proper-noun route name (generalized per A4). "umami / kombu-jime" are strong, true keywords for overseas readers.

# 8. Heshiko — mackerel buried in rice bran for a year

At the edge of the local fish section there's a small pouch that looks like something out of a pickling crock. It's mackerel that has spent about a year packed in rice bran and salt — a fermented preserve of this coast.

You salt raw mackerel to pull the water out, then pack it into a barrel with rice bran, salt, and chili and weight it down for roughly a year. What comes out is deep brown and frankly pungent; first-timers tend to flinch at the smell. One bite delivers salt, fermented umami, the sweetness of the bran, and a touch of chili all at once. I like it lightly grilled, the fat dripping off, beside sake. It exists because winters here once shut down fishing for long stretches, and a household needed the autumn's mackerel to last until spring. A survival trick, now a thing food people abroad study.

**Practical info**
- **Season:** Year-round (a roughly one-year preserve). The raw mackerel comes from summer–autumn catches.
- **What it is:** Heshiko — mackerel salt-cured, then fermented about a year in rice bran (nuka), salt, and chili. Deep brown, pungent, dense with umami.
- **How to eat it:** Thin-sliced raw; or, the resident's pick, lightly grilled so the fat renders and it turns fragrant; also good flaked over rice as ochazuke.
- **Where to find it:** The true home is the neighboring prefecture, but it's made and sold here too — a single-slice pack for a few hundred yen at ordinary supermarket fish counters.
- **Can you order or ship it?** Not stated; as a shelf-stable-ish ferment it seems suited to shipping, but the route `[to verify]`.
- **Tip:** Start with a small piece if the smell is new to you — the flavor is much friendlier than the aroma warns.

*Fact-check note:* ✅ Heshiko is a Hokuriku (Fukui/Ishikawa/Toyama) rice-bran-fermented mackerel preserve; the preservation-out-of-necessity history is sound. ⚠️ Exact fermentation length ("about a year") and price kept approximate. Neighboring-prefecture origin stated at prefecture level (A4).

# 9. Ika no kuro-zukuri — squid cured in its own ink

There's a jar on a lot of tables here that is jet black, and the first time an out-of-prefecture friend sees it, the reaction is reliably "that looks a little frightening."

It's raw squid, its own innards, its own ink, and salt — mixed, left to ferment, jarred. That's the whole recipe. A spoonful over white rice and the bowl is gone. The dish started as a fisherman's way to waste nothing of a caught squid: body, organs, ink, all preserved with salt and time before refrigerators existed. It's the same lineage as the fermented mackerel and the kelp-cured fish — what to do with the sea's surplus. The color scares people once; the taste converts almost all of them.

**Practical info**
- **Season:** Year-round (a fermented, salted preserve that keeps).
- **What it is:** A jet-black salted ferment of raw squid, its innards, its ink, and salt. Strong: salt, squid umami, the ink's deep bitterness, and a fermented lift, all at once.
- **How to eat it:** A small spoonful over hot rice (it makes the bowl disappear); also a sake snack. Newcomers should start with a little.
- **Where to find it:** Sold in small jars at souvenir shops in the prefecture's main cities, and in supermarkets. Keeps well, so it suits taking home.
- **Can you order or ship it?** Likely — small jars with good shelf life already move as souvenirs — though the exact mail-order route `[to verify]`.
- **Tip:** It travels better than almost anything else on this list; a small jar is the souvenir people remember.

*Fact-check note:* ✅ Kuro-zukuri is a Toyama salted/fermented squid preserve made with the squid's ink; same preservation lineage as heshiko and kombu-jime. ⚠️ "Sold at station/city souvenir shops" kept at city level (A4); no specific shop named.

# 10. Kabura-zushi — a winter-only fermented turnip and fish

Some foods you simply cannot meet as a summer visitor. This is one. It's made only in the coldest weeks, and if you're not living through a Hokuriku winter, you won't run into it.

Salted turnip slices are wrapped around salted yellowtail (sometimes mackerel) and fermented in rice malt. "Zushi" is in the name, but it sits closer to a pickle than to sushi. It's made in December and January only, because the slow ferment needs the ambient cold. Sliced thin and served around New Year with warm sake, one bite gives you four things at once — salt, fermentation, the crispness of turnip, the fat of the fish. Every household swears by its own ratio.

**Practical info**
- **Season:** Winter only — December to January, when the cold supports the ferment. A New Year table fixture.
- **What it is:** Kabura-zushi — salted turnip enclosing salted yellowtail (or mackerel), fermented in rice malt (koji). Closer to a pickle than sushi.
- **How to eat it:** Sliced thin, as is. Pairs especially well with warm sake on a winter evening.
- **Where to find it:** Fewer families pickle their own now, but town pickle shops in this prefecture and the neighboring one make it every winter; available locally in the cold season.
- **Can you order or ship it?** Not stated; winter-only and needing refrigeration, so availability and timing `[to verify]`.
- **Tip:** Plan a winter trip if you want this — there is no off-season substitute, and households differ (yellowtail vs. mackerel, salt-heavy vs. light), so it's worth trying more than one.

*Fact-check note:* ✅ Kabura-zushi is a Toyama/Ishikawa winter koji-fermented dish. ⚠️ Origin/history details vary by locality. ⚠️ "Yellowtail camp vs. mackerel camp" is a real tendency between areas but stated as variation, not rule. Decline of home-pickling noted without a "dying out" claim.

# 11. Himi himono — dried fish that upgrades a breakfast

There's a port town on the prefecture's western side that the whole country knows for its winter yellowtail. What I quietly like more than the yellowtail, though, is the dried fish.

Cooking dried fish on a weekday morning is a nuisance — the smell stays, the pan needs scrubbing. This is one of the few I think earns it. Horse mackerel, barracuda, young yellowtail, each salted its own way; the flesh stays firm yet the fat melts when you chew. Rice, miso soup, and this, and breakfast is complete. The quality comes from distance: the port works mostly by fixed nets that catch migrating fish without injuring them, and processing happens close by — short trip from sea to drying rack. It's not cheap, two or three times an ordinary pack `[to verify]`, but bought a few times a year it's the prop that makes a normal breakfast feel like a small occasion.

**Practical info**
- **Season:** Year-round (dried, so it keeps); each species has its own peak `[to verify]`.
- **What it is:** Dried fish (himono) from a port town on the prefecture's west — horse mackerel, barracuda, young yellowtail and the like. Firm flesh, fat that melts on chewing.
- **How to eat it:** Pan-grilled, with rice and miso soup — enough on its own for a "slightly upgraded" breakfast.
- **Where to find it:** Supermarkets and roadside stations across the prefecture; the fishing port also ships directly.
- **Can you order or ship it?** Yes — the port ships directly. Specific shop or site name omitted on purpose `[to verify]` (generalized for privacy).
- **Tip:** You don't have to travel to the port to taste it. Keeping it in the rotation at home is the easier pleasure.

*Fact-check note:* ✅ The town is a west-coast port nationally known for yellowtail; its port is fixed-net-centered; its dried fish is a regional specialty. ⚠️ "Two or three times the price of ordinary dried fish" is the writer's impression, not a measured figure. "Fukuragi" is the local word for young yellowtail.

# 12. Salt-cured wakame — seaweed that arrives in salt

Most people abroad picture dried wakame. Here the local preference runs the other way — toward the salt-cured kind, because it keeps more of its crunch and its smell of the sea.

The seaweed peaks in spring and early summer, and the old way to hold a harvest peak is to pack it in salt. You buy a tub, rinse it, soak it five minutes to draw the salt out, and it's ready for miso soup, a salad, or a vinegared side. Cut with cucumber and rice vinegar, it sharpens a summer table immediately. Salt-cured, it keeps about a month in the fridge — stockpiled at spring's end to carry the household through the heat. It's a humble thing that nonetheless brings the smell of the shore indoors.

**Practical info**
- **Season:** Spring to early summer for the harvest (varies by area and year); salt-cured stock then carries into summer.
- **What it is:** Salt-cured wakame seaweed. Locals favor it over dried for better retained crunch and ocean aroma.
- **How to eat it:** Rinse, soak about five minutes to de-salt, then use in miso soup, salads, or vinegared dishes (good with cucumber and rice vinegar in summer).
- **Where to find it:** Local supermarkets in the prefecture; sold by the tub. (Price kept unstated — it moves with shop and season `[to verify]`.)
- **Can you order or ship it?** Not stated; salt-curing gives it shelf life, so it seems shippable, but the route `[to verify]`.
- **Tip:** The bay also yields tengusa (for tokoroten jelly), mozuku, hijiki, and kombu — if you like seaweed, the salt-cured wakame is a low-stakes entry to a wider local pantry.

*Fact-check note:* ✅ The bay produces wakame, tengusa, mozuku and other seaweeds; salt-curing is a widely used coastal method. ⚠️ "Spring to early summer peak" varies by area/year. ⚠️ Specific price left out deliberately (A4/A5). ✅ Mixing of cold and warm currents favoring seaweed diversity is a general oceanographic point.

# 13. Kombu in Takaoka — a 200-year-old trade still in the fridge

Here is a small puzzle: a coastal city that doesn't harvest kelp nonetheless ranks among the country's heaviest household consumers of it, topping the national figures in some years. The answer isn't geography. It's an old trade route.

In the Edo and Meiji eras, cargo ships carried Hokkaido kelp down the Japan Sea toward Osaka, and a port in this area was a major waypoint. A waypoint doesn't just pass goods along — some stays. Premium kelp was always around, and when you live with a surplus you invent uses for it: kelp-cured fish, kelp-wrapped rice balls, shaved kelp over rice, kelp rolled into fish cake. Walk into a supermarket here and the kelp aisle is visibly wider than elsewhere. In my own kitchen it's in something at nearly every meal, almost without thinking. A trade from two centuries ago, still alive in a refrigerator — that's a quiet way to eat history.

**Practical info**
- **Season:** Year-round (a pantry staple, not a seasonal catch).
- **What it is:** Kelp (kombu) in many household forms — shaved (tororo/oboro), shredded, salted, stock-grade — and as the base of kelp-cured fish and kelp-wrapped foods. Harvested far to the north, but consumed heavily here for historical reasons.
- **How to eat it:** Tororo-kombu over morning rice balls, a pinch of shredded kelp in instant noodles, kelp-cured sashimi at dinner — woven through ordinary meals.
- **Where to find it:** Supermarkets across the city, where the kelp section is unusually broad; an easy, everyday buy.
- **Can you order or ship it?** Dried kelp products travel and keep well, so they suit taking home or shipping `[to verify]` for specific sellers.
- **Tip:** If you want one souvenir that explains the whole table, buy shaved kelp — it connects directly to the kelp-cured sashimi in entry 7.

*Fact-check note:* ✅ Toyama (and this city) ranks very high, and tops the table in some years, for household kelp spending. ⚠️ The kitamae-bune trade-route history is widely told; stated here without a specific port proper-noun (generalized per A4). "Tops the national figures in some years" mirrors the source's hedged claim rather than a flat superlative (A5).

## Closing — what stays after you leave

If there's a single thread through these thirteen, it's that almost none of them were meant to be remarkable. The shrimp was near because the sea is deep close in. The ferments exist because the winters were hard. The kelp is here because old ships left it behind. What looks like a regional cuisine is mostly a record of a place solving its own problems and then keeping the habit long after the problem went away.

That's the part I'd want a visitor to take home — not a checklist of must-eats, but the idea that a quiet, unremarkable table can be carrying a few hundred years inside it. You can taste that without anyone explaining it. You just have to eat where people actually live.
