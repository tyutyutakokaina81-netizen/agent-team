# note記事：富山湾の冬の王様。— 「寒ブリ」

- **日付**: 2026-06-09（食 #3）

富山湾の冬の王様。— 「寒ブリ」
```

## 本文

```
富山の冬の食卓で、いちばん格が上の魚をひとつ挙げろと言われたら、
迷わず寒ブリと答える。

冬、富山湾で揚がる、脂ののったブリ。
氷見で揚がるものは特にブランドとして知られているが、住人にとっては
ブランド以前に「冬になったら食べるもの」という感覚が近い。

---

■ 出世魚という考え方

ブリは大きくなるにつれて呼び名が変わる、いわゆる出世魚だ。
富山ではコズクラ、フクラギ、ガンドと段階を経て、最後にブリになる。

だから「ブリ」と言えば、しっかり育ちきった、いちばん脂ののった
状態を指す。同じ魚でも、名前が変われば扱いも値段も変わる。
このあたりの感覚は、住んでいると自然に身につく。

---

■ 食べ方は、わりと素朴

刺身、しゃぶしゃぶ、そして煮物のブリ大根。
高級魚と言われるわりに、家庭での食べ方は気取らない。

ブリ大根は、大根がブリの脂を吸って、主役の大根になる料理だ。
切り身よりも、味の染みた大根のほうを楽しみにしている人も多い。
冬の鍋やしゃぶしゃぶでは、脂が湯にとけて、最後の雑炊までうまい。

---

■ 冬を一本で語る魚

春のホタルイカが「軽やかな季節の知らせ」なら、
寒ブリは「冬の重み」を一本で背負っている。

年の瀬、台所にブリが一本届くと、家のなかが少しあらたまる。
そういう存在感のある魚は、そう多くない。

---

🌏 For English readers

If you asked a Toyama household which fish sits at the top of the
winter table, the answer comes fast: kan-buri, cold-season
yellowtail. Caught in Toyama Bay in winter, heavy with fat, the
ones landed at Himi are especially prized — though to locals it is
less a brand than simply "what you eat when winter comes."

Yellowtail is a "promotion fish": its name changes as it grows
(kozukura, fukuragi, gando, and finally buri). So "buri" means the
fully grown, fattest stage — same fish, different name, different
price.

The cooking stays humble. Sashimi, hot-pot shabu-shabu, and
buri-daikon — simmered with radish that drinks up the fish's fat
until the radish becomes the real prize. When a whole yellowtail
arrives in the kitchen at year's end, the house feels a little more
formal. Few fish carry a whole season the way this one does.

## English (travel writing)

```
In Toyama, winter has a king, and it swims. Kan-buri — cold-season yellowtail — is the fish that quietly reorders the household table from December into the new year. Heavy with cold-water fat, the ones landed at Himi carry a name that food lovers across Japan recognize.

For travelers, the appeal is that this is a working coast, not a tourist set piece. Toyama Bay is reachable from Kanazawa in about 15 minutes by the Hokuriku Shinkansen to Shin-Takaoka (roughly ¥1,560, JR Pass valid), which makes a winter food day-trip genuinely easy. No reservations, no English fuss — just a region that takes its fish seriously.

Here's a detail outsiders enjoy: this is a "promotion fish." As the yellowtail grows it earns new names, and only the largest, fattest stage is called buri. The grade is built into the language.

The eating is unpretentious. Sashimi shows off the fat directly; shabu-shabu hot-pot lets it melt into the broth (save room for the rice porridge cooked in it at the end); and buri-daikon simmers the fish with radish until the humble radish steals the show. Order the hot-pot on a cold night and you understand why people here measure winter by this one fish.

If you want a Japan that locals actually eat through the season — not a photo-op, but a real cold-coast meal — this is it.
```
