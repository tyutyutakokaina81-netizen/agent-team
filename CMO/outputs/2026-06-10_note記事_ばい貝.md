■ つまようじで、引き出す

ばい貝は、しょうゆとみりんで甘辛く煮る。
食べるときは、つまようじを身に刺して、くるりと回しながら引き出す。

最後まできれいに引き出せると、ちょっと達成感がある。
身は、こりこりした歯ごたえで、噛むと甘辛い煮汁の味と、貝のうま味が出てくる。

この「自分で回して引き出す」というひと手間が、
お酒のつまみとして、ちょうどいい間合いをつくる。
急いで食べる料理じゃない。ちびちび飲みながら、ゆっくり付き合う貝だ。

---

■ 炊き込みごはんにも

ばい貝は、煮付けだけじゃなく、炊き込みごはんにもなる。
貝のだしが米に染みて、磯の香りのするごはんになる。

派手な高級魚ではない。地元のスーパーや魚屋に、ふつうに並んでいる。
でも、富山の家の食卓や、晩酌の席には、しっかり居場所がある貝だ。

つまようじ片手に、ばい貝を回しながら一杯。
富山の、ありふれて、でも幸せな夜の風景のひとつだ。
```

```
There's a small dish that makes me happy when it turns up as the first bite at a Toyama izakaya: baigai, a sea snail from Toyama Bay, simmered sweet and salty in soy and mirin. It's humble, and it's a ritual.

You eat it with a toothpick. Spear the meat, then turn the shell slowly, winding the body out in one curling piece. Get it out whole and there's a tiny flush of triumph. The flesh is firm and a little chewy, carrying the sweet-savory broth and the snail's own brine. That one bit of effort — twist, ease, release — is exactly what makes it such a good drinking companion. It isn't food to rush. It's a snail you keep company with, sip by sip, late into the evening.

Toyama is an easy reach for this kind of unshowy local eating, about 15 minutes from Kanazawa to Shin-Takaoka on the Hokuriku Shinkansen, into a prefecture that points its bay straight at the dinner table. Baigai also goes into takikomi rice, the broth seeping into the grains until the whole pot smells of the shore.

This is the Japan you don't book a tour for: a sea snail, a toothpick, a cup of sake, an ordinary happy night. Skip one famous restaurant and find the counter where locals are quietly winding out their whelks.
