## 本文

```
春になると、富山湾の話題はホタルイカ一色になる。

体長7センチほどの小さなイカ。
産卵のために沖から岸へ寄ってくる時期、富山ではこれを食べる、見る、
拾う、と三つの楽しみ方がある。

---

■ まず、食べる

定番は釜揚げ。さっと茹でて、酢味噌で食べる。
ぷりっとした身のあとに、わたの濃いうま味がくる。
小さいのに、味の密度が高い。

生で食べる「沖漬け」や「刺身」も出回るが、地元の人間ほど
釜揚げの素朴さに落ち着く気がする。スーパーに春先だけ並ぶ、
あの少し青みがかったパックを見ると、季節が動いたと感じる。

---

■ そして、見る

夜の海が青白く光る。
ホタルイカが発光する性質を持つためで、湾内の決まった季節に
だけ起きる現象だ。

闇のなかにぼんやり浮かぶ青の帯は、写真ではなかなか伝わらない。
住んでいても、毎年見られるわけではない。波・月・潮の条件が
揃ったときだけの、運まかせの景色だ。

---

■ 小さいけれど、富山の春そのもの

派手な高級食材ではない。
ひと口で消えてしまう、つつましいサイズだ。

でも、食卓に上がり、夜の海を光らせ、春が来たことを知らせる。
この三役を一匹でこなすあたりが、富山湾の主役らしいと思う。

---

🌏 For English readers

Every spring, one tiny creature takes over conversation along
Toyama Bay: the firefly squid, hotaru-ika. Barely seven
centimeters long, it rises from deep water to spawn — and locals
enjoy it three ways at once.

You eat it: lightly boiled, dipped in vinegared miso, a small body
followed by a deep, rich burst from the innards. You watch it:
on certain spring nights the bay glows faint blue, because the
squid is bioluminescent. And, in season, you simply feel the year
turn when those pale-blue packs appear in the supermarket.

It is not a luxury ingredient. One bite and it is gone. Yet it
fills the table, lights the sea, and announces spring — three jobs
for one small squid. That is why, here, it is the star.
```

## English (travel writing)

```
Some spring nights, the sea off Toyama glows. Not metaphorically — the water itself turns a faint, drifting blue, lit from within by millions of tiny squid that have risen to spawn. They are called hotaru-ika, firefly squid, and for a few weeks each spring they belong entirely to this coast.

Toyama sits closer than most travelers think. From Kanazawa, the Hokuriku Shinkansen reaches Shin-Takaoka in about 15 minutes (around ¥1,560, covered by the JR Pass), dropping you into the bay region where the squid run. It makes an easy add-on for anyone already spending a day or two in Kanazawa and looking for something the crowds haven't found.

The eating is as good as the glow. The classic preparation is the simplest: briefly boiled, then dipped in vinegared miso. The small body gives a soft snap, and then the innards release a deep, almost custardy umami — far bigger than the size suggests. In season you'll find it in any local supermarket, no reservation, no fuss.

A practical note: the night glow depends on tides, moon, and weather, so treat it as a bonus rather than a guarantee — even people who live here don't see it every year. The food, though, is reliable and cheap. Order a plate, watch the bay, and if the water lights up, count yourself lucky.

This is the kind of quiet, seasonal Japan that rarely makes the itineraries: no lines, no English menus, just a small squid that feeds the table and lights the sea.
```
