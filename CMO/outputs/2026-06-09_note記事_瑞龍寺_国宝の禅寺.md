## 本文

```
高岡には、国宝になっている禅寺がある。
瑞龍寺。曹洞宗の寺で、加賀前田家ゆかりの寺として建てられた、
と伝わっている。

観光地として有名というより、
住んでいる人にとっては「町の落ち着いた背骨」みたいな場所だ。

---

■ まっすぐ、という設計

瑞龍寺で最初に気づくのは、建物の並び方の整い方だ。
総門、山門、仏殿、法堂が、一直線に並んでいる。
真ん中の参道を歩くと、視線がまっすぐ奥へ抜けていく。

禅の寺の伽藍は、左右対称で、無駄がない。
建物の配置そのものが、心を整えるように作られている。
にぎやかさで魅せる場所ではなく、静けさを設計した場所だ。

---

■ 国宝という重さ

仏殿・法堂・山門が国宝に指定されている。
古い木造建築が、これだけまとまって残っているのは珍しい。

ただ、国宝だから偉い、という感じでは見ていない。
何百年も、雨や雪や火の手をくぐって、
ここまで残ってきた、という時間の厚みのほうに、いつも目がいく。

---

■ 雪の日の、しずかさ

個人的にいちばん好きなのは、雪が積もった日の瑞龍寺だ。
広い回廊と白い庭。音が、雪に吸われて消える。

観光の人が少ない時間に、ただ座っていると、
四百年近い建物の中で、自分の呼吸だけが聞こえる。
派手な感動ではない。でも、ここでしか味わえない静けさだ。
```

🌏 For English readers

```
Takaoka has a Zen temple designated a National Treasure of
Japan: Zuiryuji, a Soto Zen temple said to have been built in
connection with the Maeda family of the old Kaga domain. For
locals it isn't so much a tourist site as a quiet backbone of
the town.

The first thing you notice is the geometry. The outer gate,
the great gate (sanmon), the Buddha hall (butsuden), and the
Dharma hall (hatto) stand in a single straight line, perfectly
symmetrical, with the path running clean to the back. The layout
itself is built to settle the mind — silence by design rather
than spectacle.

The Buddha hall, Dharma hall, and main gate are National
Treasures; it's rare to find this much old wooden architecture
surviving together. What moves me isn't the title but the
weight of time — centuries of rain, snow, and fire survived.
Best of all is a snowy day, when sound vanishes into the white
garden and you hear only your own breath inside a near-400-year-
old hall.
```

## English (travel writing)

```
Most people heading from Kanazawa into Hokuriku rush past Takaoka on the way to somewhere busier. Skip it, and you miss a National Treasure most foreign visitors never reach: Zuiryuji, a Soto Zen temple whose architecture is built, quite literally, to calm you down.

Walk in and the whole place lines up. The outer gate, the great gate, the Buddha hall, and the Dharma hall sit on one dead-straight axis, mirror-symmetrical, with the path running clean to the back. Zen temple complexes are designed without waste, and here the arrangement itself does the work — your eye, and then your mind, settles. Three of the buildings are designated National Treasures, and it's genuinely rare to see this much old wooden architecture preserved together in one quiet courtyard.

ACCESS: From Kanazawa, take the Hokuriku Shinkansen to Shin-Takaoka (about 15 minutes, roughly ¥1,560, JR Pass valid), then a short local connection into central Takaoka; the temple is an easy walk or quick taxi from there. No reservation needed, rarely crowded — it pairs naturally with the town's metalwork workshops and the great seated Buddha for a calm half-day.

Come early, or come in winter. On a snowy morning the wide wooden corridors swallow every sound, and inside a hall nearly four hundred years old you'll hear almost nothing but your own breathing. This is the Japan most travelers never slow down enough to find — and it's a 15-minute train ride from Kanazawa.
```
