## 本文

```
高岡には、一年に一日だけ町を進む山車の祭りがある。
御車山祭（みくるまやままつり）。毎年五月一日に行われる、
この町の春のいちばん大きな行事だ。

ユネスコの無形文化遺産に登録された山・鉾・屋台行事の
ひとつにも数えられている。

---

■ ふだんは、しまわれている

御車山は、ふだん見られない。
祭りの日のために、一年かけて手入れされ、
当日だけ、組み立てられて町に出てくる。

金具、漆、染織、彫刻。
高岡の金属や漆の手仕事が、ぜんぶ一台の山車に乗っている。
動く工芸品、という言い方が、いちばんしっくりくる。

---

■ ゆっくり進む、ということ

御車山は、速く走る祭りではない。
重い山車を、人の手で、ゆっくり引いて進める。

その遅さが、かえって良い。
きしむ車輪の音、引き手の掛け声、見上げる人の列。
急がない時間の中で、町全体が祭りに合わせて呼吸を変える。

---

■ 受け継ぐ、という仕事

派手な一発のイベントではなく、
毎年きちんと出して、直して、また仕舞う。
その地味な繰り返しが、何百年も続いてきた、と伝わっている。

住んでいると、これは「見る祭り」というより
「守られている祭り」なのだと感じる。
一日のために、ずっと手をかけ続ける人たちがいる。
```

🌏 For English readers

```
Once a year, on May 1st, the town of Takaoka rolls out its
greatest spring event: the Mikuruma-yama Festival, a procession
of ornate festival floats. It's counted among Japan's float
festivals (yama, hoko, yatai) inscribed on UNESCO's list of
Intangible Cultural Heritage.

The striking part is that you can't see the floats the rest of
the year — they're maintained over twelve months and assembled
only for the day. Metal fittings, lacquer, woven textiles, carving:
Takaoka's metalwork and lacquer crafts all ride on a single
float. "Moving works of art" is the phrase that fits best.

These floats don't race. They're heavy, drawn slowly by hand,
and the slowness is the point — creaking wheels, the pullers'
calls, rows of upturned faces. For locals it feels less like a
show to watch than a thing being protected: a festival kept
alive by people who tend it all year for a single day, a habit
the town is said to have carried for centuries.
```

## English (travel writing)

```
If you're in Hokuriku at the start of May, here's a date worth building a day trip around: May 1st in Takaoka, when the Mikuruma-yama Festival sends a line of ornate floats slowly through the town. It's part of the group of Japanese float festivals inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list — and it draws almost none of the foreign crowds that swamp the big-name festivals.

What makes it special is that the floats vanish for the other 364 days. They're maintained all year and assembled only for this one procession, and each one is essentially a rolling museum of local craft: gilded metal fittings, deep lacquer, carved wood, woven textiles — the same metalwork and lacquer traditions Takaoka has practiced for centuries, stacked onto a single moving structure. They don't race; they're hauled by hand, slowly, and the creak of the wheels and the pullers' calls set the whole town's pace for the day.

ACCESS: From Kanazawa, take the Hokuriku Shinkansen to Shin-Takaoka (about 15 minutes, roughly ¥1,560, JR Pass valid), then a short local hop into central Takaoka where the procession runs. No ticket or reservation to watch from the street; go in the morning for the best view and pair it with the town's National Treasure Zen temple and great bronze Buddha.

This is the Japan that doesn't perform for tourists — a working town quietly protecting something precious, one slow day a year. Come for May 1st, stay for the crafts, and you'll see why the locals tend these floats so carefully the rest of the year.
```
