それが富山の海沿いの町に、昔から残っている。

バタバタ茶は、飲み物の名前であると同時に、集まりの名前でもある。
地元では、お茶を囲んでおしゃべりをする寄り合いの場が、昔から続いてきた。

お茶が主役というより、人が集まる口実としてのお茶。
そういう文化ごと残っているのが、面白いところだ。

■ 泡立てる意味は、諸説ある

なぜ泡立てるのか。まろやかになるから、冷めにくいから、など諸説あるらしい。

The tea itself is rare too: a post-fermented "dark tea," different from
both green and black tea. Drinking fermented tea as a daily habit is
uncommon in Japan, yet it has survived in this seaside town. And
batabata-cha is not only a drink but the name of a gathering — people have
long met around it to talk, nibbling pickles and simmered dishes while
sipping the foamy tea. The tea is less the point than the excuse to sit
together; that the whole custom survives, not just the drink, is what I
find good about it.

Why whisk it at all? Some say it mellows the taste, some say it keeps the
heat — theories vary, and honestly the reasons stop mattering once you hold
the whisk in both hands and make that rattling sound yourself. It's a
culture the body remembers before the head explains. You can't get it just
anywhere; it appears at traditional gatherings and local events in Asahi.
If you ever get the chance, borrow the whisk and whip it yourself — the
