Kanayamachi: the town where casting began.

The place where Takaoka started becoming a "town of bronze" is Kanayamachi.
A quiet townscape of senbon-goshi (fine-latticed) houses lines a
cobblestone street. Takaoka's bronze workshops and Great Buddha are famous,
but trace the source and you arrive here — a place where the starting point
of an industry remains, town-shape and all.

When Takaoka was founded, casters (imonoshi) were invited to make the town
prosper — craftsmen who melt metal and pour it into molds to make wares.
Where they lived and worked was Kanayamachi. The metalworking that began
here grew into the later Takaoka bronze industry. The "kana" (metal) in the
town's name feels like a trace of its days as a metal town.

Along Kanayamachi's street stand houses with senbon-goshi, slender lattices.
The run of cobblestone and lattice doors isn't built for tourism — it's the
trace of a life lived. There's no flashy attraction, but walk slowly and you
sense the quiet remaining time of a craftsmen's town.

The workshops of Takaoka bronze, the Great Buddha, the metal fittings of the
Mikuruma-yama floats — the very first step of all that technique began in
this town. Seeing the finished specialties is sightseeing too, but tracing
"where it began" changes how the place looks.

Kanayamachi isn't lively, nor curated as a tourist site. Yet Takaoka's
character as a "town of making things" started here. When you visit, walk
Kanayamachi's cobblestones along with the Buddha and workshops — a time of
confirming the town's origin through the soles of your feet.
