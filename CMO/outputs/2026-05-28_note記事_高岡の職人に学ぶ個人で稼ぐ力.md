400年前、加賀藩がこの地に7人の鋳物師（いものし）を呼び寄せた。
そこから始まった「高岡銅器」は、いまや国内シェアの大半を占める。
お寺の鐘も、街の銅像も、その多くが高岡で生まれている。

私はこの町の話を知ったとき、ふと思った。

「職人って、世界でいちばん古い“ひとり起業家”じゃないか」と。

やっていることは「自分の手で価値を生み、信用を積む」こと。

🌏 For English readers (英語版・要約)

There is a town in Japan called Takaoka (Toyama).

400 years ago, seven metal casters were invited here. That was the start of
"Takaoka copperware" — today, many of Japan's temple bells and bronze statues
are born in this town.

When I learned its story, one thought struck me:
A craftsman is the world's oldest "solo entrepreneur."

No company. No ads. No social media.
Just their hands, their skill, and their trust.
And yet, they have made a living for centuries.

That is exactly what we now call "earning as an individual."
Freelancers, creators, small businesses — we all create value by hand and
build trust. Only the tools changed: from a chisel to a PC and AI.
The core hasn't changed in 400 years.

Three lessons from Takaoka's craftsmen:
1. A repeatable system beats a single masterpiece.
2. Quiet trust is the strongest advertising — it never disappears.
3. What you polished locally can become a weapon worldwide
   (Takaoka copperware is now praised in art and interiors abroad).

Honestly, I don't yet have a craftsman's skill or 400 years of trust.
I'm still at the entrance of this journey.
But Takaoka reminded me: creating value alone is not special —
it's the oldest, most certain form of work humans have ever done.

If you're making something by yourself today,
you might be the newest person in a 400-year lineage of craftsmen.

I'll keep documenting this journey honestly. Follow along if you'd like.
