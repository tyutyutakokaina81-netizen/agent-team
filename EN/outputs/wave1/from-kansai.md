# Reaching Toyama from Osaka and Kyoto

If you are planning a trip to Toyama from Osaka or Kyoto, the route changed in March 2024, and a lot of older guides still describe the old one. I live in Takaoka, in Toyama Prefecture, and I make this trip a few times a year. Here is how it actually works now, including the part most articles skip: getting yourself and your luggage across the transfer at Tsuruga.

## The short version of the route

The Hokuriku Shinkansen extension now runs to Tsuruga. Because of that, the direct limited express that used to go all the way through was cut back. The common pattern today is:

1. Board the **Thunderbird** limited express at Osaka (it also stops at Kyoto) and ride it to **Tsuruga**.
2. Transfer at Tsuruga to the **Hokuriku Shinkansen** (Tsurugi or Hakutaka services) heading toward Toyama and Kanazawa.

From Osaka, the Thunderbird leg is roughly a bit over an hour to Tsuruga; from Kyoto it is shorter. The shinkansen leg from Tsuruga to Toyama is around 50 minutes to an hour depending on the service. Total door-to-door from Osaka tends to land in the three-hour range, but exact times shift by service and season, so check a timetable app on the day. (Times here are approximate — please verify.)

## The Tsuruga transfer, honestly

This is the part worth reading twice. At Tsuruga the Thunderbird arrives on a lower-level limited-express platform, and the shinkansen departs from an upper level. The station was rebuilt for this handover, so it is designed for the connection — there are escalators, elevators, and clear signage in English pointing "for Shinkansen." In my experience the walk is a few minutes at a normal pace.

The catch is that it is a genuine level change with a moving crowd, because a full Thunderbird empties out and heads for the same connecting train. If you are carrying a large suitcase, that means an escalator or a wait for the elevator alongside everyone else. It is manageable, not stressful, but it is not a step-across-the-platform transfer. Give yourself the scheduled connection time and don't book the very tightest gap if you can avoid it. If you use the elevator, look for it early rather than at the last moment — the queue can build.

## Tickets and reserved seats

You can buy a through ticket that covers both legs, and reserved seats are worth it on the Thunderbird, which can be busy on weekends and holidays. The Japan Rail Pass and some regional passes cover this corridor, but coverage and rules change, so confirm what your specific pass includes before you rely on it. If you are booking reserved seats separately for each leg, try to leave a sensible buffer at Tsuruga.

## Which city to start from

From Kyoto the Thunderbird leg is simply shorter, so if your Kansai base is flexible, Kyoto shaves off some time versus Osaka. Both are straightforward. The Thunderbird runs frequently through the day, so you are not tied to one narrow departure window.

## A local note on arriving

Many overseas visitors are aiming past Toyama city toward Takaoka, the coast around Himi, or onward. From Toyama Station the local connections are easy to pick up, and Takaoka is a short additional hop. If that is your plan, you don't need to overthink it at Toyama — the signage and staff are used to travelers.

## Fact-check note

- ✅ The Hokuriku Shinkansen now runs to Tsuruga, and the Osaka/Kyoto–Toyama trip involves a Thunderbird-plus-shinkansen transfer at Tsuruga (opened March 2024).
- ✅ At Tsuruga the limited-express and shinkansen platforms are on different levels, connected by escalators and elevators.
- ⚠️ Exact journey times (roughly three hours from Osaka) vary by service, day, and season — check a live timetable.
- ⚠️ Pass coverage (Japan Rail Pass and regional passes) and fare details change; confirm before you travel.
- ⚠️ Transfer walking time depends on the crowd and whether you use the elevator; my "a few minutes" is a typical case, not a guarantee — 諸説あります.

## Where to stay

For accommodation in Toyama, Takaoka, or Himi, booking sites like Booking.com or Agoda are usually the easiest option for overseas visitors, since they handle English and international cards.
