# Etchu-Yatsuo Old Town and the Owara Kaze-no-Bon

I live in Toyama Prefecture, and Yatsuo is one of those places I keep coming back to — sometimes for the festival, more often for a quiet weekday walk. If you searched for the Owara Kaze-no-Bon and September access, here is what I would actually tell a friend visiting from overseas: the town has two very different faces, and which one you want changes when you should go.

## What the Owara Kaze-no-Bon is

The Owara Kaze-no-Bon is a folk dance festival held in the Yatsuo area of Toyama City in early September. Dancers in straw hats move slowly through the sloping streets after dark, accompanied by the kokyu (a bowed string instrument), shamisen, and singing. The mood is restrained and a little melancholy rather than loud — no fireworks, no big floats. It is usually held over three nights, commonly cited as September 1–3, but dates and the exact evening schedule can shift, so treat those as ⚠️ needing confirmation each year on the official information before you book anything.

## The two faces of the town

For most of the year, Yatsuo is a small hillside district of stone-paved lanes, wooden houses, and old shop fronts. On an ordinary weekday you can walk for stretches without meeting many people, drop into a paper workshop or a small cafe, and hear mostly your own footsteps. It is calm and photogenic in an unhurried way.

During the three festival nights it becomes a completely different place. Large crowds arrive from across the region and beyond. Trains and the roads in fill up, popular viewing spots get dense, and the same narrow lanes that felt empty in July become slow rivers of people. Both versions are worth seeing — you just cannot get both on the same visit.

## When to go

- **You mainly want the atmosphere and the quiet streets:** avoid the festival dates. Late spring, summer, or autumn on a weekday gives you the old town almost to yourself. Some say early autumn light suits the stone lanes especially well, though that is personal taste.
- **You specifically want the Owara dance:** come for the early-September nights, but plan around crowds. Arrive with low expectations about moving quickly or getting front-row views, and give yourself a long, patient evening.
- **A middle path:** there are smaller Owara-related dance occasions and rehearsals at other times of year, but details vary and are not always open to visitors in the same way, so confirm in advance (⚠️).

## Getting there in September

Yatsuo is reached from central Toyama City by a local JR line; the ride is short, on the order of half an hour, though I would check current timetables rather than trust a number from memory (⚠️). During the festival, railway operators and the town usually run extra or special services and adjust the last trains, because ordinary schedules cannot absorb the crowds. Some visitors also use organized bus tours. Driving in on festival nights is the hardest option — parking is limited and routes get congested — so public transport is the calmer choice.

Practical notes for the festival nights: the dancing runs late into the evening, streets are uneven and sloped, so wear comfortable shoes, and bring a light layer since early-September nights can cool down. Food and drink options exist but get busy, so do not count on a relaxed sit-down meal at peak hours.

## Where to stay

Yatsuo itself has limited lodging, and many visitors base themselves in central Toyama City and travel in. For overseas visitors, booking sites like Booking.com or Agoda are usually the easiest way to compare and reserve a room.
