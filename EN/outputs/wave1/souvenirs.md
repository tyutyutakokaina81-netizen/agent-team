# What Souvenirs to Buy in Toyama

I live in Takaoka, in Toyama, and I have handed over more boxes of local sweets than I can count — to relatives, to coworkers, to a neighbor who watered my plants. So this is not a "top 10" list scraped from somewhere. It is what people around here actually buy when they need something that survives a train ride and still means something when it lands on someone's desk.

Toyama faces the sea on one side and the mountains on the other, so the omiyage (souvenir) shelves lean toward two things: things from the water, and sweets that keep. Here is how a resident sorts through them.

## Start at the station if you are short on time

The main station areas in Toyama City and in Takaoka have gift corners that carry most of the standard boxes. If your schedule is tight, you can do all your shopping there and not miss much. The selection is smaller than a full department store, but the classics are almost always in stock, and boxes are already wrapped for gifting. For travelers connecting to the Shinkansen, this is the practical option.

## Sweets that travel well

The safest gift, in the sense that almost anyone will accept it, is boxed regional sweets. A few worth knowing:

- **Masuzushi-shaped and fish-themed sweets** — Toyama is known for its pressed trout sushi, and that image shows up on cookie tins and jellies. Cute, and they keep for weeks.
- **Beniya / traditional wagashi** — small dry sweets (higashi) that last a long time and pack flat. Good for older relatives.
- **White-shrimp (shiro-ebi) crackers** — the small local shrimp is a regional point of pride, and the senbei made with it are light and hard to crush in a bag. These are what I personally reach for most, because they survive a suitcase.

Individually wrapped boxes are the norm here, which matters if you are splitting one box across an office.

## Kombu (kelp) — the quiet local obsession

Toyama consumes a notably large amount of kombu per household compared with much of Japan — this is often repeated locally, though exact rankings shift year to year, so treat the "number one" claims as requiring a check. Regardless of the statistics, kombu products are genuinely everywhere: tororo kombu (thin shavings), kombu-wrapped snacks, kombu candy, and seasoned kelp for rice. It is lightweight, keeps for a long time, and is unusual enough to be a real conversation piece abroad. If you want a gift that says "Toyama" and not just "Japan," this is my honest pick.

## Medicine — the souvenir people forget

Toyama has a long history as a center of household medicine and traveling medicine sellers. Some shops still sell traditional-style remedies and the paper-and-print packaging associated with that trade. As an actual gift, the reproduction medicine-pouch designs, small tins, and printed paper goods are more giftable than the medicine itself. It is a genuinely local story, and few visitors know it.

## Seafood you can carry

Fresh seafood is the region's pride, but most of it will not survive your trip home, so be realistic. What travels: dried firefly-squid (hotaru-ika) products, bottled or vacuum-packed seafood preserves, and shrimp-based snacks. Buy the shelf-stable versions, check the date on the label, and keep the truly fresh stuff for a meal while you are here.

## How a resident actually decides

Two questions settle it: does it keep, and can it survive a bag? That is why locals default to crackers, dry sweets, kombu, and printed goods over anything fresh or fragile. If you only take one thing, a box of individually wrapped shrimp crackers or a pack of kombu snacks covers most situations.

## Fact-check note

- ✅ Toyama is known regionally for pressed trout sushi, white shrimp, and kombu products, and station gift corners stock boxed versions of these. (Reliable.)
- ✅ Toyama has a documented historical connection to the household-medicine trade. (Reliable.)
- ⚠️ Claims that Toyama ranks "number one" in kombu consumption are commonly repeated locally but vary by survey and year — treat as "requires a check." (諸説あります／要確認)
- ⚠️ Specific shop names, opening hours, and current stock change often — confirm before you go.
