# Getting to Toyama from Tokyo by Shinkansen

I live in Toyama Prefecture, up on the coast that faces the Sea of Japan, and one of the first questions visiting friends ask me is simple: how do I get there from Tokyo, and how long does it take? The good news is that the answer is mostly straightforward. The Hokuriku Shinkansen runs from Tokyo Station all the way to Toyama, so you can go the whole way on a single train without changing. Here is what I tell people, from a resident's point of view.

## The direct trains: Kagayaki and Hakutaka

Two train types matter for this trip. The Kagayaki is the faster service, stopping at fewer stations; the Hakutaka makes more stops along the way. Both run direct between Tokyo and Toyama, so in either case you board at Tokyo Station and stay on until you reach Toyama Station.

A Kagayaki typically covers Tokyo to Toyama in a little over two hours. The Hakutaka takes longer because of the extra stops, often closer to two and a half hours or a bit more. Exact timings shift with the timetable, so check the current schedule before you travel rather than trusting a single number you saw online.

## What it costs

For an unreserved-seat fare, a one-way ticket from Tokyo to Toyama runs somewhere in the region of 12,000 to 13,000 yen when you combine the base fare and the Shinkansen surcharge. A reserved seat costs a little more. Prices do get revised from time to time, so treat any figure as a ballpark and confirm at the machine or counter.

If you already hold a Japan Rail Pass, note one catch that trips people up: the nationwide JR Pass is not valid on the Kagayaki. You can still use it on the Hakutaka, which reaches the same place, just with a slightly longer ride. Regional passes covering the Hokuriku area also exist and may work out cheaper depending on your route, so it is worth comparing before you buy anything.

## About unreserved seats

Here is the part that catches some overseas visitors off guard. The Kagayaki is an all-reserved train, meaning there are no unreserved seats on it at all. If you want to ride the Kagayaki you have to book a seat in advance or on the day.

Unreserved seating is available on the Hakutaka. On a quiet weekday you can usually just walk into an unreserved car and find a seat without much drama. Around long holidays, weekends, and busy travel seasons, unreserved cars fill up, and you may end up standing for part of the ride. If you are travelling in a peak period or with luggage and want certainty, paying the small extra for a reserved seat is the safer choice. Larger suitcases sometimes need an oversized-baggage reservation on the Shinkansen; the rules on that have changed over the years, so check the current policy.

## Is a Tokyo day trip realistic?

People often ask whether they can visit Toyama from Tokyo and come back the same day. Honestly, it is possible, but I would not call it relaxing. With roughly two-hour-plus rides each way, a day return eats four to five hours of your day just sitting on trains. If you leave Tokyo early and take a late train back, you get a solid chunk of daytime here, enough for a focused look at one area or a single sight.

What you do not get on a day trip is the evening and the early morning, which for a coastal region like this is where a lot of the appeal sits. My honest suggestion is one overnight if you can spare it. A day trip works if your schedule is tight; a single night turns a rushed errand into an actual visit.

## Fact-check note

- ✅ The Hokuriku Shinkansen runs direct between Tokyo and Toyama with no transfer required.
- ✅ The Kagayaki is an all-reserved service and has no unreserved seats.
- ✅ Unreserved seats are available on the Hakutaka.
- ⚠️ Journey times (roughly two hours on the Kagayaki, a bit more on the Hakutaka) vary by timetable — confirm the current schedule.
- ⚠️ The one-way fare figure (around 12,000–13,000 yen) is approximate and subject to revision — check before you book.
- ⚠️ JR Pass validity (not valid on Kagayaki, usable on Hakutaka) and oversized-baggage rules can change — verify the current terms.

## Before you go

Where to stay: for overseas visitors, booking sites like Booking.com or Agoda are usually the easiest way to sort a room without a Japanese phone number or address.
