# Inami: A Woodcarving Town Day Trip from Toyama

I live in Takaoka, and Inami is one of those places I keep sending visitors to when they want something quieter than a temple checklist. It's a small woodcarving town in the Nanto area, south of the Toyama plain, where the main draw is a sloping street lined with carving workshops. You can hear chisels through open doors. Here's how I actually get there, and how to behave once you're standing in front of someone's life work.

## Why Inami is worth a half day

Inami built its reputation on *ranma* — the carved transom panels that sit above sliding doors in traditional Japanese rooms. The carvers here trace their craft back to temple reconstruction work, and a large wooden temple still anchors the town. Walk the main slope and you pass workshop after workshop: some are shop-fronts selling finished pieces, others are working studios where you'll see sawdust and half-finished panels. It isn't a theme park. People are working. That's the appeal, and also the reason to be a considerate guest.

Budget two to four hours for the town itself. It rewards slow walking more than a tight schedule.

## Getting there from Takaoka / Toyama

There's no single fast route, so here's the honest version.

**By train + bus.** From Toyama or Takaoka, you take a local line toward the Nanto area and then a connecting bus into Inami. Trains on rural lines run roughly once an hour, sometimes less, so the connection is the thing that makes or breaks your day. Check the current timetable before you leave — I'd treat any schedule you find online as **要確認 (verify)** because rural services get revised. Rough total travel time from Takaoka is around an hour and a half depending on connections, but I've had it stretch longer waiting for a bus.

**By car.** Honestly the easiest option if you can manage it. The town sits near an expressway interchange, and there's parking near the base of the main street. If you're already renting a car to see the Toyama coast, adding Inami is straightforward.

**A practical tip:** go on a weekday morning if you want workshops open and the street calm. Some studios keep irregular hours, and a few close on set weekdays — there's no reliable single list, so it's a bit of a gamble. Weekends are livelier but busier.

## Etiquette inside the workshops

This is the part I care about most, because it's where visitors accidentally cause friction.

- **Ask before photographing people or unfinished work.** A carver's in-progress design is essentially their intellectual property. Finished shop pieces are usually fine; a half-cut panel on the bench is not automatically fair game. A quick gesture at your camera and a questioning look works even without Japanese.
- **Don't touch.** The panels are delicate and often for sale. Hands off unless invited.
- **It's okay to just look, but read the room.** Some fronts are shops and welcome browsers. Some are working studios where you're a guest, not a customer. If someone's mid-cut, don't expect a conversation.
- **Buying small is appreciated.** You don't need to commit to a large panel. Many workshops sell smaller carved items — coasters, ornaments, chopstick rests — that make the visit reciprocal rather than extractive.

## What else is nearby

The Nanto area spreads across former villages, so people often pair Inami with other stops in the region on the same trip — a reason a car helps. If you're relying on transit, I'd keep expectations to Inami alone rather than trying to chain destinations against a thin bus schedule.
