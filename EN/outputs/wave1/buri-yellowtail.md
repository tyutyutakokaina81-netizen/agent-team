# Winter Yellowtail (Buri) in Toyama: Where and When

I live in Takaoka, and every winter the same thing happens at the fish counters: the yellowtail gets fatter, the prices climb, and locals start comparing notes on who has the good stuff this week. If you're planning a trip to Toyama for *kan-buri* (cold-season yellowtail), here's an honest look at when to come, what it costs, and how people around here actually eat it.

## What "buri" and "kan-buri" mean

Buri is Japanese yellowtail (amberjack). In winter, the fish carries more fat, and that's when it's called *kan-buri* — "cold buri." The prized run is associated with the Toyama Bay area, and fish landed here in peak season is often marketed under the name *Himi kan-buri*. There's a certification tied to that name, though the exact criteria and the volume that qualifies each year vary, so treat any "certified" label as something to ask about rather than assume (諸説あります / please verify locally).

## When to come

Peak season is roughly December through January, sometimes stretching into early February. The catch depends heavily on weather and the sea, so a good year and a thin year can look very different. If you want the best odds of fatty buri on the menu, aim for late December to mid-January. Come in March and you'll still find yellowtail, but it won't be the same fish that people line up for in January.

## How the price works

This is the part most guides skip, so I'll be blunt. Buri is affordable most of the year, but *kan-buri* in peak season is not a cheap fish. When a strong, well-known catch comes in, prices at the market spike, and restaurants pass that along.

Rough, honest ranges (2026, subject to change):

- **Buri sashimi, a few slices as part of a set meal:** often around ¥1,500–¥3,000 in a casual place.
- **Buri-shabu (hot-pot) course for one:** commonly ¥4,000–¥8,000, and higher at ryokan or specialist restaurants during peak weeks.
- **A whole good fish at a market:** can run into the tens of thousands of yen when the season is hot. Locals often buy a portion, not the whole thing.

If a menu quotes a *jikaka* (market price) rather than a fixed number, that's a sign the kitchen is buying day to day — ask before ordering.

## Buri-shabu vs. sashimi

Two ways locals eat it, and they're different experiences.

**Sashimi** shows off the fat directly. Good winter buri is rich, almost buttery, and doesn't need much beyond a little soy and wasabi. If you only try one form, this is the honest test of freshness.

**Buri-shabu** is thin slices swished briefly in hot broth — a few seconds, not cooked through — then dipped in ponzu. The heat softens the fat and mellows the flavor. It's the more forgiving dish if you find straight raw fish too intense, and it's a warm, sociable meal for a cold night.

## Where locals go

I won't send you to a specific address, partly because the good spot one winter isn't always the good spot the next. In practice, people eat buri in a few settings: neighborhood *izakaya* and sushi counters in the cities, ryokan set dinners along the coast (often the priciest and most polished route), and home cooking from fish bought at a local market. If you're staying at a coastal inn during peak season, a buri course is frequently offered — worth confirming when you book, since not every property runs it every night.

A practical tip: reserve ahead in late December and January. Peak-season buri courses at popular places do sell out, and walking in cold can mean the kitchen has already run out of the good cuts for the day.

## Fact-check note

- ✅ Winter (roughly Dec–Feb) is when yellowtail carries the most fat and is called *kan-buri*.
- ✅ Peak-season *kan-buri* is noticeably more expensive than off-season buri.
- ⚠️ Exact price ranges shift year to year with the catch — treat the numbers above as a guide, not a quote.
- ⚠️ The *Himi kan-buri* name has a certification behind it, but criteria and qualifying volumes vary; confirm locally before assuming a fish is certified.
- ⚠️ Availability of buri-shabu courses depends on the season and the property — verify when booking.
