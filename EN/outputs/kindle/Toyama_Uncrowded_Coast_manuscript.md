# Why Toyama — Japan Beyond the Golden Route

This opening chapter makes the honest case for Toyama: who it's for, who should happily skip it, how it stacks up against neighbouring Kanazawa, and whether it's a safe, easy place to travel. Read it to decide whether this quiet stretch of the Sea-of-Japan coast belongs on your trip at all.

## Is Toyama worth visiting?

Here's the honest version, from someone who actually lives here. Toyama is not a blockbuster destination, and it shouldn't pretend to be. There's no single must-see that ends up on every Japan highlight reel. But if you're the kind of traveller who's already done Tokyo and Kyoto and wants somewhere real, scenic and uncrowded, Toyama is one of the best detours on Japan's west coast. Let me tell you who it's perfect for — and who should skip it.

## The honest case for the detour

The single best reason is how easy it is to combine with what you're probably already doing. Toyama is about 30 minutes by train from Kanazawa and reachable by bullet train from Tokyo — so it slots in as a day trip or a calm two-night base after the big cities. You trade the famous-name checklist for fresh seafood, real scenery and streets without crowds. For a lot of repeat visitors to Japan, that's exactly the trade they're looking for.

## So — worth it?

For a first, short trip to Japan: probably not yet. For a second trip, or anyone who wants the country at a quieter, more local altitude: yes, clearly. If that sounds like you, start with things to do, decide when to go, and pick where to stay.

## Off the beaten path in Japan: 7 reasons to choose Toyama

Kyoto is magical — and, these days, mobbed. If you're after the Japan behind the postcards, where you can still feel like you've discovered something, point yourself at the Sea of Japan coast. Here are seven honest reasons a local thinks Toyama is one of the country's most rewarding under-the-radar regions — and barely a foreign tourist in sight.

## 1. The Alps literally rise out of the sea

On a clear morning at Amaharashi or Himi, 3,000-metre snow peaks appear to grow straight out of the bay. There are only a handful of places on Earth like it, and almost no one photographs it.

## 2. Possibly the best-value seafood in Japan

Toyama Bay is one of the country's richest fishing grounds. White shrimp, winter yellowtail, snow crab, kelp-cured sashimi — even a modest counter serves fish that would cost a fortune in Tokyo. See the food guide.

## 3. A 400-year-old craft town

Takaoka has been casting bronze for four centuries. Walk its old metal-casters' streets and you're in a working craft city, not a museum.

## 4. Snow villages without the crowds

Everyone queues for Shirakawa-go. The UNESCO thatched villages of Gokayama are just as beautiful and far quieter.

## 5. One of Japan's great mountain journeys

The Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route climbs over the Northern Alps by cable car, ropeway and bus — with towering walls of spring snow.

## 6. It's a manga pilgrimage

Toyama is the birthplace of the creators of Doraemon and Ninja Hattori-kun. Two beloved legends, two small towns, one short train line. See the pilgrimage guide.

## 7. It's absurdly easy to reach

For somewhere this under-visited, it's remarkably accessible: about 2 hours from Tokyo by bullet train, and just 20 minutes from Kanazawa — so you can add it without backtracking. See the day trip or how many days you need.

## So, is Toyama worth it?

If you want temples-and-crowds Japan, stay on the Golden Route. If you want a quieter, more local, more delicious Japan — with mountains, sea and real craft — Toyama is one of the best bets in the country. Stay a night or two and see for yourself.

## Toyama or Kanazawa? Which to visit — and how to do both

If you're planning the Sea of Japan side of central Japan, you've probably landed on the same question many travelers do: Toyama or Kanazawa? The honest answer is that they're very different in character — and, because they're barely 20 minutes apart by train, you don't really have to choose. Here's a local's straight comparison to help you decide where to spend your time.

## The short answer

Kanazawa is the polished, famous one: a celebrated garden, preserved geisha and samurai districts, gold leaf, and excellent museums. Toyama is the quieter, more local one: arguably even better seafood, mountains that rise straight out of the sea, craft towns, and a fraction of the crowds. Kanazawa is the headline; Toyama is the place you tell your friends about afterwards.

## Choose Kanazawa if…

…you want a compact, classic Japanese city experience: famous gardens, atmospheric old streets, museums and refined food, all walkable. It's a superb first stop on this coast and deserves its reputation.

## Choose Toyama if…

…you'd rather trade polish for authenticity: some of Japan's best-value seafood, dramatic sea-and-mountain scenery, real craft towns like Takaoka and the fishing port of Himi, and the quiet thrill of being somewhere most foreign tourists skip. If you're a manga or anime fan, Toyama also wins outright as the home of two legendary creators.

## Honestly? Do both

This is the move most people miss. Kanazawa and Toyama are about 20–25 minutes apart on the train, so you can base yourself in either and day-trip to the other. A classic combination: a couple of days in Kanazawa for the highlights, then a day in Toyama for the food, the coast and the calm. See how to get around for the practical details.

## Where to stay

If Toyama is in your plan, basing yourself in Toyama City gives you easy reach of both the coast and the mountains; a seaside inn toward Himi is the scenic alternative.

## Is Toyama safe?

Short version, from someone who lives here: yes, Toyama is a calm, low-crime place, and it's one of the easier corners of Japan to travel solo or on a first trip. But "safe" doesn't mean "nothing to think about." The real things to plan around here aren't crime — they're nature: heavy winter snow, the occasional earthquake, and bears in the mountains. Here's the honest, practical version, with no scare-mongering and no false promises.

## Everyday safety & crime

Japan consistently ranks among the safest countries in the world, with very low levels of violent crime, and as of 2025 it sits at the lowest tier of the U.S. State Department's travel advisory (Level 1: exercise normal precautions). Toyama is a smaller, residential region rather than a big nightlife city, so it's quieter still. Petty theft and street crime are uncommon, though never zero — keep the same everyday habits you'd use anywhere: mind your bag, don't leave a phone or wallet unattended on a café table, and use a hotel safe for valuables.

One genuinely reassuring local habit: lost property tends to come back. If you leave something on a train or in a shop, ask staff or report it at a police box (kōban) — there's a real chance it turns up.

## Walking at night

Toyama's towns are generally comfortable to walk in after dark, and many solo travellers — including women — describe Japan as one of the easier places to feel at ease at night. That said, "comfortable" isn't a guarantee, and common sense still applies anywhere in the world: stick to lit main streets, keep to areas with some foot traffic, and trust your instincts if a place feels off. Outside the city centre, streets get dark and very quiet early, so plan your route back before you head out for dinner.

## Earthquakes

Earthquakes are a normal part of life in Japan and can strike with little warning, so it's worth knowing the basics rather than worrying about them. After the January 2024 Noto Peninsula earthquake, nearby Hokuriku cities including Toyama and Takaoka felt strong shaking but, in Toyama Prefecture, did not see the widespread damage seen closer to the epicentre. Simple, calm preparation goes a long way:

If the ground shakes: protect your head, get under a sturdy table if you can, and stay away from windows. Don't rush outside mid-tremor. When it stops, follow hotel staff instructions and watch for aftershocks. Coastal areas may issue tsunami advisories — if you're near the sea and told to, move to higher ground.

When you check in, take ten seconds to note your hotel's evacuation route, and keep your phone charged — it's your map, translator and alert system in one.

## Bears in the mountains

If you plan to hike, this is worth taking seriously and not waving away. Japan has seen a sharp rise in bear sightings and encounters in recent years, with most activity in rural and mountainous areas, and bears most active from spring through late autumn. Cities and ordinary tourist routes are not where the risk lies — hiking trails and forest edges are. Sensible precautions:

## Health, food & tap water

Tap water is safe to drink, food hygiene standards are high, and pharmacies are easy to find. Carry any prescription medicine in its original packaging, and note that some common Western medications are restricted in Japan — check before you travel if you rely on something specific. Travel insurance is still worth having; ambulances themselves are free, but hospital treatment is not.

## Emergency numbers to know

Police — 110Crime, theft, traffic accidents, or anything needing the police.

Fire & ambulance — 119Medical emergencies and fires. Ambulance transport to hospital is free.

Coast guard (at sea) — 118Accidents or distress on the water.

These numbers are free, work 24 hours, and can be dialled from any phone, including public payphones. Operators answer in Japanese; many areas can connect you to an interpreter, but it helps to have your location written down or saved, and to speak slowly and simply.

## So — is it safe?

For a solo or first-time traveller, Toyama is one of the more reassuring places in Japan to visit: low crime, easy public transport, helpful locals. The honest caveats are about nature, not people — respect the winter snow, know what to do in an earthquake, and take real care if you head into the mountains. Plan for those and you're very well placed. If that sounds like your kind of trip, see the travel FAQ, work out when to go, sort out getting around, and start with the Toyama guide home.

# Getting There & Around

Everything you need to reach Toyama and move around once you arrive — the bullet train, whether a rail pass pays off, the trams and buses, and the honest verdict on renting a car. By the end you'll know how to come from Tokyo, Kanazawa or Kansai, and how to link the city, the coast, the craft town and the mountains without stress.

## The quick version

Exact times and connections change with timetable updates, so always confirm current schedules when you book.

## From Tokyo

This is the easiest route. The Hokuriku Shinkansen runs directly from Tokyo Station to Toyama in about two hours. Trains are frequent, comfortable and punctual, and the ride itself is scenic as you approach the mountains. If Toyama is your main Sea-of-Japan stop, a simple round trip from Tokyo works beautifully. For Takaoka, get off one stop earlier at Shin-Takaoka.

## From Kanazawa

Kanazawa and Toyama are close neighbours — barely 20–25 minutes apart by train. This is why Toyama makes such a natural add-on to a Kanazawa trip: you can come for a single day or stay overnight without any real travel friction. If you're already in Kanazawa, there's almost no excuse not to see the quieter side of the coast. See our day-trip itinerary from Kanazawa.

## From Kyoto and Osaka

From the Kansai region, the usual route is the limited express "Thunderbird" up to Tsuruga, then a transfer to the Hokuriku Shinkansen onward to Toyama — roughly three to three and a half hours in total. It's a single, logical chain of trains rather than anything complicated, and it pairs Toyama neatly with a wider Kyoto–Kanazawa–Toyama itinerary.

## Rail passes — worth it?

If you're combining Toyama with Tokyo, Kanazawa and the Alps, a regional or nationwide rail pass can pay for itself, since the Shinkansen legs add up quickly. If you're only doing a short there-and-back, individual tickets may be simpler. Do the maths against your actual route before buying — passes reward travelers who move around a lot.

## Getting around once you arrive

Toyama City is compact and tram-friendly, and the surrounding sights are close: Takaoka (bronze craft and the Great Buddha) and the Amaharashi Coast are a short ride away, the fishing port of Himi is on the local JR Himi Line, and the Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route climbs into the mountains from a local railway. You can comfortably do a mountain day and a coast day from a single base.

## Where to base yourself

For most travelers, Toyama City is the easiest base thanks to its train and tram connections, with hotels clustered around the station. If you want sea views and crab-season dinners, a seaside inn toward Himi is a lovely alternative. Book ahead in spring (Alpine Route season) and winter (crab season).

## In short

From Tokyo, it's a two-hour bullet train. From Kanazawa, it's around the corner. From Kyoto or Osaka, it's a single easy transfer. However you come, Toyama rewards the small effort with food, mountains and a calmer side of Japan — see when to visit to time it well.

## Rail passes for Toyama — worth it?

Rail passes can save you real money in Japan — or quietly waste it. For a lot of Toyama trips, the honest answer is that you don't need one. But it depends entirely on your wider route. Here's a clear, current breakdown of the three passes that matter for Toyama, what each actually covers, and the one catch that trips people up every year.

## Read this first: no JR pass covers the Alpine Route

The single most common mistake: assuming a rail pass covers the Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route. It doesn't. The route is run by private mountain operators, so the Japan Rail Pass and the Hokuriku Arch Pass will get you to Toyama (and to Nagano on the other side) but not along the route itself — you pay that separately (see Alpine Route costs). The one exception is the Alpine-Takayama-Matsumoto pass below, which does include it.

## 1. Japan Rail Pass (nationwide)

The famous all-Japan pass. It covers the Hokuriku Shinkansen from Tokyo to Toyama (about ¥13,000 one way on its own, ~130–160 min), plus JR trains nationwide. After several price rises it's now expensive, so it only pays off if you're doing a lot of long-distance rail across the whole country. For a trip centred on Toyama and Hokuriku, it's usually overkill.

## 2. Hokuriku Arch Pass

The regional pass built for exactly this corner of Japan: unlimited JR travel between Tokyo and Osaka via the Hokuriku Shinkansen — through Toyama and Kanazawa — for 7 consecutive days, including JR access to Narita and Kansai airports.

## 3. Alpine-Takayama-Matsumoto Area Pass — the one that covers the route

If crossing the Alpine Route is central to your trip, this is the pass to look at. It gives unlimited travel roughly between Nagoya and Toyama (taking in Takayama and Matsumoto) and, crucially, includes all transport along the Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route itself.

## The honest bottom line

Rail passes are sold hard, but they're not automatically a saving — recent price increases mean that for a focused Toyama trip, ordinary tickets are frequently the cheaper choice. Add up your actual planned journeys first, then buy a pass only if it genuinely beats the sum. And remember the catch: budget for the Alpine Route on top, unless you're on the Alpine-Takayama-Matsumoto pass. Whatever you choose, base yourself somewhere well-connected — see where to stay in Toyama.

## Getting around Toyama

Good news for trip planning: Toyama is one of the easier parts of rural Japan to get around without a car. The main sights line up along train and tram routes, the city is small and walkable, and the bullet train puts you in the centre of it all. Here's how a local actually moves between the city, the coast, the craft town and the mountains — and the honest answer on whether you need to rent a car.

## Getting here

The Hokuriku Shinkansen (bullet train) runs to Toyama from Tokyo, and Shin-Takaoka Station serves Takaoka. From Kanazawa it's about 30 minutes by train, which is why Toyama works so well as a day trip or a calm base after the big cities. Full details: how to get to Toyama.

## Out to the mountains and gorges

The big nature is reached by a mix of rail, cable car and bus rather than by driving. The Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route is a chain of mountain transport you ride through (cars can't cross it anyway), and the Kurobe Gorge has its own scenic trolley train. The thatched villages of Gokayama are reached by bus. For most visitors, public transport covers the highlights comfortably.

## Do you need a car?

Honestly, most visitors don't. If your trip is the city, the coast, Takaoka and the famous mountain routes, trains, trams and buses will get you everywhere — and the Alpine Route and gorge are experiences you ride, not drive. A car only starts to make sense if you want to chase quiet rural corners on your own schedule, or you're travelling with a lot of luggage or small children. Otherwise, base yourself well and let the trains do the work. If you do want to drive, see our full guide to renting a car in Toyama (including the winter-snow caveats).

## A practical tip

Because the sights fan out from a couple of hubs, where you stay matters more than how you travel. Pick a well-connected base and most days become a simple out-and-back. See where to stay in Toyama.

Here's the honest local view: you don't need a car for Toyama's headline sights — trains, trams and buses reach the city, the coast, Takaoka and the famous mountain routes just fine. But a rental car genuinely opens up the other half of Toyama: the quiet villages, the small fishing ports, the back-road onsen, and the freedom to go on your own schedule. # When to Go

Toyama changes completely with the seasons, and a few of its best experiences happen only in a narrow window. This chapter walks through the year season by season — what to see, what's in season on the plate, and how many days to give it — so you can time your visit to whatever you most want.

## The best time to visit Toyama — by season

Toyama rewards travelers who time it right. Because it sits between the deep Toyama Bay and the high Northern Alps, the seafood and the scenery change completely through the year — and a few of its best experiences only happen in a narrow window. Here's an honest season-by-season guide to help you decide when to come, depending on what you most want to see and eat.

## Spring — the headline season

If you only know one thing about this region, it's probably the Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route and its towering walls of snow. Spring also brings the strange, beautiful firefly squid to the bay and the first sweet white shrimp. It's popular for good reason — reserve transport and rooms early.

## Summer — mountains and calm

The cities get warm, but the high country stays cool, making summer ideal for alpine air, ponds and easy hiking. Down on the coast, white shrimp is in season and the pace is relaxed.

## Winter — the most dramatic

This is when the bay shows off: rich Himi yellowtail, snow crab, and — on a clear morning at Amaharashi Coast — the Alps rising straight out of the sea. The thatched villages of Gokayama under snow are the postcard image of rural Japan. Pack warm and book crab-season inns well ahead.

## So, when should you go?

Want the snow corridor and a buzz? Spring. Want crab, yellowtail and the most dramatic sea-and-mountain views? Winter. Want it mild, quiet and easy? Autumn. Whichever you choose, basing yourself in Toyama City keeps the bay and the mountains both within easy reach.

## How many days do you need in Toyama?

It's the most practical planning question, and the honest answer is: more than most people give it. A lot of travelers squeeze Toyama into a rushed day trip from Kanazawa — and it's lovely as that — but the region really rewards an overnight or two, and slots beautifully into a longer, relaxed Hokuriku trip. Here's how to think about the nights, depending on your time.

## 1 night — a proper taste

Even a single night beats a day trip: you get the late-afternoon and early-morning light, an unhurried seafood dinner, and a dawn view over the bay. Do the coast-and-culture loop from the day-trip guide, but sleep over — ideally at a seaside inn toward Himi for crab or yellowtail.

## 2–3 nights — do it justice

This is the sweet spot. Give one day to the coast and the manga pilgrimage, one to the mountains (the Alpine Route in season, or the Kurobe Gorge), and a relaxed day for Toyama City and food. Spend at least one of those nights at an onsen inn — a hot-spring ryokan dinner is a trip highlight in itself. Follow the 2–3 day itinerary.

## 4–5 nights — the full Hokuriku trip

With more time, don't just stay longer in one spot — string the region together. A classic relaxed route: a couple of nights around Kanazawa (gardens, old districts), two or three around Toyama (coast, mountains, onsen), and you're within reach of Takayama and the Alps too. Staying multiple nights in each base — rather than packing and unpacking daily — is far more restful, and lets you slow down for the food and the scenery. See Toyama or Kanazawa for how the two compare.

## Where to stay (and book ahead)

For a multi-night trip, basing in Toyama City keeps the mountains and coast both in reach, with one night moved to an onsen or seaside inn for variety. Multi-night and onsen stays are popular — book ahead, especially in spring (Alpine Route) and winter (crab).

## Toyama in spring

Spring might be the single best time to visit Toyama. It's when the mountains open up for the year's most famous spectacle — walls of snow towering over a mountain road — while down on the coast the sea puts on its own strange, glowing show. Add the first sweet shrimp of the year and cherry blossom, and you have a season that's hard to beat. Here's a local's guide to a Toyama spring.

## What to eat

Spring brings the year's first white shrimp (shiroebi) — delicate and sweet — and firefly squid, usually boiled or marinated. It's a fresh, light counterpoint to winter's rich crab and yellowtail. See the food guide.

## Where to stay

Toyama City is the easiest base for both the Alpine Route and the coast; a seaside inn toward Himi adds bay views and seafood dinners.

## Toyama in summer

Japanese summers can be punishingly hot and humid — which is exactly why Toyama is such a clever choice. Within an hour or two of the warm coast, you can be standing in cool alpine air at 2,000 metres, or riding a little train into a deep, green river gorge. Down on the bay, the season brings the year's sweetest white shrimp. If you want mountains, water and seafood without the crowds of the famous summer spots, here's a local's guide to a Toyama summer.

## What to eat

Summer is prime time for white shrimp (shiroebi) — the "jewel of Toyama Bay" — as sashimi or a crisp kakiage tempura. Lighter seasonal white fish and local sushi round out a fresh, summer-friendly table. See the food guide.

## Where to stay

Toyama City makes an easy base for both the gorge and the high mountains; a seaside inn toward Himi trades that for sea breezes and shrimp dinners.

## Toyama in autumn

Ask a local for the most underrated time to visit, and a lot of us will quietly say autumn. The mountains catch some of the earliest foliage in Japan, the weather turns mild and easy after the summer humidity, the crowds thin out, and the bay starts getting rich again as the water cools. You get the scenery and the food without the spring rush or the deep-winter logistics. Here's a local's guide to a Toyama autumn.

## What to eat

As the water cools, the bay turns generous again — seasonal white fish, kelp-cured sashimi and local sushi all come back into their own, with the first hints of the winter richness to come. It's a fine, comfortable season to work through the food guide without the peak-season scramble.

## Where to stay

Toyama City is the easy, well-connected base for both the gorge and the high mountains; a seaside inn toward Himi trades that for sea views and seafood dinners as the bay turns rich.

## Toyama in winter

If you ask a local when Toyama is at its best, many will say winter — and not despite the cold, but because of it. This is when the bay turns generous (crab, rich yellowtail), when the air clears enough for the Northern Alps to float above the sea, and when the thatched mountain villages disappear under deep, quiet snow. It takes a little planning, but a Toyama winter is one of the most rewarding seasons anywhere in Japan. Here's how to do it.

# Toyama City & the Coast

This chapter covers the flat, walkable heart of the region: Toyama City as a base, its contemporary glass-art scene, and the stretch of coast at Amaharashi and Himi where the Alps appear to rise straight out of the sea. Read it for where to anchor yourself and where to catch the region's most famous view.

## Toyama City — glass, a canal park, and the perfect base

Most people pass through Toyama City on the bullet train without stopping. That's a missed chance. It's a clean, walkable, surprisingly stylish city on the Sea of Japan — with a world-class glass art scene, a canal park that holds one of the prettiest cafés in Japan, and retro trams that make getting around half the fun. Best of all, it's the ideal base for everything around it: the bay, the craft town of Takaoka, the fishing port of Himi, and the high mountains. Here's how a local would use it.

## Why base yourself here

Toyama City sits on the Hokuriku Shinkansen (about 2 hours from Tokyo), with easy local trains and trams fanning out in every direction. Stay here and you can do a mountain day and a coast day without changing hotels. It's also relaxed and affordable compared with the bigger tourist cities — you get good food and calm streets without the crowds.

## Use it as a hub

This is the real reason to stay. From Toyama City you can reach Takaoka (bronze craft, the Great Buddha) and Himi (a fishing port where the Alps rise over the sea) in well under an hour, take the Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route up into the mountains, or make the easy day trip loop if you're coming from Kanazawa. Mountains one day, sea the next — all from one base.

## Where to stay

Hotels cluster conveniently around Toyama Station, which makes early starts for the mountains or the coast painless. Book ahead in the spring Alpine Route season and in winter (crab season), when rooms fill up.

## When to go

Spring and autumn are mild and easy; spring also opens the Alpine Route. Winter brings crab and yellowtail (and snow). Summer is green and good for escaping the heat in the nearby mountains. The city works year-round as a comfortable, well-connected base.

## Toyama glass — a modern craft, and a museum worth the stop

Beyond seafood and mountains, Toyama City has quietly become a center for contemporary glass art. It makes an easy, indoor half-day — especially handy on a rainy or very cold day — anchored by a striking modern museum in the center of town.

## Why glass, here?

The city has invested in glass as a modern craft for decades, building studios, a school and public collections. The result is a small but real scene of contemporary glassmaking you can actually see and, in places, buy.

## The Glass Art Museum

The Toyama Glass Art Museum sits in a bold modern building in the city center (shared with the public library), with a calm wood-and-light interior and rotating glass exhibitions. It is central, easy to reach, and a good rainy-day option.

## Make a half-day of it

# The Mountains — Alps, Gorges & Falls

Here is the high country: the Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route with its towering snow walls, the little Kurobe Gorge trolley train, Japan's tallest waterfall, and the UNESCO thatched villages of Gokayama. This chapter explains what each experience is, when it's open, what it really costs, and how to pair the mountains with the coast.

## The Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route, from the Toyama side

Imagine a bus inching through a canyon carved out of snow, with white walls rising higher than a four-storey building on either side. That's the Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route in spring — one of Japan's most extraordinary mountain journeys, and a lot easier to reach than most travelers realize. The route climbs from the lowlands up over the Northern Japan Alps using a chain of cable cars, ropeways and buses. Here's how a local would ride it from the Toyama side, and how to pair it with the quiet coast at Takaoka and Himi.

## What the Alpine Route actually is

It's not a single train or road — it's a relay. You cross the mountains by switching between mountain railways, a cableway, a long ropeway with no support towers, and buses through the high passes. Private cars can't drive the whole thing, which is part of why the scenery stays so clean. The journey links Toyama Prefecture with Nagano on the far side, climbing to around 2,450m at its highest point, Murodo.

## The famous snow corridor (and the other seasons)

Opening dates shift slightly each year and sections can pause for weather, so always check the official schedule before you commit.

## Riding it from Toyama

From Toyama city (on the Hokuriku Shinkansen, ~2 hours from Tokyo), a local mountain railway carries you to the foot of the route, where the climb begins. You can travel the whole way across to Nagano, or go up to Murodo and come back down the Toyama side — a good option if you're basing yourself on the coast. It's popular, so reserving ahead in peak spring and autumn is wise.

## Make a trip of it: mountains + coast

Here's the move most visitors miss. Pair the high mountains with the low coast. After the Alpine Route, drop back down to Toyama and spend a day on the bay — historic metal-craft Takaoka and the fishing port of Himi, where on a clear day those same Tateyama peaks float above the sea. You'll have seen the range from 2,450m up and from sea level in a single trip. See the one-day coast itinerary for how.

## Tateyama Alpine Route: costs & how to do it

The Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route is one of Japan's most spectacular mountain journeys — and, honestly, one of its more expensive day trips. It's worth it, but you should walk in knowing the real numbers, the season, and how to plan so you don't get caught out. Here's the practical, honest version for 2026, from the Toyama side.

## What it costs — be ready, it's not cheap

## The sections you ride (Toyama side → Nagano)

You don't drive any of this — you switch between cable cars, a highland bus, a ropeway and tunnel buses. ## Shomyo Falls — Japan's tallest waterfall, off the Tateyama snow

Shomyo Falls, in the mountains of Toyama, drops about 350 m — the tallest waterfall in Japan. It is fed by snowmelt from the Tateyama range, so it is at its most powerful in spring and early summer. It sees a fraction of the crowds of Japan's famous sights.

## How big is it?

At roughly 350 m, Shomyo is Japan's tallest waterfall by drop. In the snowmelt season a second cascade, Hannoki Falls, appears alongside it and is seasonally even taller — together they are a genuinely large sight, not a roadside trickle.

Toyama sits on one of Japan's richest fishing grounds, the deep, cold Toyama Bay, with the Northern Alps rising right behind it. The result is a quiet food paradise that most travelers miss. The everyday Toyama crab is the red snow crab (benizuwai), landed from the deep bay. It is delicate and sweet, often sold boiled and ready to eat. The pricier zuwai (snow crab) also appears in season; benizuwai is the one locals reach for more often.

## White shrimp (shiroebi) — the jewel of Toyama Bay

There's a seafood in Toyama so delicate that locals call it "the jewel of the bay." It's a tiny, almost transparent shrimp called shiroebi — white shrimp — and it's caught in meaningful quantities in just a handful of places on earth, with Toyama Bay the most famous of them. If you only try one local specialty here, make it this one. Here's what it is, how to eat it, and how to plan a trip to taste it at its best.

## What makes shiroebi special

Shiroebi are small — just a few centimetres — and so pale they look almost translucent when fresh, with a faint pink shimmer. They live in the deep underwater canyons of Toyama Bay, and because they're fragile and spoil quickly, truly fresh shiroebi is hard to find outside the region. The taste is the opposite of what "shrimp" suggests to most people: clean, gently sweet, almost creamy, with none of the heaviness of larger prawns.

## When it's in season

Shiroebi fishing in Toyama Bay generally runs from spring into autumn (roughly April to around autumn), with the exact dates set by local fishery rules each year. Spring is prized for the first catch. Outside the season, you'll still find it frozen or as senbei and other products — but if you can, time a visit for the fresh season.

## Where to try it

The freshest shiroebi is eaten close to where it's landed, around Toyama Bay. Even a modest sushi counter or a place near the station can serve it beautifully, and the price is far gentler than the same quality would be in Tokyo. The easiest way to plan it is to stay a night near the bay and make a leisurely meal of it.

## A note for first-timers

Don't drown it in soy sauce. Shiroebi is all about a soft, sweet, fleeting flavour — treat it gently and it'll be one of the most memorable bites of your trip. And if you fall for it, the senbei travel well as a gift.

## Firefly squid: the spring nights Toyama Bay glows blue

Some natural wonders you have to travel a long way to see. This is one of them. Every spring, the deep waters of Toyama Bay fill with millions of tiny squid called hotaruika — "firefly squid" — that glow with an eerie, electric-blue light. On the right night, the shoreline shimmers as if the sea has swallowed the stars. It's one of the strangest and most beautiful light shows in the natural world, it happens in only a handful of places, and Toyama is the most famous of them. Here's how it works — and yes, you can also eat them.

## What are firefly squid?

Firefly squid are small — only a few centimetres long — and live deep in the bay. They're covered in tiny light-producing organs (photophores) that flash a vivid blue, probably to confuse predators and attract mates. In spring, vast numbers rise toward the surface to spawn, and waves and tides can push glowing masses of them right up to the shore.

## When and where to see them glow

The season runs roughly March to May, peaking in spring. The blue glow is best seen on dark nights, often in the small hours before dawn, along the Toyama Bay coast — and it depends heavily on tides, moon and weather, so it's never guaranteed. Some seasons put on special boat tours to witness it offshore. If seeing the glow is your dream, build in flexible nights and check local conditions; nature doesn't run to a timetable.

## Masuzushi — Toyama's trout pressed sushi

Masuzushi is Toyama's best-known local sushi: vinegared trout pressed over a round of rice, wrapped in bamboo leaves and packed in a thin wooden hoop. It travels well, keeps for a day, and has long been a favorite train lunch (ekiben).

## What it is

Unlike the nigiri you slice and eat at once, masuzushi is a pressed sushi: the fish and rice are packed together and weighted, then cut into wedges like a cake. The bamboo leaves aren't decoration — they add a faint aroma and help it keep.

## Why Toyama

The dish grew up around the rivers and rail of Toyama, and became a classic station lunch sold across the region. Each shop guards its own balance of vinegar, salt and how lightly or firmly the trout is cured.

## Where to find it

Look around Toyama City and its station. For the wider picture of the region's food, see what to eat in Toyama.

## Toyama Black — the ramen built to refuel workers

Toyama Black is a striking, jet-dark bowl of ramen — soy-sauce broth so deep it looks like ink. It was not made to be trendy. It was made strong and salty to feed hard-working laborers in the postwar years, and the look is just a side effect.

## Where it comes from

The story goes that a Toyama shop seasoned the broth heavily so workers — sweating through long days of rebuilding — could flavor their rice and replace the salt they had lost. Function first; the dramatic color came along for the ride.

## What it tastes like

Expect a sharp, salty, peppery soy hit, usually with thick noodles, plenty of coarse black pepper, menma (bamboo) and slices of pork. It is intense — many locals eat it with a bowl of plain rice rather than finishing the broth.

## Where to try it

Several Toyama Black shops cluster around Toyama City, and a few have branches in larger cities. While you're in the area, balance it with the bay's lighter flavors — see what to eat in Toyama.

## The town that wraps rice balls in kelp, not seaweed

Almost everyone who loves Japanese food knows the onigiri — the triangular rice ball wrapped in a crisp sheet of dark nori seaweed. So here's a small surprise from the Sea of Japan coast: in Toyama, people often skip the nori entirely and wrap their rice balls in soft, fluffy tororo kombu — finely shaved kelp — instead. It looks like the rice ball is wearing a pale, feathery cloud. And it's delicious. Here's the story behind one of Japan's most charming regional food quirks.

## Why kelp instead of seaweed?

It comes down to history. Toyama barely harvests any kelp of its own — but for centuries it was a key port on the old kitamae-bune trade routes that carried premium kelp down from Hokkaido. Kelp piled up here, local artisans learned to shave it paper-thin, and a deep kelp culture took root. Today Toyama has one of the highest kelp-consumption rates in all of Japan. Wrapping onigiri in shaved kelp is just one delicious expression of that obsession.

## What does it taste like?

Where nori is crisp and faintly smoky, tororo kombu is soft, melting and savoury, with a gentle, building umami. As it sits against the warm rice it half-dissolves, so the kelp flavour seeps into every bite rather than staying on the outside. It's comforting in a way that's hard to describe until you've tried it — the kind of simple thing locals get quietly homesick for.

## How to try it at home

It couldn't be easier: shape a warm rice ball (with a salty filling like pickled plum, salmon or bonito flakes), then press a loose handful of tororo kombu gently all around the outside until it's coated. A pinch of salt helps the umami pop. The same kelp also makes an instant soup — just put a pinch in a bowl with a little soy sauce and pour over hot water. For a fuller local food guide, see what to eat in Toyama.

## Where to taste the real thing

You'll find tororo kombu onigiri and kelp dishes all over Toyama — in shops, markets and home kitchens. It pairs perfectly with the bay's other treasures: white shrimp, kelp-cured sashimi and local sushi. The easiest way to dive into Toyama's food culture is to stay a night and eat your way around the bay.

## Toyama sake — what mountain water does to rice wine

Toyama sits below the Tateyama mountains, and the snowmelt water that runs off them is soft and clean. Combined with locally grown rice, it gives the region's sake a tendency toward a clean, dry, quietly food-friendly style — though every brewery has its own character.

## Why the water matters

Sake is mostly water, so its source shapes the result. Toyama's soft snowmelt tends to produce sake that is crisp rather than heavy — easy to pair with the bay's delicate seafood.

## What to try

Look for local junmai and ginjo styles. Many small breweries dot the prefecture; a chilled glass alongside white shrimp or sashimi is an easy, very local pairing.

## Where to stay in Toyama

Toyama isn't a place where you agonise over neighbourhoods — but where you base yourself does change the trip. The simple version: stay in the city if you want easy trains and a relaxed base, stay by the sea in Himi if you've come for crab and the Alps-over-the-bay view, and stay in Takaoka if you want a quieter, craft-town feel. Here's how a local would choose between them, and when to book.

## 1. Toyama City — the easy, well-connected base

For most first-time visitors, this is the right call. Hotels cluster around Toyama Station, so early starts for the mountains or the coast are painless, and you can reach Takaoka and Himi in well under an hour. It's clean, walkable and good value, with the Alpine Route and the Kanazawa day-trip loop both within easy reach. Mountains one day, sea the next — all from one base.

## 2. Himi — seaside inns, crab and yellowtail

If the food and the view are why you came, sleep by the sea. Himi is a fishing port known for winter yellowtail (kanburi) and crab, and its inns are built around generous seafood dinners — many with a shot at the dawn view of the Tateyama range floating above the bay from the Amaharashi Coast. This is the splurge-worthy, slow-paced choice. Crab-season rooms (roughly late autumn to winter) book out early.

## 3. Takaoka — quieter, with a craft-town feel

Takaoka is the 400-year bronze-casting town — home of the Great Buddha and the manga artist behind Doraemon. It's calmer than the city and handy for the western side of the prefecture (Gokayama's thatched villages, Himi, the coast). A good pick if you'd rather stay somewhere with a bit of local texture and don't mind a slightly smaller choice of hotels.

## So, which one?

One practical note: book well ahead for the spring Alpine Route season and for winter crab season — those are when rooms across the prefecture fill up fastest. See when to go for the seasonal picture.

## Money in Toyama

Japan has a reputation for being a cash country, and that's still partly true — but it's changing fast, and a traveller can now get through most of a trip with a card and a phone. Here's the honest, practical picture for Toyama: when you'll genuinely need yen in your pocket, where your foreign card will work, how to tap onto the trams, and a few things (like tipping) you can simply forget about.

## Do you need cash here?

Short answer: carry some, but you won't live on it. Hotels, larger restaurants, department stores, convenience stores and most attractions in Toyama City take cards comfortably. Where cash still rules is the small and lovely end of things — a tiny family-run eatery, a stall at a market, a little shrine donation box, a coin locker, or a single-operator bus in a quieter area. Across Japan, cashless payment has been rising and reached well over half of consumer spending in recent years, but smaller and rural businesses are slower to switch. A reasonable habit: keep a few thousand yen on you and reach for it at the small places.

Other convenience-store chains have ATMs as well, though foreign-card support varies, so the two above are the safest bets.

## Cards and QR pay — how far they get you

Credit and debit cards (Visa, Mastercard, and often JCB and others) are widely accepted at hotels, chain and mid-sized restaurants, shops and stations. QR-code phone payments — PayPay is the big one locally — have spread even to many small independent shops, and several overseas wallets can now link in for visitors. The honest caveat: acceptance is broad but not universal. A handful of the most characterful little places are still cash-only, and they rarely advertise it, so it's worth a glance for a card sticker before you order, or just asking. Treat cards and QR as your default and cash as your backup, not the other way round.

## IC cards for the trams and buses

For getting around, a rechargeable transport IC card is the smooth way to ride. If you already have a Suica, ICOCA, PASMO or another of Japan's nationwide IC cards from elsewhere on your trip, you can tap onto Toyama's trams and many buses with it — they joined the nationwide system, so there's no need for a separate card. Toyama also has its own local IC card (ecomyca), which residents use and which can carry a slightly cheaper local fare, but for a visitor the nationwide card you're already carrying is the simplest choice. You top these up with cash at machines, and the same card doubles as a tap-to-pay wallet at convenience stores and many shops. For routes and whether you even need much transport at all, see getting around Toyama.

## Changing money

If you'd rather bring foreign cash and exchange it, you can — but you rarely need much. Exchange counters exist at international airports and at some banks and larger hotels, and rates for major currencies like US dollars and euros are generally reasonable once you're in Japan. For most travellers, though, withdrawing yen from a convenience-store ATM as you go is simpler and competitive, so there's no need to over-prepare a big exchange before you fly. Smaller towns have fewer exchange options, so handle any currency exchange in a major city or at the airport rather than counting on it in a quiet area.

## No tipping — really

One nice simplification: tipping isn't part of the culture in Japan, and Toyama is no exception. You don't tip in restaurants, taxis or hotels, and trying to can cause polite confusion. The price you're told is the price you pay (tax included on displayed prices in most shops). Just settle the bill and go — no mental arithmetic required.

## A practical packing note

Bring at least one card that works abroad (ideally two, from different networks, in case one is finicky), make sure you know your PIN, and tell your bank you'll be travelling so a withdrawal abroad doesn't get blocked. Arrive with a little yen for the very first taxi, locker or snack, then top up at an ATM once you're settled. That's genuinely all the money planning Toyama needs.

## SIM, eSIM & Wi-Fi for Toyama (and Japan)

You'll want mobile internet from the moment you land — for maps, train times, translation and looking up where to eat. Toyama is no different from the rest of Japan here: the same four options apply, and the choice comes down to your phone, your group size and how much fuss you want. This is a plain-English rundown, current as of 2026, of how to stay connected on a Toyama trip — what each option is, where to pick one up around the city, and how much data you actually need.

## The four options, in one table

There are really only four ways to get online in Japan. Here's the honest short version before the detail:

For most solo travellers and couples in 2026 the simplest answer is an eSIM; for a family or a group sharing one connection, pocket Wi-Fi often works out cheaper and easier. Below is each in plain terms.

## eSIM — the simplest option for most phones

An eSIM is a digital SIM built into your phone — there's no physical chip. You buy a data plan online, you're sent a QR code by email, and you install it before you fly. Many plans only start counting down once you activate them after landing, so you can set everything up at home and switch it on when you arrive.

Will your phone work? Two things must be true: your phone supports eSIM, and it isn't locked to your home carrier. As a rough guide, iPhones from the iPhone XR onward support eSIM, as do recent Google Pixel and Samsung Galaxy flagship models — but check your own model and your carrier-lock status before buying. One known catch: iPhones sold in mainland China don't support eSIM at all, and some Hong Kong/Macao models are hit-or-miss.

Setting it up (the gist): on an iPhone, go to Settings → Cellular (Mobile Data) → Add eSIM, then scan the QR code from your provider. On Android it's typically Settings → Connections (or Network) → SIM manager → add an eSIM and scan the code. Do the install on Wi-Fi before you leave home; switch the line on after you land. This is where any future eSIM recommendation from this guide would live — for now, just buy from a reputable provider you've researched.

Note on data plans: some "unlimited" eSIMs actually give a generous high-speed allowance per day and then slow down until the next day; fixed plans (say 5GB or 10GB) run until used up. Read the fine print so the word "unlimited" doesn't surprise you.

## Physical SIM — for older or specific phones

A physical SIM is the traditional chip you slot into your phone, replacing your home SIM for the trip. It's a solid, stable option, especially if your phone doesn't do eSIM. The trade-offs: your phone must be unlocked, you have to physically swap (and not lose) your home SIM, some SIMs need manual network (APN) settings, and a few prepaid SIMs don't allow hotspot sharing. If your phone supports eSIM, an eSIM usually does the same job with less hassle.

## Pocket Wi-Fi — best for families and groups

A pocket Wi-Fi is a small rented router that creates your own Wi-Fi bubble; several phones, tablets or laptops connect to it at once. For a family or a group of friends this is often the cheapest and easiest route — one device, one bill, everyone online — and it keeps your home SIM untouched. The downsides are real but minor: it's an extra gadget to keep charged (batteries last a limited number of hours), and you usually have to return it, typically by dropping it in a prepaid envelope at a postbox or handing it back at an airport counter at the end of your rental.

## Free public Wi-Fi — a backup, not a plan

Japan does have free public Wi-Fi — at the airport, major train stations, many convenience stores, cafés and tourist information centres — and apps and services exist that bundle large networks of these hotspots together. It's genuinely useful as a free top-up. But it is hotspot-based: the moment you walk out of the station or café, the connection is gone. That's exactly when you tend to need maps or translation most, so treat free Wi-Fi as a backup and carry your own data as the main plan.

## Getting connected around Toyama

The cleanest approach is to sort your internet before you fly — buy and install an eSIM at home, or reserve a pocket Wi-Fi for airport or hotel pickup. Most pocket Wi-Fi and SIM rentals in Japan are set up around the big international gateways (Tokyo's Haneda and Narita, Kansai, Chubu and others), with pickup at the airport or your hotel and a prepaid return envelope.

If you're flying into the region, note that Toyama has its own small airport, and most visitors arrive via the Hokuriku Shinkansen into Toyama Station — so the practical move is to have your eSIM already live, or your pocket Wi-Fi posted to your first hotel, rather than counting on buying something on the spot in a smaller city. Around Toyama City and the station you'll also find free Wi-Fi in the usual places (the station area, convenience stores and tourist information), which makes a fine backup while you finish any setup. We don't list specific shops or counters here, as availability changes — confirm current options with the official airport, station or tourist-information sources when you plan.

## How much data do you actually need?

Less than you'd think. A typical tourist day — a couple of hours of maps and navigation, some translation, casual social media and looking up restaurants — runs to roughly 0.5–0.7 GB. As a rough planning rule, a 5 GB plan suits many week-long trips and 10 GB a two-week trip; reported median use across recent trips has been on the order of a few gigabytes total. Streaming a lot of video on the go pushes that up, so if you binge on trains, size up. When you're on free Wi-Fi at the hotel or station, you're not touching your plan at all.

## The honest bottom line

If you have a recent, unlocked phone and you're travelling solo or as a couple, an eSIM is the least-fuss choice: set it up at home, switch it on when you land. Travelling as a family or group, or carrying an older phone, pocket Wi-Fi (or a physical SIM) is the friendlier pick. Either way, sort it before you arrive and keep free public Wi-Fi as a backup — and you'll spend your time in Toyama looking at the Alps over the bay, not at a "no signal" icon.

## What to wear in Toyama, by season

Toyama sits on the Sea of Japan side of the country, between a deep bay and the high Northern Alps — so the weather has more range than many first-time visitors expect. The lowland city is mild in spring and autumn, warm and humid in summer, and genuinely snowy in winter; the mountains are a different climate entirely. Here's an honest, local packing guide with rough temperature ranges so you bring the right layers. (All figures are approximate seasonal averages and vary year to year; check a forecast before you travel.)

## Spring — mild, but pack layers

Spring is one of the most comfortable times to visit. In the city, expect roughly 10–25&deg;C, with pleasant May days often in the low-to-mid 20s&deg;C; April has the lowest average humidity of the year, so it feels fresh rather than sticky. Mornings and evenings can still be cool, so bring a light jacket or a cardigan you can take off by midday. A compact umbrella is useful — spring rain comes and goes.

Heading up the Alpine Route? This is the big catch. The famous spring snow corridor on the Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route is high-altitude alpine terrain. At Murodo, around 2,450 m, mornings in the snow-wall season can be roughly 5–10&deg;C even when the city below is warm — it's real mountain weather. Wear a warm insulating layer, a windproof outer jacket, gloves, sunglasses, and sturdy shoes, plus strong sun protection (snow glare is intense). Treat it as a separate, colder climate from the city.

## Summer — warm, humid, and wet

Lowland summers are warm and humid. Highs commonly sit around 26–32&deg;C, and the air is muggy, especially in August. July tends to be the wettest month of the year, so pack light, breathable clothes, good sun protection, and a packable rain jacket or umbrella. The upside: the high mountains stay cool. Along the Alpine Route from roughly mid-June through September it's mild rather than hot, so if you go up, carry a light fleece or jacket even on a warm city day — the temperature drops sharply with altitude. See Toyama in summer for what's in season.

## Autumn — the comfortable sweet spot

Autumn is mild and arguably the easiest season to pack for. Temperatures ease from the low 20s&deg;C in September down toward single digits by late November, so think comfortable layers: a long-sleeve top, a light-to-medium jacket, and something warmer for evenings as the season progresses. Early September can still feel humid, while October and November are crisp and clear — ideal for foliage. By the end of autumn, a proper warm coat earns its place in your bag. More in Toyama in autumn.

## Winter — dress for serious snow

This is where Toyama surprises people. As a Sea-of-Japan-side city, it gets a lot of snow — among the snowiest of any sizeable city in Japan, with the heaviest snowfall typically in January. City temperatures often hover around 0–7&deg;C, with the coldest months near freezing, and it's frequently grey, windy and wet. Pack a properly warm, water-resistant coat, a hat, gloves, and — importantly — waterproof shoes or boots with good grip, because pavements get slushy and icy. Thermal base layers make a big difference if you'll be outdoors for the winter scenery, crab and yellowtail. Note that some high mountain access closes for the season.

## One trip, two climates — a quick rule

The simplest way to think about it: pack for the lowland season you're visiting in, then add a dedicated warm-and-windproof mountain layer if your plans include the Tateyama heights or other high country. The city and the mountains can easily differ by 15&deg;C or more on the same day. For timing your trip around weather and what's in season, see the best time to visit Toyama.

## Where to stay — and dry off

After a snowy or rainy day, a comfortable base with a hot bath is worth a lot. Basing yourself in Toyama City keeps the bay and the mountains both within easy reach, while a seaside inn is a treat in the cold yellowtail and crab season.

## Toyama with kids

Toyama is one of the easier parts of rural Japan to travel with children. The distances are short, the trains and trams are simple and step-free, there's a genuinely big park that's free to enter, and a rainy afternoon doesn't ruin the day because several of the best spots have indoor options. Here's how a local would plan a family trip — what actually keeps small kids happy, and the practical bits (trams, food, where to base yourself) that make the difference.

## A big free park (with a rainy-day option)

If you're travelling with children, the easiest win is a large family park in Takaoka — around 11 hectares of lawns, a river running through it, long slides and big climbing equipment. There's an indoor hall with a trampoline dome and a ball pool, so a sudden shower doesn't end the visit, and the open grass is made for letting kids run and picnic. The park is free to enter (some indoor facilities keep set hours), and it leans into the fact that Takaoka is the hometown of the manga artist behind Japan's most famous blue robot cat — there's a small, photo-friendly corner themed around the comic that families queue up for. Photography there is free. It's an easy half-day, and a good first stop if you arrive tired from the trains.

## A glass museum that's surprisingly kid-friendly

Back in the city, the Toyama Glass Art Museum sounds like an adults-only stop, but it works well with children. The building (designed by architect Kengo Kuma) also houses the city library, including a large children's section that runs storytelling and workshops, so one parent can browse glass while the kids settle into picture books. Admission to the museum is free for high-school age and younger, and it's an easy tram ride from Toyama Station — get off near the city-centre stops and it's a short walk. The glass installations are colourful and genuinely eye-catching for little ones.

## The tram ride is the activity

Don't overthink entertainment in the city — the trams do a lot of the work. They're cheap (a flat fare, with a reduced child fare for under-12s), step-free, and frequent, and small children tend to love riding the retro and modern carriages around town. You can string together the glass museum, the canal park and the station area almost entirely by tram, which keeps tired legs out of the equation. Spring for the Alpine Route snow corridor, winter for crab, yellowtail and the clearest Alps-over-the-sea views, and autumn for mild, quiet weather and foliage.

## Do you need a car?

For the headline sights, no — trains, trams and buses reach the city, the coast, Takaoka and the famous mountain routes. A car only helps if you want quiet rural corners on your own schedule.

## Is the Alpine Route covered by a JR pass?

No. The Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route is run by private mountain operators, so most JR passes get you to Toyama but not along the route itself — you pay that separately.

## Can you visit Shirakawa-go from Toyama?

Yes. There's a direct highway bus from Toyama Station, and quieter Gokayama — in Toyama itself — is reached by the World Heritage Bus from Takaoka or Shin-Takaoka.

## What food is Toyama known for?

White shrimp (shiroebi), winter yellowtail (buri) and crab, kelp-cured sashimi (kombu-jime), pressed trout sushi (masuzushi), and the inky, salty Toyama Black ramen.

## Is English spoken in Toyama?

Less than in Tokyo or Kyoto, but major stations, signage and tourist sites have English, and a translation app covers the rest. It's a comfortable, easy place to travel.

## Toyama or Kanazawa?

They're very different in character and only about 20 minutes apart, so the honest answer is do both: Kanazawa for gardens and old districts, Toyama for seafood, sea-and-mountain scenery and calm.

## A perfect day trip from Kanazawa: Takaoka & Himi

Kanazawa is wonderful — and increasingly crowded. Here's a secret the tour buses haven't caught onto: one stop east on the bullet train, a quieter, older, more delicious side of Japan is waiting, and almost no foreign visitors get off the train. Spend a single day in Takaoka and Himi and you'll see 400-year-old metal craft, a giant bronze Buddha, a real fishing port, and — on a clear day — the Alps rising straight out of the sea. Here's exactly how a local would plan it.

## Why do this instead of another day in Kanazawa

You've already seen Kenrokuen and the geisha districts. A day trip east costs you one short train ride and gives you something most travelers never get: an un-staged, lived-in Japan, with seafood pulled from the bay that morning — and space to breathe. This is the "Japan most tourists never see," and it's far easier to reach than people assume.

## What to eat

This is the part you'll remember. Toyama Bay is one of Japan's richest fishing grounds, so even modest counters punch far above their price. Look for white shrimp (shiroebi), the delicately sweet "jewel of the bay"; in winter, cold yellowtail (kanburi) as sashimi or hot-pot; and pressed-kelp sashimi (kombu-jime), a Toyama specialty. A seafood lunch here can be the best meal of your whole trip.

## Getting there (real times)

From Kanazawa, Takaoka is roughly 15–40 minutes depending on the train; Himi is about 30 minutes further on the local JR Himi Line. From Tokyo, take the Hokuriku Shinkansen to Shin-Takaoka (about 2.5–3 hours), then a short local ride. If you're combining Tokyo, Kanazawa and the Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route, a rail pass can pay for itself — worth checking before you go.

## Make it an overnight

If you can spare more than a day, stay a night in a Himi seaside inn (famous for winter crab and yellowtail dinners) or use Toyama city as a well-connected base. You'll catch the bay at dawn — the quietest, clearest time for that mountains-over-water view.

## Things to do in Toyama, Japan

Toyama doesn't shout for attention the way Kyoto or Kanazawa do — and that's exactly why it's such a rewarding place to spend a few days. Wedged between one of Japan's richest bays and a wall of 3,000-metre mountains, it packs dramatic scenery, deep food culture and real pop-culture history into a compact, easy-to-reach area. Here are the things a local would actually send you to do.

## 1. Watch the Alps rise out of the sea

On a clear morning, the Tateyama mountains appear to grow straight out of Toyama Bay — a rare sight you can catch from the Amaharashi Coast or the fishing town of Himi. Winter is the most dramatic. It's the kind of view that justifies the whole trip.

## 2. Ride the Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route

One of Japan's great mountain journeys — a relay of cable cars, ropeways and buses over the Northern Alps, famous for the springtime "snow corridor" of towering snow walls. A bucket-list day out from spring through autumn.

## 3. Follow the manga pilgrimage

Toyama is the birthplace of the legendary "Fujiko Fujio" duo: Doraemon's creator was from Takaoka, Ninja Hattori-kun's from Himi. For fans, the two-town manga pilgrimage is a genuinely moving, uncommercial experience.

## 4. Wander a craft town: Takaoka

Takaoka has been casting bronze for 400 years. Walk the old metal-casters' streets of Kanayamachi, see the Great Buddha, and feel a working craft city that most tourists never reach.

## 5. See UNESCO thatched villages at Gokayama

The famous "praying hands" thatched houses — like Shirakawa-go, but quieter. Gokayama gives you the storybook village without the crowds.

## 6. Eat your way around Toyama Bay

This might be the real headline. Don't miss white shrimp (shiroebi), winter yellowtail, kelp-cured sashimi and local sushi — see the food guide. Even modest counters serve fish that would cost a fortune in Tokyo.

## 7. Use Toyama City as a stylish base

Toyama City has a striking glass-art museum (by architect Kengo Kuma), a canal park with one of Japan's prettiest cafés, and retro trams — plus the train links to do all of the above from one hotel.

## How long do you need?

You can sample Toyama on a single day trip from Kanazawa, but two or three days lets you pair a mountain day with a coast day and a manga or craft day. See the best time to visit and how to get there.
