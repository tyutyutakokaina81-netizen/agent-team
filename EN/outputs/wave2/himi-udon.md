# Himi Udon: The Chewy Noodle You Haven't Heard Of

If you've eaten udon in Japan, it was probably Sanuki udon from Kagawa — thick, square-cut, with a firm bite. Or maybe you've had Inaniwa udon from the Akita area, which is thin, pale, and smooth. Himi udon, from a small coastal city in Toyama, sits somewhere between those two, and I think it deserves more attention than it gets.

I live in Toyama, about a half-hour drive from Himi, and I eat this noodle often enough to have opinions about it. Here's what it actually is, and how people around here eat it.

## What makes it different

Himi udon is *tenobe* — hand-stretched. Like Inaniwa, the dough is pulled by hand and oiled during the process, which gives the noodles a glossy surface and a thin-to-medium thickness. But Himi udon is noticeably chewier and springier than Inaniwa. Where Inaniwa slips down almost delicately, Himi udon has more resistance and bounce. It isn't as fat or square as Sanuki, either.

So the short version: thinner than Sanuki, chewier than Inaniwa. If you like texture in a noodle, this is the interesting middle ground.

I've been told the local production still involves a lot of hand-stretching rather than full machine extrusion, which is part of why the texture is what it is. I can't verify exactly how every maker works, so treat that as the general reputation rather than a guarantee for every shop.

## How people eat it here

There are two common ways, and both are worth trying.

**Cold (*zaru* style):** In warmer months, most people I know eat Himi udon cold — drained, served on a bamboo tray, dipped into a soy-based sauce. This is where the chewiness shows off best. The cold water tightens the noodle and you really feel the spring. If you only try it one way, try it cold.

**Hot in broth (*kake* style):** In winter it's served in a hot broth. Because this is a coastal area with a strong fishing tradition, you'll often see it paired with seafood or a fish-forward broth rather than a heavy meat one. It's lighter than you might expect.

A local habit worth mentioning: people here pay attention to the season. Toyama Bay is known for cold-water fish, and udon shops near the water often lean into whatever's fresh. Don't be surprised if a place recommends a topping you didn't plan on.

## Where to eat it

There are specialist shops in the Himi area, and you'll also find dried Himi udon in local supermarkets and souvenir shops across Toyama — the dried version is genuinely good and easy to carry home. If you're actually visiting, a sit-down shop is the better experience, because freshly boiled hand-stretched noodles have a texture the dried packs can't fully match.

I'm deliberately not naming specific shops or streets here. Places open, close, and change hours, and I'd rather you check a current map or ask locally than trust a name I can't keep up to date. Himi is a small enough city that finding an udon shop is not hard once you're there.

Practical note: Himi is reachable by train and by car from central Toyama. It's a modest fishing town, not a big tourist hub, so plan meals around normal lunch and dinner hours — smaller shops sometimes close between services.

## Fact-check note

- ✅ Himi udon is a hand-stretched (*tenobe*) noodle associated with the Himi area of Toyama. This is well established locally.
- ✅ It's commonly eaten both cold with a dipping sauce and hot in broth, and the cold style is popular in warm months. (First-hand, from living here.)
- ✅ Dried Himi udon is widely sold in Toyama supermarkets and souvenir shops.
- ⚠️ The exact degree of hand-stretching versus machine work varies by maker — I can't verify every shop's method, so treat "hand-stretched" as the general tradition.
- ⚠️ Comparisons to Inaniwa and Sanuki (thinner than Sanuki, chewier than Inaniwa) are my own taste impressions. Texture is subjective; yours may differ.
- ⚠️ Any claim about Himi udon's origin or history I've left out on purpose, because accounts differ and I couldn't confirm them firsthand. If a shop tells you a founding story, take it as their story.
