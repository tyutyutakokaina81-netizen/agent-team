# Himi Winter Yellowtail (Kanburi): The Season Locals Wait For

If you search around for "Himi buri" in winter, you'll find a lot of glossy photos and a lot of superlatives. I live in Takaoka, next door to Himi on the Toyama Bay coast, and I want to give you something plainer: what the season actually feels like on the ground, and when it's worth timing a trip around.

*Buri* is Japanese yellowtail. *Kanburi* — "cold buri" — is the fatty, cold-season version that fishermen and cooks around here get genuinely excited about. The short version: it's a real thing, it's seasonal, and the window is narrower than most travel pages let on.

## Why winter, and why here

Buri swim down the coast as the water cools, and by deep winter the fish caught in Toyama Bay carry a thick layer of fat. That fat is the whole point. Warm-season yellowtail is fine; cold-season yellowtail is a different eating experience — richer, softer, with a clean finish rather than a heavy one.

Himi has a local certification called *Himi Kanburi* (氷見寒ぶり) that only applies when the catch and conditions meet the local fishing cooperative's criteria for the season. When those conditions aren't met in a given period, the branded label simply isn't declared — so "Himi kanburi" is not something on offer every single winter day. It's worth knowing that before you build a whole trip around one word.

## The window is short — and not fixed

Here's the honest part. The genuinely fatty run lands over roughly a few weeks in the depth of winter, broadly across December to January in a normal year. But the exact timing shifts year to year with water temperature and weather, and a stretch of rough seas can shut the boats down for days at a time. Some winters are generous; some are thin.

So if you're flying in specifically for kanburi, treat it as a bet, not a booking. Aim for the heart of winter, keep your dates a little flexible if you can, and check with your lodging or a local source close to your trip rather than trusting a fixed "best week."

## How locals actually eat it

A few forms you'll run into:

- **Sashimi / buri no shabu (buri-shabu):** thin slices swished briefly in hot broth so the fat just softens. This is the one people around here talk about most.
- **Buri no shioyaki / teriyaki:** salt-grilled or glazed. Homier, very good on a cold night.
- **Buri daikon:** simmered with daikon radish. A winter home dish more than a restaurant showpiece, but comforting.

You don't need a famous restaurant. A solid local set meal, or a fillet from a market taken back to where you're staying, gets you most of the way there.

## Practical notes for a visit

Himi is reachable by train and bus from the Takaoka area, and Takaoka connects to the wider Hokuriku rail network. Winter here is grey and can be genuinely snowy — pack for cold, wet weather, and don't assume every small place keeps long hours off-season. If a specific restaurant or market matters to you, confirm its winter schedule directly before you go.

## Fact-check note

- ✅ Buri is yellowtail; *kanburi* refers to the fatty cold-season fish, and Toyama Bay winter catches are prized for that fat. Confident.
- ✅ *Himi Kanburi* is a locally administered seasonal designation, not an every-day guarantee. Confident on the concept.
- ⚠️ The exact declaration criteria and the precise dates of any given season vary and are set locally — check current-year sources. Requires verification.
- ⚠️ "Roughly December–January" is a general pattern, not a promise; rough seas can interrupt the catch. Requires verification for your specific dates.
