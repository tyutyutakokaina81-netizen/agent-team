# Murodo: The High Point of the Alpine Route

Murodo sits at about 2,450 meters, the highest stop you can reach by public transport on the Tateyama Kurobe Alpine Route in Toyama. I live down in the city, and I go up a few times each season — for snow walls in spring, alpine flowers in summer, and cold clear air in autumn. It is a straightforward place to visit, but the altitude changes how you should plan. Here is what I have learned by going, not from a brochure.

## What the altitude actually feels like

At 2,450 meters the air holds roughly a quarter to a third less oxygen than at sea level. Most healthy people feel nothing worse than being a little short of breath on the stairs and slopes. But some visitors — and it is hard to predict who — get a mild headache, light nausea, or tiredness within an hour or two of arriving. This is ordinary altitude sickness, and it is more likely if you sleep poorly the night before, arrive dehydrated, or rush uphill the moment you step off the bus.

My practical advice: walk slowly for the first thirty minutes. Drink water before you feel thirsty. If a headache starts, sit down, rest, and do not push higher. The single most reliable cure is going back down, and the transport system makes that easy — the next bus or cablecar takes you to lower, thicker air. If you have heart or lung conditions, or you are travelling with small children or elderly relatives, it is worth asking a doctor before the trip. I am a resident, not a medical professional, so treat that as a prompt to check rather than advice.

## The temperature gap

This is the thing overseas visitors underestimate most. Murodo runs far colder than the cities below. As a rough rule, expect it to be well over ten degrees Celsius cooler than Toyama city on the same day, and wind makes it feel colder still. In spring, when the snow walls are the draw, people arrive in light jackets and regret it — there is still deep snow underfoot into May. Even in midsummer, an overcast, windy afternoon up there can feel like early spring. Bring a warm layer and a windproof shell regardless of the season, and wear shoes that handle wet or snowy ground. Weather also shifts fast; a clear arrival can turn to cloud and cold within the hour.

## How long to stay

For most visitors, two to three hours at Murodo is a comfortable amount. That covers the main viewpoints, an easy loop walk near the terminal, a hot drink, and time to simply take in the scale of the place. If you want a longer hike toward the ponds or higher trails, budget more and start early, because the last downhill transport of the day is fixed and you do not want to chase it.

Two timing notes. First, the route is one long chain of buses, cablecars and a trolley, so your real day is longer than the time at the top — check the last-departure times for your direction before you commit to a late lunch. Second, the season is short: the route is closed in deep winter and typically operates roughly from mid-April into November, with exact dates set each year. Confirm current dates and any weather closures before you travel.

## Fact-check note

- ✅ Murodo is around 2,450 m and is the highest point reachable by the Alpine Route's public transport.
- ✅ It is meaningfully colder than the cities below, and snow remains into spring.
- ✅ Altitude sickness at this height is usually mild and eased by descending.
- ⚠️ Exact opening and closing dates for the route change every year — verify before your trip.
- ⚠️ The "over ten degrees cooler" figure is a general rule of thumb, not a guaranteed reading for any given day; check the day's forecast.
- ⚠️ Whether a specific person feels altitude effects varies a lot; medical questions should go to a doctor, not to me.
