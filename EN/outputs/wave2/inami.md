# Inami: The Woodcarving Town Near Gokayama

I live in Takaoka, and when friends visit from abroad and ask for a half-day that isn't a big city, I often point them toward Inami, a woodcarving town in Nanto City, Toyama. It pairs naturally with the Gokayama gassho-zukuri villages, so you can do both in one outing. This is what I'd actually tell you over coffee — not a brochure.

## Why woodcarving, and why here

Inami's identity is carving, specifically the ornamental work you see above sliding doors in old Japanese houses — the pierced wooden panels called *ranma*. Walk the town's old main street and you notice the trade is still alive: workshops open to the street, the smell of wood shavings, and carvers actually working, not just posing for tourists. What surprised me the first time is how the craft spills into ordinary things. Shop signs, bus-stop details, even small figures tucked by doorways are carved rather than printed. It's a town where the everyday surface is wood.

The tradition is tied to the local temple. As I understand it, carpenters and carvers who worked on rebuilding the temple passed their skills down, and the woodcarving trade grew from there. The exact dates and details I'd flag as "please confirm" — I know the story broadly as a resident, not as a historian.

## What it's actually like to visit

It's small and walkable. You park once and go on foot. The pull of the town is the sloped old street that runs up toward the temple grounds; that's where most of the workshops cluster. Give yourself an hour or two to wander slowly, look into a couple of workshops, and watch someone work if they're open to it. Some studios sell smaller carved pieces, so if you want a souvenir that isn't mass-produced, this is a reasonable place for it — prices for real hand-carved work are not cheap, and that's fair.

Don't come expecting a theme park. It's a working town with quiet stretches, a temple, some cafes and craft shops, and not a huge amount of English signage. That plainness is the appeal, but manage your expectations: a couple of hours is honest, not a full day on its own.

## Pairing it with Gokayama

Here's why I bundle the two. Gokayama, also in Nanto City, has the steep-roofed thatched farmhouses (*gassho-zukuri*) that are registered as a UNESCO World Heritage site together with Shirakawa-go. Gokayama is the quieter, less-crowded side of that pairing, which is exactly why I like sending people there. Inami gives you the living-craft street; Gokayama gives you the historic mountain village. Together they make a satisfying day that feels like the inland side of Toyama rather than the coast.

A practical note: the two are not next door to each other, and you'll want a car or to check bus timing carefully, since rural connections are limited. I'd confirm current schedules before you go rather than trust anything I say about exact times.

## When to go

Any season works, but the mountain roads toward Gokayama get real winter snow, so if you're driving in winter, take that seriously. Spring and autumn are the easiest and, honestly, the most pleasant for walking Inami's street.

## Fact-check note

- ✅ Inami (in Nanto City, Toyama) is known for woodcarving, including ornamental *ranma* panels. I see this firsthand.
- ✅ Gokayama's gassho-zukuri villages are a UNESCO World Heritage site, registered together with Shirakawa-go (1995 to my knowledge).
- ⚠️ The origin story linking Inami's carving trade to temple reconstruction is something I know in broad strokes; exact dates and names — please confirm with the temple or town sources. 諸説あります。
- ⚠️ Workshop opening hours, whether carvers are working on a given day, and bus/car connections between Inami and Gokayama all change — verify before your trip.
