# Tokyo to Toyama by Shinkansen: Times, Cost, Tips

I live in Takaoka, on the Toyama side of the Hokuriku Shinkansen line, and I take this route to Tokyo a few times a year. Here is how the trip actually works, based on what I see when I ride it — not the glossy version.

## How long it takes

The Hokuriku Shinkansen runs between Tokyo Station and Toyama Station. In my experience the fastest trains cover it in a little over two hours; slower services that stop at more stations run closer to two and a half hours. So budget somewhere around 2 to 2.5 hours depending on which service you catch. If you are heading to Takaoka rather than Toyama city, note that not every train stops there, and it is one stop further along, so check the specific service before you book.

There are different service names on this line with different stopping patterns. The number of stops is what changes your total time more than anything else, so if speed matters, look for the service with the fewest stops to Toyama.

## What it costs

A one-way reserved seat in ordinary (non-Green) class is, from what I have paid, roughly in the ¥12,000–¥14,000 range. Prices shift with the season and the exact fare rules, so treat that as a ballpark, not a quote — please confirm the current fare when you book. Green Car (first class) costs noticeably more; Gran Class more still. For most visitors ordinary class is perfectly comfortable.

If you already hold a Japan Rail Pass or a regional pass that covers this line, the calculation changes entirely — check whether your pass includes the Hokuriku Shinkansen before paying separately.

## Reserved vs. non-reserved seats

You can buy a reserved seat (指定席, *shitei-seki*) or ride in the non-reserved cars (自由席, *jiyū-seki*). On ordinary weekdays I have found non-reserved fine. But around long holidays and weekends the trains fill up, and I have stood in the aisle before because I did not reserve. If you are travelling with luggage or as a group, or during a busy period, I would reserve. You can reserve at station ticket machines, at the green-window counter, or online — the online options for foreign cards have improved, though I would not promise every card works, so have a backup.

If you bring large suitcases, some cars have an oversized-baggage area that you reserve in advance. Whether this is required or just recommended has changed over time, so check the current rule when booking rather than trusting an old blog.

## The Mount Fuji question

A lot of visitors ask if you see Mt. Fuji on this line. Honest answer: no. The Hokuriku Shinkansen runs north and west through Nagano and the mountains, not the Tokaido corridor past Fuji. If seeing Fuji from the train is your goal, that is a different line. What you do get on this route is the Japan Alps — on a clear day the mountain views out the window are genuinely good, and coming into Toyama you get a sense of the wide plain between the mountains and the sea. I think that is a fair trade, but I want you to book with the right expectation.

## Small practical notes

Toyama Station connects to the local tram and the line toward Takaoka, so onward travel is straightforward once you arrive. Buy an ekiben (station bento) at Tokyo before you board if you want lunch on the train — the selection at the departure end is much larger. Bring a light layer; the cars are air-conditioned and the mountain section can feel cool even in summer.

## Fact-check note

- ✅ The Hokuriku Shinkansen connects Tokyo and Toyama; the trip is roughly 2–2.5 hours depending on stops. (Consistent with my own repeated trips.)
- ✅ Mt. Fuji is not visible from this line — it runs through Nagano, not past Fuji.
- ⚠️ Exact fares (~¥12,000–¥14,000 one-way reserved, ordinary class) shift with season and fare rules — confirm at booking.
- ⚠️ Fastest/slowest service times vary by stopping pattern and timetable changes — check the specific train.
- ⚠️ Oversized-baggage reservation rules have changed over time — verify the current policy before you travel.
