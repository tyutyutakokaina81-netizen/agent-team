# Genge and Toyama's Weird Deep-Sea Fish

If you look at a plate of genge for the first time, you might hesitate. It's a soft, pale, slightly gelatinous fish that doesn't photograph well and doesn't try to. Around Toyama Bay, though, nobody treats it as an oddity. It shows up in home kitchens and casual set meals the way any cheap, useful fish does. I've lived in Takaoka for years, and to me the interesting thing about genge isn't that it's a deep-sea creature — it's that it's ordinary here.

## What genge actually is

Genge is the local name for certain eelpout-type fish that live on the deep bottom of the bay. Toyama Bay drops off steeply not far from shore, so boats can bring up genuinely deep-water fish without going far out. That geography is the whole reason these fish reach local tables at all. Genge itself is not expensive and not glamorous; it's the kind of fish an older relative might buy without thinking twice.

The texture is the surprise. Cooked, it's soft and a little slippery, closer to something between fish and jelly than to a firm white fillet. People who grew up with it barely register that — it's just how genge is.

## How people eat it

The most common ways I see are simple. Dried genge (a flat, almost translucent dried fish) gets grilled quickly and eaten as a snack, often with a drink. Fresh genge more often goes into soup — a light broth or miso base where the soft flesh and the gelatin do their thing. It's a warming, unfussy dish, more everyday comfort food than special occasion.

You won't usually find genge as the centerpiece of an expensive meal. It's a supporting fish, the kind that fills out a home dinner or a modest set meal. That's genuinely part of its character here, not a marketing angle.

## The other deep-sea fish

Genge is one of several deep-bottom fish that turn up around the bay. Toyama is probably better known abroad for firefly squid and for white shrimp, both of which have a real seasonal following. Alongside those, various less-photogenic deep fish appear at markets and in local cooking depending on the catch. I'd rather not overstate how "famous" any single one is — the honest picture is a bay where deep and shallow fish both land close to home, and locals cook whatever's around.

If you want to actually try genge as a traveler, the realistic path is a local-style eatery or an izakaya near the coast rather than a big-name restaurant. Ask; it's cheap enough that staff won't blink. It may or may not be on the menu on any given day, since it depends on what came in.

## Why it feels normal here

The short version: the bay is deep, the fish is cheap, and people have eaten it for a long time without ceremony. Calling genge a "delicacy" oversells it in one direction; calling it "weird" oversells it in the other. It's closer to the truth to say it's a local everyday fish that happens to come from deep water. That's the part outside visitors tend to miss, and it's the part I find worth telling.

## Fact-check note

- ✅ Toyama Bay is unusually deep close to shore, which lets deep-water fish reach local markets — this is well established locally and widely repeated.
- ✅ Genge (an eelpout-type fish) is eaten locally as dried snacks and in soups, and is generally an inexpensive, everyday fish rather than a luxury item.
- ⚠️ Exact species names, biology, and depth figures I'm not certain of — I'm writing from what I see on tables and at markets, not from a formal source. Verify specifics if they matter to you (諸説あります).
- ⚠️ Availability is seasonal and inconsistent; whether any given eatery serves genge on a given day is not something I can promise.
