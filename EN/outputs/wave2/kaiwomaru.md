# Kaiwomaru Sailing Ship Park: A Quiet Harbor Half-Day

I live in Takaoka, and when friends visit from overseas they usually want the big-name stops first. But if you have a spare morning and you like harbors more than crowds, I send them one bay over, to the new port area of Imizu, where a four-masted sailing ship named Kaiwomaru sits at anchor. It is calm, it is walkable, and on a clear day you get the Tateyama mountains lined up behind the water. Here is how I'd spend half a day there.

## The ship itself

Kaiwomaru is a sail training vessel, retired and now kept as a floating exhibit in the harbor park. She's a tall ship with a lot of rigging, and even from the dock she photographs well against the sky. Depending on the day and the season there are times when the crew and volunteers set the sails for display — the ship is sometimes called "the lady of the sea" for how she looks with the canvas open. I can't promise a sail-setting on your specific date, so if that's the reason you're coming, it's worth checking the schedule in advance (see the note below).

You can walk the deck when the ship is open to visitors. Inside there are cabins, the wheel, and displays about life aboard a training ship. It's a modest, hands-on kind of visit rather than a large museum, which is part of why it stays quiet.

## The park around it

The ship sits inside an open harbor park, and honestly the grounds are half the appeal. There's grass, benches, and wide paths along the water, so it works for a slow stroll or a packed lunch. Families with small kids come here on weekends because there's room to run around and the sightlines are open.

The reason I bring visitors specifically is the view across the water: on a clear cold morning the Tateyama range stands up sharp behind the harbor, and having a tall ship in the foreground makes it one of the more distinctive scenes in the prefecture. Haze is common, though, especially in summer, so a crisp autumn or winter morning gives you much better odds than a hot afternoon.

## Getting there and timing

The new port area is a short hop from central Takaoka. Most overseas visitors take the local tram-and-train line that runs out toward the coast, then walk; a taxi or rental car is simpler if you're carrying much. I'd budget roughly two to four hours total: the ship, a loop of the park, and time to just sit by the water.

Mornings are best. The light is better for the mountains, and the harbor is at its emptiest before mid-day. If you want food, the port district nearby is known for its catch, so pairing the ship with a seafood lunch is an easy plan.

## Fact-check note

- ✅ Kaiwomaru is a retired sail training ship kept in the harbor park in the Imizu new-port area, near Takaoka.
- ✅ The park is open, walkable, and free to enter; the ship's deck/interior visit is a smaller-scale, hands-on experience.
- ⚠️ Sail-setting displays, ship opening hours, and any entry fee for boarding vary by day and season — confirm the current schedule before you go rather than trusting a fixed date.
- ⚠️ The Tateyama mountain view depends heavily on weather; it is often hazy, so a clear view is not guaranteed.
- ⚠️ Some people call the ship "the lady of the sea." I've heard this locally, but treat the nickname as informal rather than official (諸説あります).
