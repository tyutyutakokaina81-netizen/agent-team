# Iwase: A Restored Sake Port Worth a Slow Afternoon

Iwase is a quiet district on the northern edge of Toyama city, where the Jinzu River meets the Sea of Japan. In the Edo and Meiji periods it was a working port on the Kitamaebune trade route — the coastal shipping network that carried rice, salt, herring, and other goods between Hokkaido and Osaka. That trade money built the merchant houses that still line the main street today. It is not a theme park and not a crowded sightseeing hub. On a normal weekday you can walk the whole thing at a slow pace and pass only a handful of other people. That is the appeal.

I live in Takaoka, one bay over, and Iwase is the kind of place I bring visitors when they want a half-day that isn't rushed.

## Getting there and the general shape of the walk

Most people arrive by the local tram line that runs north from Toyama Station out toward the coast; the ride takes roughly twenty minutes and drops you within a short walk of the old street. From the stop, the historic quarter is essentially one long machiya (townhouse) street running toward the water. You don't need a map. Walk down it, look in the doorways that are open, walk back. Budget an hour if you're brisk, an afternoon if you stop for things.

The street is genuinely old-feeling but also lived-in. Some of the frontages are restored merchant houses open to the public; others are private homes, cafes, small craft shops, or glass studios. Toyama has a strong contemporary glass scene, and you'll see that reflected in a few of the shops here.

## The merchant houses

A couple of the large former shipping-merchant residences are open to visitors, and they're the reason to come. Inside you get the long, narrow machiya layout — street-facing shop room, deep interior, a rear garden or storehouse — plus the heavy beams and lattice fronts that signal old money from the shipping years. Displays cover the Kitamaebune trade and how families here made their fortunes. Hours and any admission fee vary by house and by season, so check on the day rather than trusting a fixed schedule. Some are free; some charge a modest entry.

## The sake breweries

Iwase has working sake breweries on or near the old street, and this is a real draw for the "brewery walk" idea. A brewery here has become fairly well known in and beyond Japan, and you can sometimes taste or buy on site. Two honest cautions. First, a working brewery is not a museum — tasting availability, opening hours, and whether tours run at all depend heavily on the day, the season, and how busy they are. Sake is not brewed year-round, so what you can see changes. Second, if you specifically want to visit a particular brewery or do a proper tasting, it's worth confirming in advance rather than assuming you can drop in. On a good day, standing in a centuries-old brewery street with a small glass of local sake is exactly the experience the search intent promises. On a bad day the doors are shut. Both happen.

## Practical notes

There are cafes and a few places to eat along or just off the street, but this is a small district, not a restaurant strip — options thin out in the late afternoon and some close early or on weekdays. The walk is flat and easy. It pairs naturally with the nearby waterfront if you want to extend the day toward the harbor. Spring and autumn are the most comfortable; summer near the coast can be hot and humid, and winter here is grey and can be wet.
