# Toyama vs Takayama: Do You Need Both?

I live in Takaoka, in Toyama Prefecture, and one of the most common questions I hear from overseas travelers is some version of: "I have a few days near the Japanese Alps — should I do Toyama, Takayama, or both?" It's a fair question, because on a map they look close and the names blur together. But they play different roles in a trip, and whether you need both depends on what you actually want to see.

Here's how I'd sort it out as someone who moves between these places.

## They're closer on the map than by travel time

Takayama sits in Gifu Prefecture; Toyama is the prefecture (and city) just to the north. By road they're roughly an hour and a half to two hours apart depending on the route and season — a scenic bus runs between them, and the drive crosses mountain country. So "close" is relative. You can connect them in a day, but it isn't a five-minute hop.

The bigger point is that they face in different directions. Takayama is inland, mountain-town Japan. Toyama opens onto the Sea of Japan coast. That single difference shapes almost everything below.

## What Takayama does well

Takayama is a well-preserved old town with a walkable historic center, morning markets, sake breweries, and a strong festival tradition. It's compact, atmospheric, and easy to enjoy on foot. It's also a natural gateway toward Shirakawa-go, the thatched-roof village area that many people pair with it.

Because it's popular and photogenic, Takayama draws crowds, especially in peak seasons. That's not a knock — it's earned — but it does mean the old streets can feel busy at midday.

## What Toyama does that Takayama can't

Toyama's role is coast, water, and access to high mountains. From the Toyama side you reach the Sea of Japan, glassy bay views, and — in the warmer months — the Tateyama Alpine Route, the high mountain crossing famous for its spring snow corridor. Toyama city itself is more of a modern, low-key base than a preserved old town: trams, a redesigned canal-side park, seafood, and easy train connections.

If your mental image of this trip includes seafood, sea views, or the alpine route, that's Toyama's territory, not Takayama's.

## So — one, or both?

My honest take:

- **If your priority is old-town atmosphere and Shirakawa-go**, Takayama alone can carry a couple of days. Toyama becomes optional.
- **If you want coast, seafood, and the high-mountain route**, Toyama is the anchor and Takayama is the optional add-on.
- **If you want the contrast** — inland mountain town *and* the Sea of Japan coast — doing both makes sense, and the bus route between them is part of the appeal.

Where I'd caution against "both" is a rushed schedule. Squeezing both into a single day means mostly transit and a quick look. Two nights, splitting them, is where the pairing actually pays off.

One more practical note: the two don't duplicate much. This isn't a case of "see one, you've seen the other." Their overlap is small, which is exactly why some people do both — and why others happily pick one.

## Fact-check note

- ✅ Takayama is in Gifu Prefecture; Toyama is a separate prefecture to the north. Confirmed.
- ✅ Toyama faces the Sea of Japan; Takayama is inland. Confirmed from living here.
- ⚠️ Travel time between them (roughly 1.5–2 hours by road) varies by route, bus schedule, and season — check current timetables before relying on it.
- ⚠️ The Tateyama Alpine Route is seasonal and typically closed in deep winter. Opening dates shift year to year — verify the current season's dates.
- ⚠️ Crowd levels in Takayama depend heavily on season and festival timing. My impression is midday-heavy in peak periods, but this is a general observation, not a measured figure.
