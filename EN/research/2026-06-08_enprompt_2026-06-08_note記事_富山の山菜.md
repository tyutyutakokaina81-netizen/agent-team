Living in Toyama, spring announces itself through sansai — wild
mountain vegetables. Fukinoto (butterbur bud), koshiabura, taranome
(angelica shoot), zenmai, warabi, kogomi. As soon as locally-foraged
sansai appears in the supermarket vegetable corner, it's officially
spring.

Sansai is something you buy, but also something you receive.
Neighbors and relatives bring bags of what they foraged that morning.
"I picked too much, here" — handed over in a thin plastic bag, still
smelling faintly of forest floor.

The defining note of sansai is gentle bitterness. As a child, it was
just bitter. As an adult, the bitterness has become the taste of
spring itself — a palate-level reset after winter's heavy yellowtail
stews and pickles.

Seasonal foods aren't just about flavor preferences. They're devices
that switch your body's gear from winter to spring.

A meal of sansai is also a quiet proof: that the mountain is close,
and that people who walk into it still live nearby. That continuity,
not the dish itself, is what living in Toyama keeps offering.

■ 山菜の苦み、というやさしさ

山菜の特徴は、独特のほろ苦さ。

子どもの頃は、苦くて好きじゃなかった。
大人になると、その苦みが、春という季節そのものの味になっているような気がしてくる。

冬の重い味（寒ブリ、煮物、汁物）の後に、山菜の苦みを食べると、
体が「春モード」に切り替わる。

季節の食べものは、味の好みより、体のリズムを切り替える装置なのかもしれない。

■ 山菜文化の前提

山菜が当たり前にある食卓は、山が近いことの証拠でもある。

富山は、立山連峰がすぐ近くにある県だ。

これは、都市部では失われた食文化に近いと思う。
派手じゃないけれど、北陸に住んでいるからこその、季節の特権だ。
