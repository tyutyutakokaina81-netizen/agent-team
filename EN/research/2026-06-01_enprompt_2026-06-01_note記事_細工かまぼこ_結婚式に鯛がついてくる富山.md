When you attend a wedding in Toyama Prefecture, the gift bag handed to
each guest at the end contains something unusual: a large, pink-and-red
"sea bream" — not real fish, but a sea bream made entirely of kamaboko
(steamed fish cake), 30 to 50 cm long.

This wedding favor exists almost only in Toyama and parts of Ishikawa.
Sea bream ("tai" 鯛) puns with "medetai" (auspicious), so it's the
default symbol for any celebration: weddings, new houses, childbirths.

You don't eat it at the reception. You bring it home, cut it ceremonially
("tai-giri"), and distribute slices to neighbors and family.
A single decorated fish reaches households who weren't even at the wedding.

It's not high craft. It's not luxury food. But for people in Toyama,
this kamaboko fish *is* what "wedding gift" means. Whenever I open the
gift bag and see the pink tai, I think: yes, this is home.

Regional customs persist not because they're spectacular,
but because everyone keeps doing them.
