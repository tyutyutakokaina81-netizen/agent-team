In Toyama Bay, the spring sea sometimes glows blue at night.

Hotaru-ika — "firefly squid." A small squid (5–7 cm) that emits blue
light when disturbed. From March to May, they swim into shallow waters
to spawn, and along certain Toyama coastlines, the water's edge flickers
softly blue in the dark.

What's odd to outsiders: in Toyama, hotaru-ika is both a tourist
spectacle and an ordinary supermarket item. Boiled in soy-vinegar paste,
sold in trays for a few hundred yen, eaten with dinner. The same animal
that visitors travel hours to see at midnight is on someone's plate
the same evening.

The reason it concentrates in Toyama Bay: the same steep underwater
canyon that makes white shrimp accessible also brings firefly squid
within easy reach of the shoreline.

For me, late May means the trays start disappearing from the supermarket
aisle — and that, more than any calendar, is when spring is over.

■ なぜ、富山湾なのか

ホタルイカは日本海全体に分布しているが、産卵のために岸近くまで来るのは、
富山湾の常願寺川河口から滑川あたりが、特にまとまっている。

理由は富山湾の地形だ。
急峻な海底谷が岸近くまで届いているため、深海性のホタルイカが
浅瀬に上がるのに「ちょっとした移動」で済む。

白えびと同じ構造で、富山湾の地形が、世界的にも独特な漁の風景を作っている。
