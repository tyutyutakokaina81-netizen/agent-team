### Page 2 — Why Solo in Japan (Read First)
Japan is one of the more comfortable countries to explore alone: reliable transit, clear signage on major lines, and a strong lost-and-found culture. Solo travel still means you are your own backup, so a little planning goes a long way.

**Solo-friendly habits:**
- Share your rough itinerary with someone back home.
- Keep one offline copy of bookings, maps, and key addresses.
- Build in slower days — solo travel is more tiring than it looks.
- Trust your instincts; it's fine to leave a place that feels off.

**Solo realities to plan for:**
- Some restaurants and ryokan favor pairs/groups — book ahead and confirm solo bookings.
- Carrying your own bags everywhere; pack light.
- No one to watch your stuff — be mindful in cafes and stations.

### Page 7 — Eating Alone, Comfortably
Dining solo in Japan is easy and common — counter seating is everywhere.

**Solo-friendly dining styles:**
- Counter seats at ramen, sushi, and izakaya — made for one.
- Ticket-machine shops: buy a meal ticket, hand it over, no chatting needed.
- Convenience stores and depachika (food halls) for low-pressure meals.
- Standing noodle bars for a quick solo bite.
- Set meals (teishoku) for balanced single portions.

**Tips for solo diners:**
- Smaller places may be cash-only — carry some cash.
- Some izakaya have a small seating/table charge; confirm.
- A translation app helps with no-English menus; pointing is fine.
- Reservations help at popular spots; ask if they seat solo guests.
