### A Counter Seat at a Family Seafood Diner on Toyama Bay

On the western side of Toyama Prefecture, facing the Sea of Japan, there's a kind of restaurant that doesn't make it into most guidebooks: the small family seafood diner. No reservations, no English website, often no printed menu — just a counter, a handful of seats, and whatever the local boats landed that morning.

This is a portrait of one such place. Think of it as a stand-in for dozens of similar diners scattered along this coast.

**Why the fish here is different**

Toyama Bay is unusually deep — it drops steeply just offshore — which means cold-water fish live close to the harbors. For a diner, that translates into something simple but rare: the distance from boat to plate can be a matter of hours, not days.

One local specialty is *shiroebi*, a tiny translucent shrimp sometimes called the "jewel of the bay." It's so delicate that it doesn't ship well, so outside this region you'll rarely see it fresh. At a place like this, it might appear as sashimi over rice, or lightly fried into fritters. The same ingredient, served two ways, can taste like two different dishes.

**What a meal here actually looks like**

You sit at the counter. There may be a chalkboard listing the day's catch in Japanese — if you can't read it, the owner will usually point and gesture his way through the options. A typical lunch is a *teishoku*, a set meal: a main of fish (grilled, simmered, or raw), a bowl of rice, miso soup, and a few small side dishes. It's everyday food, the kind local fishermen and office workers eat, not a tasting course built for tourists.

Prices at diners like this tend to be modest by big-city standards — this is home cooking with a license, not fine dining.

**A note for visitors**

A few things worth knowing before you go:

- **Cash is safer than cards.** Many small rural diners still run on cash. Bring some.
- **Lunch hours can be short.** These places often open around late morning and close once the day's fish runs out. Earlier is safer.
- **A little Japanese helps, but isn't required.** "Osusume wa?" ("What do you recommend?") will almost always get you the best thing on the board.
- **Sit at the counter if you can.** It's the front-row seat to watch the food being made — and the easiest place to fall into a wordless conversation with the owner.

**Why it's worth the detour**

Toyama isn't on the standard Tokyo–Kyoto–Osaka route, and that's exactly the point. The west side of the prefecture — around the cities of Takaoka, Himi, and Toyama — is the kind of place where you can eat exceptional seafood without queuing, without a reservation, and without paying a premium for the privilege.

A counter seat at a family diner won't show up on a "top 10" list. But years later, it's often the meal people remember best: the one where they couldn't read the menu, trusted the owner, and got something they'd never tasted before.

このサンプルは、**「英語発信代行をお願いすると、実際に何が手元に届くのか」を1セットで体感してもらう**ためのデモです。口頭の説明や料金表だけでは、地方の小規模事業者にはサービスの中身がイメージしづらい。そこで、架空の海鮮食堂1軒を題材に、実際の納品物と同じ形式で「投稿・記事・編集翻訳」を作り込みました。

- **① SNS投稿3本**＝「日々の発信はこういう質感で続きます」という運用イメージ。

- **③ ビフォー/アフター**＝「ただの翻訳ではなく、伝わる英語にする編集力がある」という差別化の証明。

### 営業での使い方

1. **初回ヒアリングの後に添付する**のが基本。「御社向けに、まず1〜2本を無償で試作します」という流れの呼び水として、まずこの汎用サンプルを見せる。
