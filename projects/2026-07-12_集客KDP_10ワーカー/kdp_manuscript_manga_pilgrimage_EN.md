# Two Friends, Two Towns, One Coast

Here is a piece of trivia that surprises even lifelong manga readers: two of the most beloved names in the history of Japanese comics came from the same quiet stretch of the Sea of Japan coast, and grew up just a short train ride apart.

The pen name "Fujiko Fujio" belonged to two people — two childhood friends from Toyama who worked together for decades before eventually going their separate creative ways.

**Fujiko F. Fujio** (real name Hiroshi Fujimoto) was the creator most associated with *Doraemon*, the round blue robot cat from the future. He was born in **Takaoka**, a working craft city on the coast.

His partner, **Fujiko Fujio Ⓐ** (real name Motoo Abiko), created *Ninja Hattori-kun*, *Kaibutsu-kun* ("Monster Kid"), and the darker, more adult *Warau Salesman* ("The Laughing Salesman"). He was from **Himi**, a fishing town just up the coast from Takaoka.

The two met as schoolboys in Toyama, shared a love of drawing, moved to Tokyo together as young men, and changed manga forever. For a huge slice of the world that grew up on these stories, tracing their roots means visiting two real, lived-in towns on the same easy rail line — not a theme park, but the actual places the stories came from.

This book is a guide to that pilgrimage.

## Who this book is for

If any of the following is true, this trip is for you:

- You grew up watching *Doraemon* or *Ninja Hattori-kun* and want to stand where their creators did.
- You've already seen Tokyo and Kyoto and want a quieter, more local side of Japan.
- You like the idea of a "pilgrimage" that is really just two charming coastal towns, some excellent seafood, and one of Japan's great sea-and-mountain views.

## Who should skip it

I'll be honest, because honesty is the whole point of this book. If you want a big, polished character theme park with rides and photo sets — this isn't that. Neither town has one. The appeal here is understatement: gentle nods woven into ordinary streets, a modest gallery by the sea, and the quiet feeling of being somewhere real. If that sounds thin to you, this trip may disappoint. If it sounds wonderful, read on.

# Who Were the Two Fujikos?

To understand why these two towns matter, it helps to know the story of the men behind the name.

## A boyhood friendship in Toyama

Hiroshi Fujimoto (Fujiko F. Fujio) and Motoo Abiko (Fujiko Fujio Ⓐ) met as schoolboys in Toyama. They bonded over drawing, filled notebooks with comics, and dreamed of making manga for a living at a time when that was barely a career. Fujimoto came from Takaoka; Abiko from nearby Himi. The coast they grew up on — the bay, the boats, the mountains across the water — is still there today, and it's the through-line of this whole trip.

## Tokiwa-so and the move to Tokyo

As young men, the pair moved to Tokyo to pursue manga. They famously lived for a time in an apartment building called **Tokiwa-so**, which became legendary as the home of a whole generation of future manga masters. The names associated with that building — including Osamu Tezuka's circle and artists like Fujio Akatsuka and Shotaro Ishinomori — read like a hall of fame of postwar Japanese comics. It was a poor, cramped, hopeful place where young artists shared ideas and encouragement. You can't visit the original today, but as you'll see later, one of the towns in this book recreates a room from it — and standing there is one of the quiet highlights of the pilgrimage.

## The duo, and the split

For much of their careers the two worked under the shared "Fujiko Fujio" name. In later years they separated their work, and the two pen names we use today — Fujiko **F.** Fujio and Fujiko Fujio **Ⓐ** — reflect that. Roughly speaking, the "F." works (Doraemon and other bright, kind-hearted, often science-fiction children's stories) came from Fujimoto of Takaoka, and the "Ⓐ" works (Hattori-kun, Kaibutsu-kun, and the sharper adult satire of The Laughing Salesman) came from Abiko of Himi.

You don't need to memorize any of this to enjoy the trip. But knowing that two real friends from this coast built so much of what the world thinks of as "manga" gives the towns a weight they'd otherwise hide.

# Why This Pilgrimage Is Different

Plenty of places in Japan lean hard into their pop-culture connections. This coast doesn't, and that's the point.

## No theme park — and that's the appeal

Neither Takaoka nor Himi has turned its famous son into a giant commercial attraction. What you get instead is a working craft city and a working fishing port, each with gentle, characterful touches woven in: a wrapped tram, some public statues, a modest gallery, a themed clock, a manga-lined shopping street. For a certain kind of fan, that understatement is exactly what makes it moving. You're not visiting a set built for tourists; you're visiting the place a story actually came from, and the town is getting on with its ordinary life around you.

## Real towns, honestly told

I live here, so let me set expectations plainly. On a grey, wet afternoon, with the tram not running your way and a museum closed for the day, either town can feel like just another quiet Japanese city. The magic isn't automatic. It comes from knowing what you're looking at, catching the towns on a decent day, and pairing the character-hunt with the food and scenery that make this coast special anyway. Get those right and it's a day you'll remember; get unlucky with weather and timing and it's a pleasant, low-key outing. Either way, no hype — just the truth.

## A short, walkable, low-cost trip

This is not an expensive pilgrimage. The two towns sit on the same rail line a short ride apart, much of the fun is free (statues, streetscapes, riding a tram), and the one paid indoor attraction is modestly priced. You can do the core of it in a single day. That accessibility is part of why it's worth recommending.

# Takaoka: Doraemon's Hometown

Takaoka is a craft city on the Sea of Japan, and the birthplace of Fujiko F. Fujio, the creator most associated with Doraemon. The city wears the connection lightly.

## Doraemon touches around town

You won't find a mega-attraction. What you'll find are gentle, joyful details tucked into everyday streets:

- **A themed tram.** A colourfully decorated tram themed around the characters runs in the area. Riding it is a small pilgrimage in itself — an ordinary local tram that happens to be a rolling tribute. Routes and schedules change, so check current local information if catching it is your goal.
- **Public art and character statues.** You'll come across the characters as statues and small civic touches in public spaces around the city, including near the station area. Half the fun is simply spotting them as you walk.
- **Small themed details.** Keep an eye out for character motifs in minor civic details around the centre. They're easy to miss and lovely to catch.

Because exhibits, routes and displays can change over time, treat the above as the general shape of things rather than a fixed checklist, and confirm anything specific before a special trip.

## A city worth more than a character hunt

Takaoka rewards travelers who linger. It has been casting bronze for around 400 years, and its historic metal-casters' quarter — a district of lattice-fronted old houses — is still a living craft area, not a museum piece. The city is also home to a notable **Great Buddha** cast in its signature bronze, and a calm, well-proportioned Zen temple worth a quiet half-hour. You can fold all of this into the same day as your Doraemon stroll; see the chapter on making a full day of it.

# Himi: Hattori-kun's Hometown

A short ride up the coast from Takaoka lies Himi, a working fishing port on Toyama Bay and the hometown of Fujiko Fujio Ⓐ — the creator of Ninja Hattori-kun, Kaibutsu-kun and The Laughing Salesman.

## Manga touches woven through a fishing town

Himi celebrates its famous son gently, and the celebration is spread through the town rather than boxed into one building:

- **A manga-themed shopping street.** Himi's main shopping street is lined with a "Ninja Hattori-kun Road," where you'll find the characters as bronze figures and public art. These outdoor statues are permanent public installations, and simply walking the street spotting them is free and fun.
- **A character karakuri clock.** The town has a mechanical "karakuri" clock that performs with the manga's characters at set times — a small, delightful stop if you time it right. Performance times can change, so check locally.
- **A gallery by the sea.** At the heart of it all is a modest gallery devoted to Fujiko Fujio Ⓐ's world, covered in its own chapter next.

Exact locations, displays and clock times can change, so confirm current local information if a particular spot is the reason for your visit.

## Why the town itself is worth it

Even setting the manga aside, Himi is a genuinely rewarding place to be. It's a real fishing port — early-morning auctions, weathered boats, small family-run inns — not a tourist set-piece. And on a clear winter morning, from the Himi coast you can watch the snow-capped Tateyama mountains appear to rise straight out of the calm bay, one of the most striking views in Japan. Pairing a Hattori-kun walk with that scenery, and with some of the best-value seafood on the coast, is what turns a character-hunt into a real day out.

# Inside the Gallery by the Sea

The centrepiece of Himi's "Fujiko Fujio Ⓐ Manga World" is a modest, thoughtfully arranged gallery near the water. It is not a flashy theme park — it's a quiet building where the artist's work is presented with care. Here's what you can generally expect, with the standard caveat that displays change.

## The ground floor: art and Tokiwa-so

The main floor typically shows original and reproduction art from Abiko's best-known works, including pieces that depict Himi itself — a small thrill for anyone who then walks out into the same landscape. The floor that tends to move people most, though, is a **recreation of Room 14 of Tokiwa-so**, the legendary Tokyo apartment where a generation of young manga artists once lived and dreamed. Standing in front of that small, spare desk, you get a faint sense of the ambition of a group of penniless young men who went on to shape an entire art form. There's usually a small shop here too, a natural place to pick up a souvenir.

## Upstairs: a manga library and photo corners

The upper floor generally has a lighter, more playful mood. Expect a **manga library** where you can freely read a large collection of the artist's volumes — the kind of place where children and adults alike end up sitting on the floor, losing track of time. There are often modern touches too, such as a digital manga screen that families enjoy, and a corner where you can pose alongside the characters for a photo. It's unpretentious and warm, which suits the town.

## A practical note

Opening hours, closed days and admission fees are modest but do change, and exhibits rotate. If the gallery is the main reason you're making the trip, check the official site for the latest before you go. It is the kind of place best enjoyed unhurried, so leave yourself time.

# Beyond Hattori-kun: The Wider World of Fujiko Fujio Ⓐ

Many overseas fans know Abiko only through Ninja Hattori-kun. But part of what makes the Himi side of this pilgrimage interesting is how much range the man had.

## Not only children's comics

Alongside the cheerful little ninja, Fujiko Fujio Ⓐ created *Kaibutsu-kun*, a friendly-monster adventure that was also widely adapted, and — very differently — *The Laughing Salesman* (Warau Salesman), a darker, satirical work aimed at adults. Where the Doraemon side of the duo leaned bright and kind-hearted, Abiko's range stretched into shadow and irony. That breadth is one reason his work still draws grown-up fans as well as nostalgic ones, and it gives the Himi gallery a slightly more grown-up flavour than you might expect from a "cartoon town."

## Why it deepens the trip

You don't need to be a scholar of his work to enjoy Himi. But knowing that the town produced an artist with this range — from a beloved children's ninja to a bleak adult satire — makes the modest gallery feel less like a mascot's souvenir stand and more like the home ground of a serious, versatile storyteller. It's a good reminder that this pilgrimage is about creators, not just characters.

# The Global Fanbase: Why Hattori-kun Travels

One of the quiet pleasures of this trip, if you're visiting from abroad, is realizing how far the little ninja has traveled — and how few of his fans know where he's from.

## A cartoon that crossed borders

Ninja Hattori-kun has been broadcast well beyond Japan, and in parts of Asia an entire generation grew up on it. In India in particular, the cheerful ninja has been one of the most familiar cartoon characters on television for years; there are also devoted fans across Southeast Asia. For many of them, the show is pure childhood — and the idea that its creator came from a small, real fishing town on the Sea of Japan comes as a genuine surprise.

## An honest word on "popularity"

I want to be careful here, because it's easy to overstate. Exactly how popular the series is, and among which generations, varies a lot by country and by decade, and I'm not going to pretend to precise figures I can't verify. What I can say plainly is that Hattori-kun's overseas following is real and, in some countries, large — and that makes Himi a meaningful "other pilgrimage" for fans who never imagined they could visit the character's hometown. If you're one of those fans, this book is partly an invitation: the town is quiet, uncommercial, and genuinely pleased to see travelers who bothered to come.

# Make a Full Day (or Two) of It

The best way to enjoy this pilgrimage is to let the characters be the reason you come and the coast be the reason you stay. Both towns are wonderful on their own terms.

## In and around Takaoka

Beyond the Doraemon touches, give Takaoka time for its historic metal-casters' quarter, its Great Buddha, and its quiet Zen temple. The city has been a bronze-craft centre for around four centuries, and that living craft tradition is a large part of its character. It's an easy, walkable core.

## In and around Himi

Beyond the Hattori-kun street and gallery, Himi is about the sea. Time a clear winter morning for the mountains-over-the-bay view, eat extremely well (see below), and simply wander a real working port. A short way down the same coast, the **Amaharashi** shoreline offers the same famous composition — snow peaks apparently rising from the water, with a lone pine on an offshore rock — that has drawn Japanese poets and painters for centuries.

## What to eat

You're on Toyama Bay, one of Japan's richest fishing grounds, so eat with the season:

- **Winter:** cold-season yellowtail (*kanburi* / *buri*), rich and fatty, served as sashimi or in a hot-pot; and snow crab. This is the headline season.
- **Other seasons:** white fish, squid, and the delicate tiny white shrimp (*shiroebi*) known as the "jewel of the bay"; in spring, firefly squid.
- **A local noodle:** Himi is also known for its hand-stretched *tenobe* udon — chewy and springy, especially good served cold in warmer months.

Even modest counters here serve fish that would cost far more in a big city. It's one of the honest joys of the whole coast.

# Getting There & When to Go

This is one of the more accessible "off the beaten path" trips in Japan, which is a big part of why it's worth recommending.

## How to get there

Both towns sit on the same coast and connect by an easy local rail line:

- **Takaoka** is roughly 2.5–3 hours from Tokyo via the Hokuriku Shinkansen (to the Shin-Takaoka stop, then a short local ride), or about 15–40 minutes from **Kanazawa** by train.
- **Himi** is a short ride on from Takaoka on the local JR Himi Line. The same line passes the Amaharashi coast.

If you're already visiting Kanazawa or riding the Tateyama–Kurobe Alpine Route, this pilgrimage slots in without backtracking. Timetables and fares are set by the operators and change, so check the latest before you travel.

## When to go

- **Winter (roughly December–February):** the headline season — cold yellowtail, crab, and the clearest mountains-over-the-sea mornings. Also the coldest and least predictable weather. Early morning is best for the view.
- **Spring:** firefly squid, cherry blossoms, and gentler weather.
- **Green months:** quiet, easy and uncrowded.

The famous mountain-over-sea view depends entirely on weather and visibility and is never guaranteed; come with a flexible morning if that's your priority. The manga sites are enjoyable year-round, weather permitting.

## Where to stay

You can do the core pilgrimage as a day trip, but staying over lets you pair a relaxed character-hunt with an early-morning trip to the coast for the view. Options include Takaoka itself, a seaside inn toward Himi (known for winter crab-and-yellowtail dinners and bay views), or Toyama City as a larger, well-connected base. Book well ahead in crab season and during the Alpine Route season, when the good rooms go fast.

# A Suggested Itinerary

Here's a practical way to string it together. Adjust freely — this is a planning guide, not a rulebook.

## The core one-day pilgrimage

1. **Morning — Takaoka.** Start with the Doraemon touches: keep an eye out for the character statues and, if the schedule suits, ride the themed tram. Fold in the historic metal-casters' quarter and the Great Buddha, both a short walk from the centre.
2. **Midday — ride the coast.** Take the local line toward Himi. On a clear day, the stretch along the water past Amaharashi is beautiful; consider a short stop for the view.
3. **Afternoon — Himi.** Walk the Ninja Hattori-kun street spotting the statues, time the karakuri clock if you can, and visit the gallery by the sea (check hours in advance). Leave time to simply wander the port.
4. **Meal — seafood.** Eat with the season. In winter, that means yellowtail; year-round, the bay's white fish and shrimp are excellent and far kinder on the wallet than big-city prices.

## The relaxed two-day version

- **Day 1:** Takaoka at an unhurried pace — Doraemon touches, craft quarter, Great Buddha, Zen temple — then move to a seaside inn toward Himi for a crab-or-yellowtail dinner.
- **Day 2:** Wake early for the mountains-over-the-bay view from the coast, then spend the morning on the Hattori-kun street and at the gallery before heading on.

Either way, let the two creators be your excuse and the coast be your reward.
