# Eating Around Toyama Bay: A Local's Guide to the Food, Off the Crowds

> English Kindle short (manuscript #1). Written for overseas independent travelers who want to
> eat well around Toyama Bay without standing in lines. A resident's notebook, not a glossy guide.
> Every fact is restated from existing Japanese and English articles in this project — no new
> reporting. Structure: `# title` / `## chapter` so it converts cleanly with `EN/build_epub.py`.
>
> Ground rules kept throughout: I name cities, stations, ports and markets, but never street
> addresses, neighborhoods, or specific shops (so nothing here goes stale or plays favorites).

## Before you start: why this coast, and how to use this book

I live in Takaoka, a small city on the Sea of Japan side of central Japan, about twenty minutes
by local train from Toyama. Visitors keep asking me the same two questions: *what do I actually
eat here, and how do I do it without queuing for an hour?* This short book is my answer.

Most travel writing about this region points you at the famous restaurants, which then fill up
with everyone who read the same article. My approach is the opposite. The food here is good because
of where it comes from, not because of any one counter, so the trick is to understand the ingredient,
the season, and the ordinary places locals actually buy it — the morning market, the fishmonger's
case, the supermarket shelf. Do that and the crowds mostly stop mattering.

One piece of geography explains almost everything on the table here. Toyama Bay drops away steeply
very close to shore — a deep underwater canyon reaches to within a few kilometers of land. Cold,
deep water sits near the harbors, so deep-water species that elsewhere mean a long trip offshore
come up on day boats. That single fact is why the shrimp is translucent-fresh, why a premium fish
turns up in an ordinary case, and why so much of the local diet is built around the sea.

**How the book is arranged.** Each chapter takes one food, tells you what it is and why it exists
here, then gives a short practical block — season, how to eat it, where to look, whether it travels,
and one tip. The last chapter pulls it together into a plan for an uncrowded food trip. Read it in
order or jump to whatever is in season when you arrive.

**A word on locations.** I don't give addresses or shop names. I use the names of stations, ports,
markets and public facilities — the kind of general words you can type into a map app and the kind
that don't change when a shop closes. Where a detail (a price, a weight standard, a shipping option)
shifts with the year or the vendor, I'll tell you to check on the spot. Prices I mention are rough
and change; treat them as scale, not quotes.

**A word on seasons.** More than most of Japan, eating here is seasonal. The shrimp, the squid, the
crab, the winter yellowtail — each has its months, and turning up in the wrong season doesn't mean a
bad trip, only a different plate. The calendar at the end is there so you can aim.

## Shiraebi: the translucent shrimp you should eat where it lands

For me shiraebi was never a delicacy. It was just what appeared in the shops when the weather turned.
It took growing up and looking again to realize that people elsewhere treat a single bowl of it as a
once-in-a-lifetime thing, while here it's a weeknight side.

Shiraebi is a small deep-water shrimp, roughly six to eight centimeters, near-colorless while alive
and taking on a faint pink only in the light. Locals have long called it "the jewel of the bay," and
for once a nickname like that reads closer to plain description than marketing. It lives a few hundred
meters down — the Japanese sources say around three hundred meters or more — which usually means
fishing far offshore. Here the seabed drops within sight of the harbor, so the boats return the same
day. Commercial harvest of this shrimp on any scale happens in relatively few places; I'll leave it at
that rather than make a bigger claim I can't check for you.

The classic way to eat it is *shiraebi-don*: peeled shrimp packed over a small bowl of rice, often a
hundred to two hundred of them, each one hand-peeled. One mouthful is a clean, delicate sweetness that
seems to melt. It's also served lightly deep-fried (whole, shell and all) or salt-grilled, and there's
a crisp shrimp senbei cracker sold as a souvenir that is genuinely hard to stop eating.

The main landing areas are around Toyama city and the neighboring port district of Imizu — roughly
half an hour by train from Takaoka, the same Toyama Bay bounty, just a little further along the coast.
In season, fishmongers and even supermarkets in the coastal cities sell fried and grilled packs; sushi
places and rice-bowl specialists serve the fresh bowl.

**Practical**
- **Season:** Roughly spring through autumn; an everyday item in those months.
- **What it is:** A small, near-translucent deep-water shrimp; delicate, clean sweetness.
- **How to eat it:** The rice bowl (*shiraebi-don*) for the pure version; lightly fried or grilled for something more casual; the cracker as a souvenir.
- **Where to look:** Coastal-city markets and supermarkets, sushi counters, rice-bowl specialists.
- **Does it travel?** Peeled shrimp is delicate and labor-intensive, so it's pricey; whether frozen or fried packs ship internationally varies — check locally.
- **Tip:** Eat it where it lands. Freshness is most of the experience and it fades fast once the shrimp leaves the coast, so this is a plate to prioritize on your first day, not carry home.

## Hotaruika: the squid that is both dinner and a night sight

In spring the bay does something strange: after dark, parts of the water's edge glow faint blue. The
cause is hotaruika, the firefly squid — a small squid, about five to seven centimeters, whose body
gives off a pale blue light when disturbed. From roughly March through May, great numbers rise toward
the shallows to spawn, and along certain stretches of the coast you can see the shoreline flicker blue
at night.

What I find quietly interesting is that the same creature is, at the same time, an everyday food. In
season the supermarkets stack packs of boiled firefly squid; the standard way to eat it is dressed
with *sumiso*, a sweet vinegar-miso, for a few hundred yen a pack. It's an ordinary supper side. Yet
that same animal is what visitors drive to the coast at midnight to watch. Food and spectacle, one
species — living here you stop noticing it, but it really is an unusual arrangement.

The squid is found across the Sea of Japan, but it gathers to spawn near shore particularly along the
stretch from the Jōganji River mouth toward the city of Namerikawa. The reason is the same steep
underwater canyon: a deep-water squid has only a short way to travel up to the shallows here. The
firefly-squid boats and the boiled packs both come out of that geography.

If you're aiming for the night viewing, note that it depends on season, weather, moon and luck; it is
not a nightly guaranteed show, and organized early-morning viewing boats run in the peak weeks. Details
and dates vary year to year — check locally before you build a night around it.

**Practical**
- **Season:** Roughly March–May; peak varies by year.
- **What it is:** A small bioluminescent squid; tender, briny-sweet when boiled.
- **How to eat it:** Boiled and dressed with sweet vinegar-miso (*sumiso*) is the everyday classic; also served as *okizuke* (soy-marinated) and grilled.
- **Where to look:** In-season supermarkets and fishmongers for packs; izakaya and sushi counters for prepared versions.
- **Does it travel?** As a fresh food, best eaten in the region; processed versions vary — check locally.
- **Tip:** You can enjoy the food any spring day without chasing the glow. If you do want the night sight, treat it as a bonus that may or may not happen, not a fixed itinerary item.

## Nodoguro: the premium fish you buy whole and grill yourself

Walk into a fish shop here and one fish usually sits apart in the case, at a price tier of its own:
small body, red skin, black only inside the mouth. Locally it's called nodoguro; its formal name is
akamutsu, blackthroat seaperch.

It's a lean white fish that somehow carries the fat of a rich one — people sometimes call it "the toro
of white fish." It lives fairly deep, roughly two to four hundred meters down, and the catch is uneven,
which keeps the price up. In my family it isn't a weekly fish. It shows up two or three times a year —
a birthday, a midsummer gathering, the year's end — and someone announces it before it reaches the
table: "tonight is nodoguro." I've come to think luxury is set more by how rarely a thing appears than
by how much of it there is.

Sashimi is good, but the household favorite is salt-grilled: salt the skin, sear it hard over high heat
so the fat underneath renders and sizzles out. The flesh is mild, yet the melting fat gives it a
richness you don't expect from a white fish. It also takes well to simmering, sushi, and shabu-shabu.

Here's the practical point for a traveler, and the reason it's in this book: ordering nodoguro by the
piece in a big-city restaurant is expensive, but if you're already on the coast, buying one whole fish
from a market and having it grilled — or grilling it where you're staying, if you have a kitchen — is
far gentler on the wallet for the same fish. It rewards being here rather than reading about it.

**Practical**
- **Season:** Blackthroat seaperch is generally said to be richest in autumn–winter; how that maps exactly to this bay varies — check locally.
- **What it is:** A small, fatty white fish from deeper water; uneven catch, premium price.
- **How to eat it:** Salt-grilled is the local favorite; also sashimi, simmered, sushi, shabu-shabu.
- **Where to look:** The glass case at fishmongers and sushi counters in the prefecture — usually the single fish priced well above its neighbors. Bought whole locally.
- **Does it travel?** Premium-fish mail order may carry it, at a price — check locally.
- **Tip:** If you have any kind of kitchen at your lodging, buying it whole and grilling it yourself is the value move. Ask the fishmonger to gut and scale it for you.

## Buri and buri-okoshi: winter yellowtail, and the thunder that announces it

"There was thunder last night" — when that comes up at the breakfast table in early winter, my family
tends to brighten a little. Living here, the first thunder of the season isn't just rough weather; it's
news from the sea.

From late autumn into early winter, the Sea of Japan coast gets days of low, ground-shaking thunder.
In this region it has a name: *buri-okoshi*, the "yellowtail rouser." The storms land just as schools
of buri — winter yellowtail — migrate south, so people came to read the thunder as a sign the fish are
on their way. It's folklore more than cause and effect, but the sound and the season really do seem to
belong together once you've lived through a few winters of it.

After the thunder, the port of Himi comes alive. Fat yellowtail fill the fixed nets set in Toyama Bay,
and the larger fish landed at Himi port, if they meet a local standard, are branded *Himi kanburi*.
Each year, around late November, a local council announces that the branded season has opened, and
that's when Himi's winter really begins. The exact weight standard and the timing of the declaration
are set year to year by the council — some recent years have used a threshold around seven kilograms —
so treat any specific number as "check locally."

Honestly, kanburi is expensive; even residents don't buy a whole fish every year. And it's at the mercy
of the sea — some winters the catch is poor, the declaration comes late, and the price climbs. But you
don't need a whole fish. A single fatty slice, or a bowl of *buri-daikon* (yellowtail simmered with
daikon radish), carries the whole season. Where thunder in winter might sound ominous somewhere else,
here it's the sound that wakes the fish.

**Practical**
- **Season:** Roughly late November through winter; the branded declaration is typically around late November but shifts yearly — check locally.
- **What it is:** Fat winter yellowtail from Toyama Bay's fixed-net fishery; the top branded grade lands at Himi.
- **How to eat it:** Sashimi when you can splurge; *buri-daikon* or a grilled slice for an affordable, deeply seasonal plate.
- **Where to look:** Himi and the coastal cities in winter — markets, fishmongers, and set-menu restaurants.
- **Does it travel?** Fresh yellowtail is a same-region food; check locally for shipped or processed options.
- **Tip:** Don't fixate on the branded whole fish. The simmered dish delivers the flavor at a fraction of the price, and it's on ordinary winter menus.

## Himi dried fish: the breakfast most travelers overlook

Himi is a port town toward the west of the prefecture, near the base of the Noto Peninsula, and it's
famous for yellowtail. But the thing I quietly love most about Himi isn't the buri — it's the *himono*,
the dried fish.

At home, frying fish in the morning is a nuisance: the smell lingers, the washing-up piles up. Knowing
all that, Himi dried fish is the one thing I'll still cook at breakfast, for the simple reason that it's
good. Horse mackerel (aji), barracuda (kamasu), young yellowtail (fukuragi) — the salting is judged to
the species. The flesh is firm, yet the fat melts when you bite in. With rice and miso soup, one grilled
dried fish is more than enough of a breakfast.

Why is the dried fish here worth the trouble? It comes down to how the fish is caught. Himi port runs
mainly on *teichi-ami*, fixed-net fishing: nets are set and the migrating fish swim into them, so the
catch can be landed with little damage, close to fresh. Dried fish is really just fish, salt and wind,
and the shorter the distance from landing to salting, the better the result. That short distance is the
whole quality argument.

For a traveler this is one of the easiest, least crowded pleasures on the coast. Dried fish keeps and
packs, so it's the rare item on this list you can genuinely take with you or bring home, and buying it
is a calm market errand rather than a queue. A simple grilled-fish breakfast set at a market or a
lodging that serves one is, to me, a truer taste of Himi than anything you'd wait in line for.

**Practical**
- **Season:** Year-round, with the fish types shifting by season.
- **What it is:** Salt-dried local fish — aji, kamasu, fukuragi and others — from a fixed-net port.
- **How to eat it:** Grilled skin-side crisp, with rice and miso soup. A breakfast food above all.
- **Where to look:** Himi's market and the shops and eateries around the port; also sold packaged.
- **Does it travel?** Yes — this is the pack-and-carry item of the book. Ask about keeping time and whether it needs refrigeration.
- **Tip:** If your lodging offers a grilled-fish breakfast, take it. Otherwise buy a couple of pieces and grill them wherever you can.

## Toyama Black ramen: a working town's flavor, and how to approach it

When friends visit from outside the prefecture, I'll sometimes ask, "Want to try Toyama Black?" Almost
everyone reacts to the photo first — the soup is genuinely, startlingly dark. It's a ramen built on a
heavy pour of dark soy sauce, and the first mouthful makes most people wince: it's salty.

That saltiness is a piece of postwar life, preserved. The dish is generally traced to a workers' canteen
in Toyama city just after the war. Laborers at the port and on construction sites brought their own
plain white rice for lunch, and the black, heavily-soy soup was created as something to eat *with* that
rice — closer to a salty side broth than to a stand-alone bowl. In other words, Toyama Black was
designed as salt for people doing hard physical work, not as a delicate ramen. Read it that way and it
makes sense.

You can find Toyama Black-style shops in Takaoka too, though many here dial the color and salt back a
little from the intense versions near Toyama Station. As a resident I don't experience it as the
"shocking black bowl" a visitor does. But when I take out-of-town friends, they nearly all say the same
thing: "The first three mouthfuls are brutal. Then, halfway through, it hooks you." Human taste turns
out to tolerate a sudden hit of salt better than you'd think.

A practical note, since this book is about eating comfortably: this is a strong, salty bowl. Order rice
alongside — it's how the dish was meant to be eaten — go easy on any extra salt, and keep water handy.
Approach it as a cultural record you can taste rather than a light lunch, and you'll enjoy it more.

**Practical**
- **Season:** Year-round.
- **What it is:** A dark-soy ramen with a working-class origin; intentionally salty.
- **How to eat it:** With plain rice; pace yourself; don't add salt.
- **Where to look:** Shops around Toyama Station for the classic intensity; Takaoka for gentler versions.
- **Does it travel?** Instant and gift versions exist; intensity varies — check locally.
- **Tip:** Share a bowl if you're salt-sensitive, and treat it as a taste of history, not a full meal on its own.

## Masu-zushi: skip the single souvenir, do a tasting flight

At Toyama Station, near the gates, there's an unusually crowded row of masu-zushi shops. Masu-zushi is
pressed trout sushi: a round wooden box lined with bamboo leaf, cherry-pink trout laid over vinegared
rice, weighted into a disc. Usually a station has one or two famous makers of a local box lunch. Toyama
has several producers lined up side by side, each selling their own version.

And locals have opinions. Most people I know have a maker they're loyal to; my parents buy their
souvenir boxes from one particular producer without hesitation, while a relative swears by a different
one. When family gathers, whose maker's masu-zushi ends up on the table can become a small, good-natured
argument, because each side genuinely thinks theirs is better.

Here's the thing a visitor should know: the boxes look nearly identical — round wood, bamboo leaf,
pink trout, white rice — but they don't taste the same. Line up boxes from a few makers and eat them
one after another and the differences are obvious: how sharp the vinegar is, how firmly the trout is
cured, how the salt lands. What looks like one dish is really several.

So my advice is not to buy a single box as a souvenir and call it done. If you can, buy small sizes from
two or three producers at the station and eat them side by side back at your lodging. That comparison —
laying open the bamboo leaves and tasting maker against maker — is, I think, the most quietly luxurious
half hour of a Toyama trip, and it costs very little and requires no queue.

**Practical**
- **Season:** Year-round; sold as a box lunch (*ekiben*) and souvenir.
- **What it is:** Pressed trout sushi in a round wooden box; multiple local makers, each distinct.
- **How to eat it:** At room temperature; ideally a side-by-side tasting of several makers.
- **Where to look:** The masu-zushi row at Toyama Station; also department-store and souvenir counters.
- **Does it travel?** It's a keep-a-day box lunch, so short trips and same-day gifting are fine; check the date on the box.
- **Tip:** Buy small sizes from a few makers, not one big box. The comparison is the whole point.

## Kombu: why a coastal town loves kelp it doesn't harvest

Toyama — especially the prefectural capital — regularly ranks at or near the top of the country for
household spending on kombu (kelp), a standing that shows up in national household surveys of capital
and major cities. Takaoka isn't a separate survey city, but it shares the same kelp-heavy food culture.
And it's a genuinely odd thing: a place right on the sea consuming huge amounts of kelp that is
harvested far to the north, in Hokkaido. The explanation is a shipping route that stopped running
generations ago.

In the Edo and Meiji eras, the *kitamaebune* cargo ships carried Hokkaido kombu south down the Sea of
Japan coast toward Osaka. One of the relay ports was Fushiki, the port of Takaoka. A relay port doesn't
just pass goods along; it sells some into the local market. So this region always had good kombu on
hand — and "making something from what's around" is where a food culture begins. Here kombu became not
just a stock ingredient but a food in its own right: pressed into sashimi, wrapped around rice balls,
shaved into *tororo* kombu, folded into fish cakes.

The most striking use is *kombujime*, "kelp-cured" sashimi — a tradition shared with neighboring
Ishikawa. You lightly salt fresh white fish or squid, press it between sheets of kombu, and leave it a
night in the fridge. Kombu is rich in glutamate, the backbone of savory umami; the fish gives up some
moisture to the kelp and takes on the kelp's umami in return. The sashimi comes out denser, silkier,
far deeper in flavor, and it's superb with sake. The science is osmosis and umami synergy; the reaction
of anyone eating it for the first time is simpler — "this isn't sashimi anymore, it's a feast."

For a traveler, the kombu story is a quiet, crowd-free layer of the region. Walk into an ordinary
supermarket here and the kelp section is visibly deeper than elsewhere — shaved kombu, salted kombu,
stock kombu, cooking kombu, sometimes a whole aisle of it. It's not a photogenic attraction, but it's
one of the most real pieces of local food culture you can see, and it costs nothing to notice.

**Practical**
- **Season:** Year-round (kombujime is best with fresh in-season white fish).
- **What it is:** A deep local kelp culture — including kelp-cured sashimi (*kombujime*) — rooted in an old shipping route.
- **How to eat it:** Try kombujime at a sushi counter or izakaya; browse the supermarket kelp aisle for shaved and cooking kombu.
- **Where to look:** Sushi and izakaya menus for kombujime; any local supermarket for the range of kombu.
- **Does it travel?** Dried and packaged kombu products travel well and make good, light souvenirs.
- **Tip:** Order kombujime alongside plain sashimi so you can taste what the kelp actually does.

## Kaburazushi and the fermented winter foods

Come December, most fridges in Toyama hold a jar of *kabura-zushi*. Despite the name, it isn't
vinegared-rice sushi at all. It's closer to a pickle — a type of *nare-zushi*, or lactic-fermented
sushi. Thick rounds of salted turnip are slit open, stuffed with salted yellowtail (buri), and left to
ferment in rice *koji*. Bite in and three things arrive at once: the gentle sweetness of the koji, the
crisp turnip, and a soft fermented tang, with the richness of the fish dissolving through it. It's good
with rice, but I'd rather have it with sake.

Why winter? Snow country needed food that kept. The season of heavy snow is long here, and households
have always needed a winter store. Yellowtail, meanwhile, comes in unpredictable hauls — some days a
big catch, some days nothing. Ferment the catch in koji and it lasts, and it tastes better for the
waiting. It's a sensible piece of snow-country wisdom.

The custom traces back to the food culture of the old Kaga domain; the western part of Toyama once
belonged to Kaga, and the two share this table. As the year winds down, jars start appearing in shops,
and plenty of people around me feel it isn't really New Year without one. Every household seasons it a
little differently — how hard the koji is worked, how many days it ferments, which fish goes inside — so
there are as many New Year flavors as there are cooks. Some families use mackerel (saba) instead of
yellowtail, and fermenting times of roughly a week to ten days are one common range, though this all
varies by household — check locally if you want the exact method.

Kaburazushi belongs to a wider family of preserved foods that exist for the same reason: a long winter
and a sea whose surplus had to be made to last. If you're here in the cold months and curious about how
this coast actually fed itself, the fermented shelf is where to look — and, being a homely everyday
food, it comes with no line and no ceremony.

**Practical**
- **Season:** Winter, roughly December into the New Year.
- **What it is:** Koji-fermented salted turnip stuffed with salted fish; a *nare-zushi*, close to a pickle.
- **How to eat it:** In small slices, as is, with rice or sake. An acquired, fermented flavor — start with a little.
- **Where to look:** Shops and market stalls in winter; supermarket refrigerated sections.
- **Does it travel?** It's a refrigerated ferment; check keeping time and shipping locally.
- **Tip:** If the strong ferment isn't for you, a single slice alongside sake is the friendliest introduction.

## Beni-zuwai crab: the everyday feast

In winter, when people here say "crab," the first thing most of us picture is *beni-zuwai*, the red snow
crab. The prized premium crab — hon-zuwai, known in luxury form as matsuba or echizen crab — is landed
too, but the one we actually eat at home is the more affordable beni-zuwai. It's a feast, but not an
intimidating one, and that in-between distance is exactly right for a family table.

Beni-zuwai is red before you even cook it — a brighter red than hon-zuwai — and when it's laid out at
the market the color really does catch the eye. The meat is delicate and high in moisture, with a light,
clean sweetness rather than the dense heaviness of the premium crab. It's a different pleasure, not a
lesser one.

At home the method is simple: buy it already boiled, and eat it as is. No elaborate cooking — just snap
a leg, ease the white meat out, and dip it in a little crab vinegar, or eat it plain. The work of getting
the meat out of the shell becomes part of the ritual; a winter evening quietly organizes itself around
a plate of crab.

For a traveler this is a friendly way into the region's celebrated crab without the celebrated price. If
you have any kitchen at your lodging, or even just a table and some patience, a boiled beni-zuwai from a
winter market makes an unfussy, memorable meal. And because it's the everyday crab rather than the
trophy one, you're far less likely to be competing for it.

**Practical**
- **Season:** Winter; the fishery runs in the colder months (dates set by regulation and vary — check locally).
- **What it is:** Red snow crab — the affordable, everyday winter crab of the bay; delicate, sweet, moist.
- **How to eat it:** Bought boiled and eaten as is, with a little crab vinegar or plain.
- **Where to look:** Winter markets and fishmongers in the coastal cities.
- **Does it travel?** Boiled crab is best eaten locally; check shipping options at the vendor.
- **Tip:** For the trophy experience there's hon-zuwai; for a relaxed, affordable feast, beni-zuwai is the smarter order.

## Planning an uncrowded food trip around Toyama Bay

Here's how to turn the previous chapters into an actual trip — one built around eating well without
queues or a big-city budget.

**Base yourself small, and use the trains.** Toyama, Takaoka and Himi sit close together; local trains
link Toyama and Takaoka in about twenty minutes, and the coastal line runs from Takaoka toward Himi in
roughly half an hour. Because the prefecture is compact, you can eat at a market in the morning and be
walking a quiet temple courtyard by early afternoon without a long drive between them. A car gives you
more reach for scattered coastal spots but isn't required for the food in this book; weigh it against
train simplicity based on how far off the rail line you want to roam — that trade-off varies with your
plans, so decide once you know your route.

**Aim your visit at a season.** Firefly squid runs spring; shiraebi spans spring through autumn;
yellowtail, crab and the fermented foods are winter. Dried fish, masu-zushi, Toyama Black and kombu are
year-round anchors, so even an off-season trip has a full table. There's no wrong month, only a
different plate.

**Shop the way locals do.** The least crowded, most rewarding move is to buy at markets, fishmongers and
supermarkets and eat at your lodging: a masu-zushi tasting flight from several makers, a whole nodoguro
you have grilled, a boiled beni-zuwai you pick apart at the table, dried fish for breakfast. This skips
the restaurant queues, cuts the big-city markup, and — with premium items like nodoguro and yellowtail —
is dramatically cheaper for the same fish. Book lodging with even a small kitchen or a grilled-fish
breakfast and this whole approach opens up.

**Carry the right things home.** Most of this food is best eaten on the coast, but a few items travel:
dried fish, dried kombu products, senbei crackers, and same-day-gift box lunches. For anything fresh,
fermented or refrigerated, ask the vendor about keeping time and whether it can ship — the honest answer
is that it varies by product and season, so check on the spot rather than assuming.

**Keep expectations honest.** This is a fishing coast at the mercy of the sea. Some winters the
yellowtail is scarce and dear; some spring nights the squid doesn't glow; branded declarations and crab
seasons move year to year. That unpredictability is the point, not a flaw — it's why the food here still
tastes like it belongs to a place and a season rather than a supply chain. Come with a season in mind, a
few of these dishes on your list, and the willingness to eat what the bay actually gives you, and Toyama
will feed you very well, well away from the crowds.
