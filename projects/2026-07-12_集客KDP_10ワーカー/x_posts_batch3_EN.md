## 単発ポスト（20本）

### S1（大仏・逆張り豆知識）
> You know the Great Buddhas of Nara and Kamakura. Takaoka's bronze one, on the Sea of Japan side, is often counted as the "third" — cast by the town's own metal foundries. Fewer visitors, and last I checked no ticket gate.
> #Japan #Toyama

### S2（銅器鋳造・意外な統計）
> A large share of Japan's bronze temple bells and statues come from one small city: Takaoka, a metal-casting town for around 400 years. You can watch craftspeople pour molten bronze, then buy the tableware version.
> #Toyama #offthebeatenpath

### S3（禅寺国宝・静けさ）
> A Zen temple in Takaoka is a designated national treasure, and on a weekday you can walk its long wooden corridors nearly alone. No entry queue, no crowd noise — just old cedar and the sound of your own steps.
> #Japan #Toyama

### S4（ホタルイカ・自然現象）
> For a few weeks in spring, a bay in Toyama can glow blue at night — firefly squid rising to the surface in huge numbers. It's a natural event, so nothing's guaranteed, but when it happens the water looks lit from inside.
> #Japan #offthebeatenpath

### S5（蜃気楼・運任せ）
> Uozu, on Toyama Bay, is one of the few places in Japan where a "shinkiro" mirage sometimes appears — stretching ships and buildings along the horizon. It needs the right spring conditions, so it's luck, not a schedule.
> #Toyama #Japan

### S6（称名滝・日本一）
> Japan's tallest waterfall drops around 350m in the mountains of Toyama, and in early summer, snowmelt can bring a second fall alongside it. It's a bus-plus-walk to reach, which is part of why it stays quiet.
> #Japan #Toyama

### S7（富山ブラック・尖った食）
> Toyama's local ramen is jet-black — a soy broth so dark it stains the bowl, originally made salty for postwar laborers eating it with rice. It's not subtle and it's not for everyone. That's sort of the point.
> #JapaneseFood #Toyama

### S8（ますずし・駅弁）
> Before conveyor-belt sushi there was masuzushi: pressed trout on rice, sealed in bamboo leaf in a round wooden box. It's Toyama's old station lunch, and it travels well enough to be your bullet-train dinner.
> #JapaneseFood #Japan

### S9（昆布文化・意外な事実）
> Odd local fact: Toyama is among the top kelp-eating places in Japan, even though kelp doesn't grow here — it arrived by old trade ships. So you'll find kelp in the sushi, the sweets, even the tea.
> #Toyama #JapaneseFood

### S10（売薬・信用の歴史）
> For roughly 300 years Toyama sent traveling medicine sellers across Japan, leaving a box of remedies in each home and collecting only for what was used. That "trust first" model is still a point of local pride.
> #Japan #Toyama

### S11（黒部ダム・到達が旅）
> Kurobe Dam is Japan's tallest, set in a gorge so remote that reaching it is half the trip — cable car, trolley bus, tunnels. In summer the water release throws up spray you can often see a rainbow in.
> #Japan #offthebeatenpath

### S12（チューリップ・イメージ崩し）
> Not the Japan you're picturing: every spring a plain in Tonami turns into a grid of millions of tulips. It's a quiet, local kind of spectacle — a bit like the Netherlands, but with mountains behind it.
> #Japan #Toyama

### S13（合掌造り・世界遺産）
> UNESCO-listed thatched-roof farmhouses stand in the mountains of southern Toyama, some still lived in — and buried in snow each winter, lit up at night. Get there before the day-trip buses and the lanes are yours.
> #Japan #offthebeatenpath

### S14（運河の街・雨の日）
> Toyama city quietly rebuilt itself around trams and a canal-side park, with a coffee spot by the water locals are weirdly proud of. Not a bucket-list item — just a calm half-day if the coast gets rained out.
> #Toyama #Japan

### S15（古い港町・酒蔵）
> An old merchant-port quarter in Toyama city still has working sake breweries in wooden buildings from the shipping era. Taste a few, then walk to the sea in about ten minutes. Low-key, no crowd, real.
> #Japan #Toyama

### S16（写真キャプション・水鏡）
> Rice paddies flooded in early spring, holding a mirror image of 3,000m peaks still capped with snow. This is a roadside view in Toyama, not a famous photo spot. That's what I keep trying to explain.
> #Japan #Toyama

### S17（アクセス・近い）
> People assume Toyama is "far." It's about 2 hours from Tokyo by bullet train, direct. You can stand under 3,000m mountains that rise near the sea and still be back in the city for dinner.
> #JapanTravel #Toyama

### S18（正直告白・crowd-free）
> I live here, so honestly: Toyama has no single "must-see" that out-does Kyoto. What it has is space — mountains, sea and old craft towns in one day, with no line for any of it. Trade the icon for the quiet.
> #Toyama #offthebeatenpath

### S19（氷見うどん・安い半日）
> Himi, a fishing town, has its own hand-pulled udon — thin, springy, made in shops that have done it for generations. Pair it with a morning at the fish market and you've got a cheap, local half-day.
> #JapaneseFood #Japan

### S20（黒部峡谷トロッコ・季節）
> An open-sided little train winds along the Kurobe Gorge on tracks first laid for dam workers. In autumn the walls turn red and gold and you're riding right through it. Seasonal — it closes once the snow comes.
> #Japan #offthebeatenpath

## 短スレッド案（10本 / 各3〜5ツイート・「フック→価値→最後に1導線」）

### T1（三大仏の三つ目）
1/ Everyone photographs the Great Buddhas of Nara and Kamakura. Almost nobody talks about the "third" — and it might have the best backstory. 🧵
2/ It sits in Takaoka, a small city on the Sea of Japan side that has cast metal for around 400 years. So when the town wanted a Great Buddha, its own foundries made it — in bronze.
3/ It burned down more than once over the centuries; the current bronze one took the town decades to finish. Community-built, not commissioned from afar.
4/ Last I checked there was no ticket gate and rarely a crowd. You walk right up. For a "big three" anything in Japan, that's unusual.
5/ Full story + how to reach it in a day from Kanazawa or Toyama city: (link: en-takaoka-daibutsu.html) #Japan #Toyama

### T2（混まない一日）
1/ A full day in Japan with no queues and no "be there by 7am for the shot." Here's the crowd-free version, built around one small city on the Sea of Japan. 🧵
2/ Morning: a fishing-town fish market and hand-pulled udon in Himi. Boats come in early, so the fish is same-day.
3/ Midday: a national-treasure Zen temple and a 400-year-old copper-casting quarter in Takaoka. Watch bronze get poured; buy something small that'll outlive you.
4/ Evening: back to the coast for sushi off Toyama Bay — no two-hour wait, because nobody's fighting to get in.
5/ It's not a highlight reel, it's a real day. Full route + trains: (link: en-quiet-day-toyama.html) #Toyama #offthebeatenpath

### T3（アニメ聖地の正しい歩き方）
1/ If you grew up on Doraemon or Ninja Hattori-kun, there's a version of the "pilgrimage" with no queues and no giant theme park. It's just the towns the creators came from. 🧵
2/ Doraemon's creator was from Takaoka, a metal-casting town. You'll find character statues in a park and, if timing's right, a themed tram.
3/ The co-creator's Ninja Hattori characters line a small river through Himi, a fishing town one train away. Sea air, bronze figures, no gift-shop crush.
4/ Neither is a "destination park." They're quiet, real places — which is exactly what a lot of the fans who message me are after.
5/ Map + how to link both in one day: (link: en-anime-toyama.html) #Doraemon #Japan

### T4（富山湾の食ツアー）
1/ Toyama Bay is deep and cold, which does strange, good things to the seafood. A quick tour of what to eat — and why it's hard to get elsewhere. 🧵
2/ White shrimp: translucent, sweet, barely eaten fresh outside this bay because it's too fragile to ship. A specialty by geography, not marketing.
3/ Cold-season yellowtail: treated like an event here. Ports sit next to the towns, so the sushi is same-day and you're often not queuing.
4/ On land: pressed trout sushi in a bamboo-wrapped box (the old train lunch), and a jet-black soy ramen that is absolutely not subtle.
5/ Where and when for each: (link: en-toyama-food.html) #JapaneseFood #Toyama

### T5（雪の壁までの現実的な行き方）
1/ The photos of people walking between walls of snow taller than a bus are real. Getting there is the part nobody explains. Honest logistics. 🧵
2/ It's the Tateyama plateau at about 2,450m, reached by a chain of cable cars, buses and a ropeway — not one train, and no rail pass covers the whole crossing.
3/ The snow corridor is a spring thing (roughly mid-April into June). The walls shrink as summer comes, so timing matters more than luck.
4/ Cheapest move if you only want the walls: go up from the Toyama side and back down the same way, rather than crossing the full route.
5/ Full 2026 fares + timing: (link: en-snow-wall-guide.html) #JapanTravel #Toyama

### T6（海と3000m級の地理）
1/ In Toyama you can stand near a beach and look up at 3,000m mountains still holding snow. Almost nowhere in Japan puts sea and high alps in one frame. Here's why. 🧵
2/ The bay is unusually deep — it drops off fast just offshore. The mountains rise just as fast inland. So the horizontal distance from sea to summit is small.
3/ That same steep drop is why the seafood is cold-water, and why the "mountains floating on the sea" view at dawn is even possible.
4/ Practical upshot: coast in the morning, mountains in the afternoon, without a long transfer between them.
5/ Where to stand for the sea-and-peaks view: (link: en-sea-and-mountains.html) #Japan #Toyama

### T7（季節ごとの祭り）
1/ Japan's famous festivals come with famous crowds. Toyama's are still mostly local. A few worth planning around, by season. 🧵
2/ Spring: a plain in Tonami turns into a grid of millions of tulips. Not the Japan on postcards, but real and quiet.
3/ Summer: a float festival in Takaoka rolls out ornate wooden floats that are themselves designated cultural property.
4/ Autumn: a mountain-town dance where people move slowly through lantern-lit lanes to a haunting fiddle. Photogenic, and not overrun.
5/ Rough dates so you can plan around them: (link: en-toyama-festivals.html) #Japan #offthebeatenpath

### T8（工芸の県）
1/ Japan's craft scene isn't only Kyoto. One prefecture on the Sea of Japan side quietly makes metal, glass and thatched houses you can actually visit. 🧵
2/ Takaoka has cast bronze for around 400 years — a large share of Japan's temple bells and statues trace back here. You can watch a pour and buy the tableware version.
3/ Toyama city went all-in on glass art, with a museum inside a Kengo Kuma building. A good rainy-day move.
4/ In the southern mountains, UNESCO-listed thatched farmhouses still stand — some lived in, buried in snow each winter.
5/ How to string them into one route: (link: en-toyama-crafts.html) #Japan #Toyama

### T9（売薬の歴史）
1/ One of Japan's odder business stories comes from Toyama, and it's basically 300 years of "pay only for what you use." 🧵
2/ From the 1600s, Toyama sent traveling medicine sellers across the country. They left a box of remedies in your home and came back later to charge only for what you'd taken.
3/ Trust was the whole model — and the sellers doubled as news carriers between distant regions. A pre-modern network built on not cheating people.
4/ You can still see the paper wrappers, the boxes, and the little toys they gave kids, in a museum in Toyama city.
5/ The full story: (link: en-toyama-medicine.html) #Japan #Toyama

### T10（予算の現実）
1/ "Japan's expensive" is half a myth, and Toyama is where I'd try to prove it. A rough, honest day-cost breakdown. 🧵
2/ Sushi off the bay costs a fraction of a big-city famous counter — same-day local fish, no premium for the name.
3/ Temples and craft quarters here are often free or a few hundred yen to enter, usually with no timed tickets.
4/ The one thing that isn't cheap is the alpine route — that's a five-figure-yen day, worth knowing before you go.
5/ Sample budget for a day: (link: en-toyama-budget.html) #JapanTravel #Toyama

## 事実検証ノート（✅確実 / ⚠️要確認 / 諸説あり）
- ✅ 称名滝＝落差約350mで日本一の滝。増水期に隣接して別の滝（ハンノキ滝）が現れる → 表現は「a second fall alongside」に留め断定回避。
- ✅ 黒部ダム＝日本一高いダム（アーチ式・堤高約186m）。夏季に観光放水。「rainbow」は条件次第 → "can often see" 表現。
- ✅ 蜃気楼＝Uozu（魚津）が国内有数の観測地。春に発生 → "sometimes / luck" と明記。
- ✅ ホタルイカ＝富山湾の春の名物。身投げ/発光は自然現象で保証なし → "nothing's guaranteed" 明記。
- ✅ 富山＝昆布消費が国内トップ級。産地でないのに消費多い（北前船由来）→ "among the top" に留める。
- ✅ 売薬＝先用後利（使った分だけ後払い）、約300年の歴史 → 事実。
- ✅ ますずし・富山ブラック・氷見うどん・砺波チューリップ・南砺の合掌造り（世界遺産）・東京-富山 新幹線約2時間 → いずれも一般的に確認可能な事実。
