# Free Paper — Everyday Cooking in Toyama (Home & Local Dishes), English Edition

## A note from the editors

Toyama is known abroad for the sea — the winter yellowtail, the firefly squid, the small translucent shrimp of the bay. But the food that actually carries a household here is quieter than any of that. It is the pressed trout box opened on a slow weekend morning, the coiled summer noodles a relative mails over in July, the daikon that sits in nearly every winter pot, the simmered dish whose name is really a pun about cooking order.

This issue gathers around fifteen of those dishes and drinks, told from a resident's side rather than a guidebook's. We've kept to a city level when we say where to find things, marked anything we couldn't confirm with a small `[to verify]`, and tried to write so you'd enjoy reading even if you never make the trip — and find it useful if you do. Where you want to go deeper on a single dish, a QR code points to the longer piece online.

## 1. Masu-zushi (pressed trout sushi)

To most people from outside the prefecture, masu-zushi is the famous pressed-trout lunch box sold at Toyama Station. Live here, though, and it slides out of the "souvenir" category entirely — it becomes one more option for breakfast or lunch. Pink trout, cured with salt and vinegar, is laid over vinegared rice and pressed flat inside a round wooden frame lined with a single bamboo leaf.

At heart it's a preserved food. The bamboo's antibacterial quality, the vinegar, the salt — together they kept fish edible in an age before refrigerators, and the careful result happens to look beautiful. A widely repeated account traces it to making spawning trout last longer, though the dates and origins are told several ways.

What surprises visitors is how much one maker differs from the next. The boxes look nearly identical, yet the bite of vinegar, the firmness of the cure, and the edge of salt come out clearly distinct — so households keep a favorite shop, and at a midsummer family gathering, "who's bringing whose box" turns into a small running argument.

### Good to know
- **Season**: Year-round; often eaten on a slightly special morning.
- **What it is**: Trout and vinegared rice pressed in a round bamboo-lined frame; flavor varies by maker.
- **How to eat**: Cut pie-style with the included bamboo knife; good chilled or brought to room temperature.
- **Where to find it**: Specialist makers cluster in Toyama City; also supermarkets, roadside stations, and station stalls.
- **Order or ship?**: Sold as a souvenir outside the prefecture; mail-order availability `[to verify]`.

## 2. Toyama Black ramen

Show an out-of-town friend a photo of this bowl and the first reaction is almost always the color — a soy broth dark enough to read as ink. The opening sip lands hard with salt, and there's a reason for that salt.

The account most people repeat is that it grew up in postwar Toyama City as a salty "side soup," meant to be eaten with the white rice that dock and construction workers carried from home — though the exact origins are told more than one way. In that version it was never quite a stand-alone ramen, but a salt-delivery bowl built to ride alongside rice.

You can find Toyama Black shops in Takaoka as well, usually a touch milder than the deepest versions in Toyama City. Living here, the darkness stops shocking you, but newcomers tend to land on the same verdict: the first three sips are tough, and the next ten somehow pull you back. Locally many of us read it less as an exotic regional ramen than as a quiet record of how the area once worked — black, salty, a little blunt, and still around.

### Good to know
- **Season**: Year-round.
- **What it is**: A dark soy-based broth with strong salt; said to have been designed to go with rice.
- **How to eat**: Pair with white rice; newcomers, start small.
- **Where to find it**: Toyama City is the heartland; milder shops exist in Takaoka.
- **Order or ship?**: Whether instant or retort souvenir versions ship `[to verify]`.

## 3. Himi udon

Himi, a port town on Toyama's western coast, draws most of its fame from winter yellowtail and firefly squid. Quietly, it's also known for a noodle: a thin, springy, clean-tasting udon that slips down the throat. It's neither the bouncy heft of Sanuki udon nor the refinement of Inaniwa — thin yet elastic, and easy to eat in any season.

Why so thin? One common explanation is that morning-market fishermen needed a lunch that cooked and slurped fast; another points to the twisting step in how the dough is made. The origins go more than one way. In the first telling, the demands of the work set the shape and the texture simply followed.

The making is its own thing — the dough is hand-stretched while being twisted again and again, a different rhythm from the foot-kneading of Sanuki, and the twist leaves faint air layers inside, which is why something this thin still bites back. Himi sits next to Takaoka, about half an hour by train or car, and in my own kitchen the dried bundles are always on hand: a little firmer than somen, a little thinner than udon. On a hot afternoon, with cold dipping broth and a few condiments, it's my default lunch.

### Good to know
- **Season**: Year-round; cold in summer, warm in winter.
- **What it is**: A thin hand-stretched noodle; the twisting method keeps it springy despite the thinness.
- **How to eat**: Cold dipping broth with condiments, or in a warm soup.
- **Where to find it**: Himi City (next to Takaoka); markets, station shops, and supermarkets across Toyama.
- **Order or ship?**: Sold dried; mail-order availability and any "founded in" claims `[to verify]`.

## 4. Daimon somen

Each summer a relative mails our household a box of somen, and it doesn't look like ordinary somen at all. Open the lid and instead of straight bundles you find several round, tightly coiled discs. This is Daimon somen, a hand-pulled wheat noodle made in the old Daimon district of Tonami City.

The spiral isn't decoration. In the final stretching step each strand is twisted, and when the twisted noodle is bundled it curls into that round shape on its own — the method becomes the look. Just how far the tradition goes back is told several ways `[to verify]`.

It's made only in the cold months, because low, dry winter air suits thin hand-pulling while warm air slackens the dough. So what's made in winter is eaten the following summer: winter cold, in a sense, kept on the table until July, which feels like a small local loop. Thin as it is, it has real chew — boil, rinse in cold water, then dipping broth with grated ginger and scallion, with myoga or shiso to make it feel fully like summer. In winter it goes into a warm broth as nyumen. In my house it's a summer staple: boil it, set out a few condiments, and the meal is ready in about five minutes.

### Good to know
- **Season**: Made in winter (roughly November–February); mainly eaten in summer.
- **What it is**: A coiled hand-pulled somen that curls naturally from the twisting step; thin but chewy.
- **How to eat**: Cold with dipping broth in summer; warm as nyumen in winter.
- **Where to find it**: Tonami City; a summer staple in western Toyama homes.
- **Order or ship?**: Producer numbers, range, and mail-order all shift by season `[to verify]`.

## 5. Takaoka korokke (croquette)

Takaoka calls itself "the town of korokke." It's a city of four centuries of bronze craft, the hometown of the Doraemon creator, and the holder of the Amaharashi coast — and alongside all of that, the croquette. Come evening, the smell of fresh frying drifts out of butcher shops and delis around town.

There are numbers attached, too. In national household surveys, Takaoka has in some years ranked among the highest for korokke spending among the cities measured `[to verify]`. Why? Locals offer several guesses — a craftsman's town wanting fast hot dinners, a lot of butcher shops, fried food warming a cold winter — and no one really knows. What's clear is that the town genuinely loves it.

Takaoka is said to have launched "Takaoka Korokke" as a local brand in 2008 `[to verify]`, complete with a croquette map. Curry, shrimp, shapes modeled on the city's bronze art, café renditions — more shops than you could visit in a day, each working its own version. Near a National Treasure temple, an ordinary butcher sells them hot; tourists rarely stop, but local mothers do on the way home. It isn't fine dining; it's the humblest of foods. Yet loved by a whole town, it becomes one of that town's faces — a National Treasure on one corner, a hot croquette on the next.

### Good to know
- **Season**: Year-round; freshest in the evening.
- **What it is**: A potato-based croquette, with curry, shrimp, bronze-shape and café variations by shop.
- **How to eat**: Eaten fresh on the spot; shop-hop with the local map.
- **Where to find it**: Butchers, delis, supermarkets, and cafés across Takaoka City.
- **Order or ship?**: A fresh-fried street food; price and any mail-order vary by shop `[to verify]`.

## 6. Gokayama tofu

In the mountainous southwest of Toyama lies Gokayama, a World Heritage village known for its steep thatched-roof houses and deep snow. It's also home to a tofu said to be firm enough to tie with a rope and carry — and it really can be tied. Pick it up expecting ordinary tofu and the weight startles you.

Why so firm? It's made with more soybeans than usual and pressed hard to drive out the water. The reasons run back to terrain and climate: in a place where heavy snow once cut off travel through winter, food had to keep, to survive transport without crumbling, and to pack in nutrition. Meeting all three made it solid.

Locally it most often becomes nimono, a simmered dish — firm enough to hold its shape through long cooking, deepened with soy, sugar, carrot, and shiitake. It's also good cut thick and pan-seared like a small steak, crisp outside and dense within, closer to "a block of soybean" than to tofu. Gokayama is about an hour by car from Takaoka, an easy weekend trip, and thatched houses, deep snow, and an unusually firm tofu all belong to one way of living. A single block carries the whole local climate, compressed.

### Good to know
- **Season**: Year-round.
- **What it is**: A hard, dense tofu pressed to remove much of its water; near "a block of soybean."
- **How to eat**: Simmered, or pan-seared in thick slices.
- **Where to find it**: Gokayama, southwest Toyama, about an hour from Takaoka.
- **Order or ship?**: Needs refrigeration; mail-order form `[to verify]`.

## 7. Itoko-ni (simmered roots and beans)

The first time I heard the dish name itoko-ni, I didn't realize it was food. It turned up at a family gathering, and when I asked what it was, the answer was just "itoko-ni" — cousins-simmer. The why is a pun: you add the ingredients oi-oi, "one after another," which slides into oi-oi meaning nephews, and on to itoko, cousins. The cooking order became the name.

It's a country dish found widely from Hokuriku down to the San'in coast. The base is daikon, carrot, burdock, konnyaku, fried tofu, and azuki beans, simmered sweet-savory in soy and sugar. In Toyama it often shows up at Obon and memorial gatherings, though the occasion varies by family and area, and each household tweaks it — black beans instead of azuki, sometimes taro — so it passes down through kitchens as "our itoko-ni."

The order carries the meaning. The hard roots go in first for the long cook; the soft konnyaku and fried tofu come later, each getting only the time it needs. In one pot, everything cooks at its own pace — like cousins, the same line yet a little different, brought together in a single dish. Eaten on a Takaoka winter evening, it has a certain warmth and weight: the broth-soaked daikon, the sweetness of the beans, the cold outside. Every ingredient comes from an ordinary supermarket, yet follow the steps and something home-cooked settles in, which I think is just the time you spent on it.

### Good to know
- **Season**: Year-round; often at Obon and memorial gatherings in Toyama.
- **What it is**: Root vegetables, konnyaku, fried tofu, and beans simmered sweet-savory; named for its cooking order.
- **How to eat**: Hard ingredients first, served warm.
- **Where to find it**: Widespread from Hokuriku to San'in; a home dish in Toyama and Takaoka.
- **Order or ship?**: A home dish, so retail or mail-order rarely applies `[to verify]`.

## 8. Buri-daikon (yellowtail and daikon)

Asked to name a single Toyama winter dish, I'd answer buri-daikon without hesitation: winter yellowtail trimmings and thick-cut daikon, simmered slowly in soy, sugar, sake, and ginger. Nothing exotic in the technique — yet on a cold evening, whether or not it's on the table changes the temperature of the whole house.

Toyama Bay is a major winter ground for yellowtail. The Himi-port "kanburi" is a brand-graded fish, though the brand's season and standards are set by the fishery cooperative `[to verify]`. The trimmings — the head and the cuts around the bone — sell cheaply at local supermarkets, and they often carry even more fat than the prime flesh. Simmered with daikon, they make a home dish that satisfies completely.

The method is plain: cut the daikon into thick rounds and parboil it, blanch the yellowtail and rinse off the dark blood-line, then simmer everything low with soy, sugar, sake, sliced ginger, and water. The golden ratio shifts house to house — ours leans sweet and generous with ginger, while other kitchens go saltier. On a cold night, opening the door to that smell is a small relief, and I think most homes here would recognize it. It isn't a showy regional specialty; just a dish I can make every winter because this is where I live.

### Good to know
- **Season**: Winter, when kanburi is in season.
- **What it is**: Winter-yellowtail trimmings and daikon simmered sweet-savory; the trimmings are fatty and inexpensive.
- **How to eat**: Simmered low and slow; seasoning varies by household.
- **Where to find it**: Toyama; the bay is a major winter yellowtail ground, branded at Himi port.
- **Order or ship?**: A home dish; whether the fish itself ships `[to verify]`.

## 9. Toyama-style oden

Oden in a Toyama home leans a little away from both the Kanto and Kansai styles. The broth runs lighter in color than Kanto's and a touch stronger than Kansai's restraint — though households vary widely — and its backbone is a blend: kombu, dried small sardines, and sometimes katsuobushi all at once.

The ingredients show their region too. The usual daikon, egg, and konnyaku are there, but some homes drop in big slabs of local kamaboko to soak up the broth. In early spring, fish balls made with white shrimp turn up in the shops, and dropped into the pot they melt shrimp umami through everything; local taro, slow to give in, turns silky in the stock.

Winter in Hokuriku brings deep snow, and coming home in the evening to that broth smell filling the house is one of the season's defining memories here. It isn't a special dish — twice a week, ordinary on the table — and it pairs well with warm sake, so the adults eat and drink at once while the children fight over the daikon and the egg. The broth is distinctive most likely thanks to geography: kombu carried in by the old trading ships, a strong dried-fish culture, and access to katsuobushi material, three umami sources meeting in one place, which is itself uncommon.

### Good to know
- **Season**: Winter (often once or twice a week); shrimp fish balls in early spring.
- **What it is**: A blended kombu / dried-sardine / katsuobushi broth, with local kamaboko and taro among the ingredients.
- **How to eat**: With warm sake.
- **Where to find it**: A home dish; ingredients at local shops across Toyama.
- **Order or ship?**: A home dish, so mail-order rarely applies `[to verify]`.

## 10. Winter daikon

Summer daikon and winter daikon could almost pass for different vegetables. Summer's is watery and crisp, suited to salads; winter's is denser and sweeter, made for slow cooking and the hot pot. In Toyama, the winter daikon takes on a presence of its own once it's simmered.

Why does it sweeten in the cold? As temperatures drop, the daikon raises the sugar in its cells to keep from freezing — so it simply turns sweeter with the chill. Toyama is a snowy place, and under the snow in the field the daikon ages slowly through the cold and gathers that sweetness, before it becomes furofuki, or buri-daikon, or goes into the oden.

Our standby is furofuki daikon: thick rounds, edges trimmed and a cross cut into the face, simmered gently in kombu stock — the timing and thickness are a household rule of thumb `[to verify]`. Topped with yuzu miso or dengaku miso and eaten hot, it's a plain dish, yet with winter daikon the first bite floods with liquid, and that liquid is sweet. Through winter, scarcely a day passes without daikon somewhere on the table — grated, furofuki, in buri-daikon, in oden, in the miso soup. It's no luxury ingredient, but in a Hokuriku winter, "no daikon" means the table barely holds together.

### Good to know
- **Season**: Winter.
- **What it is**: Daikon that sweetens in the cold as its fibers tighten; well suited to simmering.
- **How to eat**: Furofuki, buri-daikon, oden, grated, or in miso soup.
- **Where to find it**: Toyama, where snow-cover cold deepens the sweetness.
- **Order or ship?**: An ordinary vegetable, so mail-order rarely applies `[to verify]`.

## 11. Tororo-kombu (shaved kelp)

In a Toyama kitchen there's usually a pack of tororo-kombu — feather-light shavings of kelp. You can eat it straight or drop it into soup, and here it works less as stock material than as a kind of all-purpose seasoning, a hybrid of rice-topping and instant umami.

A few ways my house uses it. Wrapped around a morning rice ball in place of nori, it lifts a sea-scent off the rice. A pinch into finished miso soup deepens it a notch. Put the shavings in a bowl with a little salt, a little soy, and scallion, pour hot water, and you have instant clear soup with no stock to make. Over cold soba it shifts the texture; straight onto fresh hot rice, it opens up the rice's sweetness.

Why is it so close at hand? Toyama's tie to kelp runs back to the Edo-era kitamae-bune trade, which brought Hokkaido kombu to the prefecture's ports in great volume, and that history still sits on the table. Treating shaved kelp as an everyday seasoning turns out to feel a little unusual to people from elsewhere — when I describe these uses to friends from other prefectures they're usually surprised, and I'm surprised that they're surprised. That small mismatch is the shape of a regional food culture, and in a Toyama kitchen, tororo-kombu quietly puts in a day's work, every day.

### Good to know
- **Season**: Year-round, mostly at breakfast.
- **What it is**: Soft, thin kelp shavings that double as stock and rice-topping in one.
- **How to eat**: On rice balls, in miso soup, as instant clear soup, over soba or plain rice.
- **Where to find it**: A pantry staple across Toyama homes (sold in packs).
- **Order or ship?**: A widely sold kelp product; a Toyama-specific mail-order route `[to verify]`.

## 12. Kazari-kamaboko (decorated fish cake)

Attend a wedding in Toyama and the gift bag handed to each guest holds something unexpected: a large pink-and-red "sea bream" — not a real fish, but a bream made entirely of kamaboko, steamed fish cake, anywhere from about 30 to over 50 centimeters long. Guests from outside the prefecture almost all blink and ask what they're supposed to do with it.

Putting kamaboko in a wedding favor is said to survive only in Toyama and parts of Ishikawa, an unusual local custom. The sea bream shape is the standard: tai (bream) puns with medetai, "auspicious," so it's a regular at weddings and housewarmings. Pine-bamboo-plum, shrimp, and crane-and-turtle shapes are made too, each formed by hand by a craftsman.

You don't eat the bream on the spot. You carry it home and the family performs the tai-giri, the ceremonial cut, dividing head, body, and tail in an auspicious order — and slices often go around to the neighbors, so one decorated fish is parceled out among many households, and even people who weren't at the wedding receive a share of the celebration. Living here you get invited to weddings often enough, and each time, lifting the big bream out of the bag, I think: yes, this is Toyama. It's no fine ornament or formal craft, just fish cake, and yet for people here it simply is what a wedding gift means. That's how a regional custom keeps going — everyone keeps doing it.

### Good to know
- **Season**: Year-round, as celebrations arise (weddings, housewarmings).
- **What it is**: A bream of dyed kamaboko, roughly 30 to over 50 cm; also pine-bamboo-plum, shrimp, and crane-and-turtle shapes.
- **How to eat**: Brought home, cut ceremonially (tai-giri), and shared with neighbors.
- **Where to find it**: A wedding custom in Toyama and parts of Ishikawa; from craftsman kamaboko shops.
- **Order or ship?**: A celebratory decorated item; mail-order availability `[to verify]`.

## 13. Local sake

Toyama has more than twenty sake breweries — the count shifts over time `[to verify]` — and most of them draw on two things: the underground water of the Tateyama range and rice from the Toyama plain. Few of the labels are flashy, yet they share a character: the water is remarkably clean.

Start with the water. Sake is mostly water, and the water is said to decide the taste. On the Toyama plain, the heavy snow that falls on Tateyama becomes groundwater and seeps out over decades, and many breweries use that spring water as their brewing water. A sip slips in lightly and leaves no heaviness — the shared signature of Toyama sake.

Each brewery has its own personality, but the common thread is a flavor that doesn't push: less "sake to admire" than "sake that stays out of the meal's way." It's built to be drunk with kombu-jime, masu-zushi, and white shrimp, sitting naturally inside the whole local food culture. Here it's less something for special days than something you simply pair with ordinary meals — cod hot pot in winter, firefly squid in spring, white shrimp in summer, masu-zushi in autumn, the cup shifting with the season. Ask at an izakaya for "whatever's local" and the seasonal fish arrives with the matching seasonal pour. The land's water, the land's rice, the land's food, all tied together.

### Good to know
- **Season**: Year-round; pairings shift with the season.
- **What it is**: Sake brewed with Tateyama spring water and Toyama-plain rice; a light, meal-friendly character.
- **How to eat**: With local foods like kombu-jime, masu-zushi, and white shrimp.
- **Where to find it**: Across Toyama; local-sake corners at stations and the airport.
- **Order or ship?**: Specific labels, brewery count, and mail-order `[to verify]`.

## 14. Usugoori (thin-ice sweet)

In Toyama there's a confection shaped like the thinnest sheet of ice on a lake. It's called usugoori — literally "thin ice": a small square rice cracker glazed on one side with refined wasanbon sugar, about a millimeter thick. One look and the name makes sense.

It's said to have been made for a long time by a long-standing shop in Oyabe City, though just how long `[to verify]`. The method is simple but exacting: pound rice and stretch it, dry it into thin crackers, brush each one by hand with dissolved wasanbon, and dry it again at room temperature. Only a few steps take to machines; much of it is still done by hand.

In the mouth it snaps crisp first, and then the wasanbon's refined sweetness spreads softly across the tongue. As you chew, the cracker's faint saltiness and the glaze's sweetness mingle, and the balance is quietly elegant enough to keep you reaching for another; cold sencha or matcha suits it well. Seeing it the first time, the translucent thinness is a surprise, and the sensibility that named it "ice" is a pleasure — to people in snow-deep, long-wintered Hokuriku, thin ice forming on a lake must have been a specific, quiet beauty. Oyabe City sits next to Takaoka, within half an hour by train or car, and the sweets are sold plainly in Takaoka supermarkets and department stores. Each piece is individually wrapped, easy to set out for a guest — a historic confection living in an everyday drawer.

### Good to know
- **Season**: Year-round (good for tea or as a gift).
- **What it is**: A thin white rice cracker glazed with wasanbon sugar; crisp, with a delicate sweetness.
- **How to eat**: With cold sencha or matcha.
- **Where to find it**: A long-standing shop in Oyabe City (next to Takaoka, within half an hour); also Takaoka stores.
- **Order or ship?**: Individually wrapped and keeps well; founding year and mail-order `[to verify]`.

## 15. Shiraebi senbei (white-shrimp crackers)

Asked what to bring back from Toyama, I almost always land on shiraebi senbei — white-shrimp rice crackers — for a plain reason: I've never met anyone who disliked them. Shiraebi are small, semi-transparent shrimp, roughly 5 to 8 centimeters, clear when raw and turning cherry-pink once blanched. Toyama Bay is a major ground for them, though small landings occur elsewhere, so the "where" wants careful wording `[to verify]`.

They go into sashimi, kakiage fritters, and fish paste, but the form that travels widely is the cracker. The reason is keeping quality: fresh shiraebi spoil fast, so enjoying the flavor away from the source means a processed form. Worked into rice-cracker batter and baked, the shrimp's umami turns to fragrance — no refrigeration, light, hard to break, long shelf life, meeting nearly every requirement of a souvenir.

Eat a few and one tends to lead to another. The salt-and-oil balance is judged just right, and they go with beer, tea, or coffee alike; in our house they're a standing item in the cupboard. Share a pack among family or coworkers and someone always asks for one more, which is exactly why they're so hard to get wrong as a gift. Regional souvenirs come in two kinds — the striking ones that win social media, and the quietly dependable ones bought again every visit — and these are firmly the second sort. "Oh, the usual ones" may be the strongest review a souvenir can earn.

### Good to know
- **Season**: Year-round as a snack; the shrimp's own fishing season runs from spring.
- **What it is**: A rice cracker made with white-shrimp paste; fragrant, with a balanced salt and oil.
- **How to eat**: With beer, tea, or coffee; easy to share.
- **Where to find it**: Widely sold at stations and souvenir shops across Toyama.
- **Order or ship?**: Light, sturdy, and long-keeping, so well suited to travel; mail-order `[to verify]`.
