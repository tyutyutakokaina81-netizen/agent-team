## 0. 商品コンセプト（要点）

【事実】
- クラスタAには「住人の一次観察」で書かれた高岡・富山ネタが約70本ある。
- 既存記事はすべて日本語＋短い英語要約付き。英語ガイドはこの英語パートを核に再編集する。

【考察】
- 既存の旅行ガイドは「観光地の網羅」型が多い。本商品の差別化は **"a resident's notebook"（住人の手帳）** という一人称トーン。STATE.mdの「観光ガイド調NG／住人視点MUST」をそのまま英語商品の独自性に転用できる。
- 富山・高岡は英語の一次情報が薄い土地。海外読者には「現地に住む書き手の視点」自体が希少価値になり得る。

【提案】
- 商品名（案）：**"Takaoka & Toyama: A Resident's Notebook"**（サブ：*Food, coast, and craft on Japan's quiet west coast*）
- 立ち位置：ガイドブックではなく「住人のフィールドノート」。網羅性より視点で売る。
- 体裁：PDF＋EPUB（Gumroad）。約40〜60ページ。写真はオーナー実写 or 実写生成プロンプト（A2/A3遵守、本文には`[Photo X]`プレースホルダのみ）。

### Chapter 1 — The Table
#### *What the bay and the cold bring*

I did not grow up thinking of my hometown as a food destination. The fish on the table was just dinner. It took leaving, and coming back, to notice how unusual that dinner actually was.

Toyama sits on Japan's west coast, facing a bay that drops away steeply just offshore. Cold water, deep water, close to land — that single fact shapes most of what ends up on a local plate. You taste the geography before anyone explains it to you.

Take the small shrimp the bay is known for. Translucent when alive, turning faint pink in the light, they live several hundred meters down. In most parts of the world that depth means a long voyage. Here the seabed falls away within sight of the harbor, so the catch comes back the same day. Locals call it the jewel of the bay, and for once the nickname feels earned rather than marketed. Served over rice, hand-peeled by the bowlful, it is the kind of thing a visitor treats as a once-in-a-lifetime meal and a resident treats as a Tuesday.

The cold does the other half of the work. Long before refrigerators, winters here were spent turning the sea's surplus into something that would keep. Fish pressed between sheets of kelp until the flesh firms and deepens. Mackerel buried in rice bran for months. Squid cured in its own ink. None of this was invented to impress anyone; it was invented to get a household through to spring. That it tastes remarkable now is, in a sense, a happy accident of necessity.

Kelp deserves its own mention, because the appetite for it here is hard to explain by geography alone. The answer is history: a coastal shipping route once carried kelp down from the northern seas, and the towns along the way kept the habit long after the ships stopped. So you find kelp shaved into threads over rice, pressed against sashimi, dissolved into broth — a taste for it that outlived the trade that created it.

If you want one rule for eating here, it is this: ask what season it is, then order whatever the cold or the bay is offering that week. The showy dishes are real, but the quiet ones — a winter root simmered with yellowtail, a bowl of fermented turnip and fish, a thick local noodle pulled thin by old habit — are where the place actually lives.

I will walk you through these a few at a time in the pages that follow. Not as a checklist of must-eats, but the way a neighbor might: here is what I keep in my own kitchen, here is what I order when someone visits, here is the thing I only understood the meaning of after eating it for years.
