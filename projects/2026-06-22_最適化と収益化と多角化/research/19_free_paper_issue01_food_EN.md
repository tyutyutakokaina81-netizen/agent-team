# Toyama Bay at the Table — A Resident's Food Notebook

### Free Paper · Issue 01 · Food

*Free to read. Free to share. Written from Toyama, on Japan's western coast.*

---

## From the editor

I started keeping notes about food because I kept forgetting which month things appeared. Then I noticed something stranger: the dishes I'd eaten my whole life without a second thought were the ones visitors found most surprising. So this is not a guidebook and not a "best of" list. It's a resident's notebook — the fish, shellfish, seaweed, and slow ferments that turn up on an ordinary table beside Toyama Bay.

Two things shape almost everything in these pages. One is the bay itself: deep, cold, and close to shore, so a day's fishing can reach water hundreds of meters down. The other is kelp — kombu — carried here for centuries by ship from the far north, until it soaked all the way into home cooking. Geography and history, still showing up at dinner.

A note on how to read this. Each entry has a short paragraph in my own voice, then a compact **Good to know** box: when it's in season, what it actually is, how people eat it, where you might find it (shop type and city only), and whether you can take some home. Where a fact is worth double-checking before you rely on it, you'll see ✅ for things I'm confident about and ⚠️ for things to confirm locally. Anything I couldn't verify is marked `[to verify]` rather than guessed.

Welcome to the first issue. Pull up a chair.

## 1. Shiraebi — the shrimp you can see through

Hold one up to a window and the light passes straight through it. *Shiraebi*, the glass shrimp of Toyama Bay, runs about 6–8 cm and is clear when alive, flushing a soft pink only when the light catches it. People here have called it "the jewel of the bay" for generations, though to most of us it's less a jewel than a normal spring-to-autumn fixture on the table.

It lives below roughly 300 meters. What makes the local fishery work is the shape of the seabed: a steep underwater canyon comes in close to land, so a deep-water catch can be landed on a single day's trip — a combination of conditions found in only a handful of places. The plain way to eat it is *shiraebi-don*, a small bowl of rice under a hundred or two hand-peeled shrimp, gently sweet and clean. In season you'll also see it lightly fried or grilled at the supermarket.

### Good to know
- **Season** — Roughly spring through autumn.
- **What it is** — A small deep-water shrimp of Toyama Bay; translucent raw, pale pink cooked, with a delicate sweetness.
- **How to eat** — As *shiraebi-don* over rice; also lightly fried or salt-grilled.
- **Where to find it** — Fish shops, supermarkets, and sushi places around Toyama City and the Imizu area in season.
- **Take home / ship** — Peeled shrimp is fragile and labor-intensive, so it runs pricey. Frozen or processed mail-order availability `[to verify]`.
- ✅ Translucent appearance and the deep-water habitat are well established. ⚠️ The exact peak weeks vary year to year.

## 2. Nodoguro — the white fish that eats like toro

There's usually one fish in the case priced apart from everything around it. Locals call it *nodoguro*; the proper name is blackthroat seaperch (*akamutsu*). It's small and red-skinned, with only the inside of its mouth gone dark — and it costs what it costs for two reasons at once.

It's hard to land. Living somewhere around 200–400 meters down, it doesn't come up on a reliable schedule. And for a white fish it carries an unusual amount of fat, which has earned it the nickname "the toro of white fish." At home, salt-grilling suits it best: salt on, skin seared hard, the fat under the skin rendering as it cooks until the lean flesh turns improbably rich. In our house it's a few-times-a-year fish — a birthday, a midsummer gathering, the end of the year. I've come to think luxury is set less by how much arrives than by how seldom it does.

### Good to know
- **Season** — Said to be fattest in autumn and winter; the local peak `[to verify]`.
- **What it is** — *Akamutsu*; a fatty deep-water white fish, nicknamed "the toro of white fish."
- **How to eat** — Salt-grilled is the standard; also sashimi, simmered, or in shabu-shabu.
- **Where to find it** — Fish shops and sushi spots across Toyama, sometimes sold a single fish at a time.
- **Take home / ship** — A premium fish; mail-order routes and pricing `[to verify]`.

## 3. Hotaru-ika — a blue light on a spring night

On certain spring nights the waterline glows a faint blue, and the first time you see it you don't quite trust your eyes. The source is *hotaru-ika*, the firefly squid — a little squid of 5–7 cm that lights up when something disturbs it. From around March into May they push into the shallows to spawn, and along parts of the coast the edge of the sea flickers after dark.

What I like is the double life of the thing. The same creature people drive out to watch at midnight is sitting, pre-boiled, in a tray at the grocery store the next morning, eaten with a sharp vinegar-miso dressing. It's spectacle and weeknight side dish at once. And when the trays quietly disappear from the shelves toward the end of May, that's how I know — better than any date — that spring is over here.

### Good to know
- **Season** — March to May, tapering off around late May.
- **What it is** — A 5–7 cm squid that glows blue when disturbed.
- **How to eat** — Boiled, dressed with vinegar-miso.
- **Where to find it** — Supermarkets along the Toyama City–Namerikawa coast in season.
- **Take home / ship** — Preserved forms such as sake-marinated or boiled may travel; mail-order availability `[to verify]`.
- ✅ The spring spawning run and the firefly glow are well documented.

## 4. Fukuragi — yellowtail, before its promotion

From early summer into fall, one fish keeps reappearing at the supermarket counter, and it's worth knowing what it is: *fukuragi*, the Toyama name for young yellowtail (*buri*). Around 30–50 cm, it's a yellowtail before the heavy winter fat sets in.

Yellowtail is a "promotion fish" — it takes a new name at each stage of growth. In Toyama the run goes roughly kozukura → fukuragi → gando → buri, though where one name stops and the next begins shifts from place to place. The grown buri is a prized winter fish; fukuragi is its lighter, warm-season self. At home it usually turns into salt-grilled fillets or teriyaki — the leaner flesh takes a sweet-soy glaze nicely and skips the heaviness of a wintry simmered *buri-daikon*. There's a quiet pleasure in eating the fish before it's grown, knowing the winter version is coming.

### Good to know
- **Season** — Early summer through autumn.
- **What it is** — The Toyama name for young yellowtail; lean and mild, a middle stage of the "promotion fish."
- **How to eat** — Salt-grilled or teriyaki; also as sashimi.
- **Where to find it** — Supermarket fish counters across Toyama, sold as fillets.
- **Take home / ship** — An everyday household fish; price varies by shop, size, and season. Mail-order availability `[to verify]`.
- ✅ The name-by-stage custom is real, though ⚠️ the exact size cutoffs differ by area and cooperative.

## 5. Beni-zuwai — the feast that isn't too special

Say "crab" in winter here and the first thing most of us picture isn't the luxury one. The high-end *hon-zuwai* (sold elsewhere as Matsuba or Echizen crab) is landed nearby, but what actually lands on the home table is *beni-zuwai*, the red snow crab — more affordable, and pitched at exactly the right distance: a feast that doesn't feel like an occasion.

It's red before it ever meets boiling water, more vivid than its pricier cousin, and rows of it seem to glow at the market. The meat is delicate and full of moisture, lightly sweet. We keep it plain: buy it already boiled, eat it as is, snap a leg, slide the meat out, maybe a touch of crab vinegar. The fiddly work of getting meat from shell is part of the ritual. An ordinary feast you stop noticing turns out, when you stop to think, to be its own kind of good fortune.

### Good to know
- **Season** — Winter; exact fishing dates `[to verify]`.
- **What it is** — Red snow crab; already red before boiling, with delicate, moist, lightly sweet meat.
- **How to eat** — Bought pre-boiled, eaten as is with a little crab vinegar.
- **Where to find it** — Markets and supermarkets across Toyama, at a reachable price.
- **Take home / ship** — Mail-order availability of boiled crab `[to verify]`.

## 6. Bai-gai — the quiet regular

Look in the corner of the case at a fish shop and you'll find something unglamorous holding its spot: *bai-gai*, a small spiral sea snail from the bay, a relative of the whelk. It's modest, local, and largely unsung — and it's there almost every day, which is rather the point.

You can eat it as sashimi, but at home it nearly always becomes a simmered dish, cooked whole in the shell with soy, sake, sugar, and ginger. You wind the meat out with a toothpick: a firm chew first, then a rush of concentrated sea broth. It slots neatly beside sake, and Toyama takes its sake seriously — chilled, warm, or hot, the snail holds up. First glass with sashimi, second with simmered bai-gai is the standard evening pattern at our place. You won't see it on a poster; it isn't photogenic. But a town that keeps a plain everyday regular like this, year in and year out, has a food culture with deep roots — regulars outlast fashions.

### Good to know
- **Season** — Available across much of the year, with some seasonal swing; a clear peak `[to verify]`.
- **What it is** — A small spiral sea snail from Toyama Bay; firm bite, rich broth.
- **How to eat** — Simmered in soy, sake, sugar, and ginger; a natural match for sake.
- **Where to find it** — Fish shops across Toyama, most days.
- **Take home / ship** — The simmered form keeps reasonably well; retail and mail-order form `[to verify]`.

## 7. Kombu-jime — sashimi, rested overnight in kelp

Here's a kitchen habit shared along this coast, in Toyama and neighboring Ishikawa: *kombu-jime*. Take fresh sashimi — usually a white fish or squid — salt it lightly, lay it between sheets of kelp, and leave it in the fridge overnight. By morning it isn't quite the same food anymore.

The reason is chemistry. The kelp pulls water out of the fish and gives back its own glutamic acid, which is umami in its purest form. What you get is denser, glossier, and far deeper-tasting than where you started. As for why this coast: Edo-era trading ships, the *kitamae-bune*, hauled great quantities of northern kelp down the Japan Sea, and this region sat on the route. Good kelp has been an everyday material here for so long that it became an ingredient in its own right, not just a stock. You'll find kombu-jime in an ordinary supermarket — special enough for a New Year's table, plain enough for a Tuesday. It's one of the clearest tastes of umami I know.

### Good to know
- **Season** — Year-round; a technique rather than a catch, so not tied to a season. Common at New Year and family gatherings.
- **What it is** — White fish or squid pressed with kelp; supple texture, deep umami.
- **How to eat** — Sliced and eaten as sashimi; flavor deepens the longer it rests.
- **Where to find it** — Markets and supermarkets in Toyama and Ishikawa; this coast is its heartland.
- **Take home / ship** — A refrigerated, prepared sashimi product; mail-order form `[to verify]` — buying locally is the sure bet.
- ✅ The kombu-jime tradition and the kitamae-bune kelp route are well established.

## 8. Heshiko — mackerel, a year under bran

At the edge of the fish section in a local supermarket you might notice small bran-packed pouches. That's *heshiko*: mackerel cured with rice bran and salt and left to ferment for about a year. Its home base is neighboring Fukui, but it's made along this coast too and turns up on ordinary tables.

The making is patient. You salt-cure the raw mackerel first to draw out the water, pack it into a barrel with rice bran, salt, and chili, weight it down, and let time do the rest for roughly a year. What comes out is deep brown and frankly pungent — a first sniff can stop a newcomer cold. You can slice it thin and eat it raw, but I prefer it lightly grilled, when the fat renders and the aroma turns toasty and it falls into step beside sake. Over rice as *ochazuke* it's good too. It exists because winters once shut down fishing for stretches at a time, so a summer catch had to be made to last until spring. A survival measure then; something food lovers seek out now.

### Good to know
- **Season** — Year-round (a preserve aged about a year); the source mackerel is a summer–autumn fish.
- **What it is** — Mackerel fermented in rice bran; deep brown, salty, with fermented depth, bran sweetness, and a little chili heat.
- **How to eat** — Thin-sliced raw, lightly grilled, or laid over rice.
- **Where to find it** — Fukui is the heartland; also at supermarkets in Toyama and Ishikawa.
- **Take home / ship** — A long-keeping ferment well suited to mail order; specific sources `[to verify]`.

## 9. Ika no kuro-zukuri — squid in black

There's a small jet-black jar that lives on a lot of tables here, and the look of it surprises people before they've tasted a thing. *Ika no kuro-zukuri* — "squid in black" — is raw squid, its own innards, its own ink, and salt. That's the entire list. You mix it, let it rest, let it ferment, and you end up with a dark, intense paste that makes a bowl of plain white rice vanish.

It started, the story goes, as a fisherman's way to use every part of a squid — body, organs, ink — with nothing wasted, kept by salt and time before refrigerators existed. It sits in the same family as heshiko and kombu-jime: this coast's habit of turning preservation into flavor. A teaspoon over rice is plenty; salt, squid umami, the deep bitterness of the ink, and the lift of fermentation all arrive in one bite, and it carries sake well in small doses. The color startles first-timers, so start small — friends from out of region tend to flinch, take a careful taste, and then quietly come around. Because it keeps, a small jar travels.

### Good to know
- **Season** — Year-round (a salt-preserved ferment).
- **What it is** — A fermented paste of squid, innards, ink, and salt; jet-black and intense.
- **How to eat** — A teaspoon over rice; also alongside sake.
- **Where to find it** — Souvenir shops and supermarkets in Toyama, in small jars.
- **Take home / ship** — Keeps well and suits a souvenir; specific mail-order routes `[to verify]`.

## 10. Kabura-zushi — a winter ferment with the wrong name

Winter brings an odd, wonderful thing to tables in Toyama and Ishikawa: *kabura-zushi*. Salted turnip slices are wrapped around salted yellowtail (sometimes mackerel) and fermented in rice malt. Don't be misled by the "zushi" in the name — it's far closer to a pickle than to anything you'd find at a sushi counter.

It's made only in December and January, when the cold air supports the slow fermentation. Fewer households pickle their own these days, but local pickle shops turn it out every winter, and a plate of it sliced thin, set out around New Year beside warm sake, is one of the season's signature sights. A single bite delivers four things at once — salt, fermented umami, the crispness of the turnip, the richness of the yellowtail. Everyone swears by their own version: yellowtail or mackerel, salt heavy or light, malt forward or restrained. A summer visitor will simply never meet it. This one belongs to winter alone.

### Good to know
- **Season** — Winter only; made in December and January.
- **What it is** — Salted turnip around salted yellowtail (or mackerel), fermented in rice malt; closer to a pickle. Four flavors at once.
- **How to eat** — Sliced thin and eaten as is; pairs with warm sake.
- **Where to find it** — Pickle shops in Toyama and Ishikawa each winter.
- **Take home / ship** — Seasonal and refrigerated; mail-order availability and window `[to verify]`.

## 11. Himi himono — the dried fish that earns the morning

On Toyama's western side, near the base of the Noto Peninsula, sits a small port town called Himi. It's known for winter yellowtail, but I'll admit the thing I look forward to more is the dried fish — Himi's *himono*.

Cooking dried fish on a weekday morning is a chore, and I won't pretend otherwise: the smell lingers, the pan needs a scrub. Himi's himono is one of the few worth the bother. Horse mackerel, barracuda, young yellowtail — each salted its own way, firm to the bite yet melting with fat as you chew. Rice, miso soup, and one of these, and breakfast is settled. What sets it apart comes down to fish, salt, and wind: Himi's port works largely with fixed nets set out in the bay, which take migrating fish without bruising them, and the drying happens close by. A short trip from sea to rack is much of the secret. It isn't cheap, but I buy it a few times a year, and a morning we've decided on "the Himi one" feels a notch above the usual. You don't have to travel to the port to taste it — keep it in the rotation from home.

### Good to know
- **Season** — Year-round (dried and keeps); peak by species `[to verify]`.
- **What it is** — Dried fish from Himi, a port town in western Toyama — horse mackerel, barracuda, young yellowtail; fixed-net fishing keeps the fish fresh.
- **How to eat** — Grilled, with rice and miso soup.
- **Where to find it** — Supermarkets and roadside stations across Toyama; the port also ships directly.
- **Take home / ship** — Direct mail order exists via the port; specific shops and sites `[to verify]`.

## 12. Salt-cured wakame — keeping the sea for summer

Wakame from the bay peaks in spring and early summer — though, as with most things here, the timing drifts with the year and the spot. The local trick for stretching a harvest peak across the months that follow is salt-curing. The supermarket sells it by the bowlful; you rinse it quickly and it goes into miso soup, salads, and vinegar dishes alike.

Most people picture dried wakame, the kind that swells when you soak it. But plenty of us here prefer the salt-cured sort, purely for texture — it keeps more crunch and bite. Rinse it, soak it five minutes to pull the salt back out, dress it with vinegar, and the smell of the shore lifts right off the plate. It holds about a month in the fridge, so the old habit is to lay in plenty at spring's end and let it carry the summer table — with cucumber in a vinegar dish, over tofu, as the green note in soup. The bay, where cold and warm currents meet, is rich in seaweed of many kinds; this is one of the quieter ones, but it brings the shore indoors when the weather turns hot.

### Good to know
- **Season** — Wakame peaks in spring and early summer (varies by area and year).
- **What it is** — Salt-cured wakame seaweed; keeps more bite than the dried kind.
- **How to eat** — Rinsed and desalted, then into soup, salad, or vinegar dishes.
- **Where to find it** — Local supermarkets across Toyama.
- **Take home / ship** — Keeps about a month refrigerated; mail-order routes `[to verify]`.

## 13. Kombu — what the ships brought, still in the fridge

Here's a small puzzle: a place right on the sea that leans heavily on kelp harvested far to the north. Toyama is sometimes cited as ranking among the highest in Japan for household spending on kombu in certain years `[to verify]`, and the explanation runs back to the *kitamae-bune* trade ships of the Edo and Meiji eras.

Those ships carried Hokkaido kelp down the Japan Sea toward Osaka, and one of their waypoints was the port of Fushiki in the Takaoka area. A waypoint doesn't just forward goods — it sells some into the local market. So there was always good kelp on hand, and food culture begins with making something from what's around. Here kombu became more than a stock material; it turned into an ingredient — kelp-cured sashimi, kombu-wrapped rice balls, shaved *tororo-kombu*, kombu-rolled fish cake. To this day a local supermarket's kombu shelves are visibly fuller than you'd expect, sometimes a whole aisle of it. At our table it's simply always there: tororo-kombu on the morning rice ball, a pinch of shredded kombu in instant noodles, kombu-jime with the evening sashimi. I can't recall a day without it — something a ship carried two centuries ago, still living in the refrigerator.

### Good to know
- **Season** — Year-round (a dried, keeping staple).
- **What it is** — A food culture built on northern kelp left behind by the trade ships; used as an ingredient, not just for stock.
- **How to eat** — As tororo-kombu, shredded kombu, kombu-jime, and across everyday cooking.
- **Where to find it** — Supermarkets across Toyama carry an unusually wide kombu selection.
- **Take home / ship** — Dried and well suited to mail order; the local-range selection and its mail-order routes `[to verify]`. ⚠️ The claim that Toyama ranks near the top nationally for kombu spending varies by year and survey `[to verify]`.
