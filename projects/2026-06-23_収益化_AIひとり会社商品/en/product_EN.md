# The AI Solo Operator's Prompt Pack

**28 working prompts to run a one-person business with AI — for solo founders, freelancers, and indie workers worldwide.**

Version 1.0 · Last updated 2026-06-23

---

## Before you start (read this — it's short)

These prompts assume you work alone (or nearly alone) and use a general-purpose AI assistant (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or similar) as your "team." They are written to be **copy-paste-and-edit**, not theory.

Three habits make every prompt below work better:

1. **Replace the brackets.** Anything in `[SQUARE BRACKETS]` is a placeholder. Fill it before you send. The more specific you are, the less generic the output.
2. **Give the AI a role and a goal first.** Most prompts here open by assigning a role ("You are my…"). That single line does most of the quality work.
3. **Iterate, don't accept the first draft.** The fastest pattern is: get a draft → reply "tighten this / make it more concrete / cut 30%" → use it. Treat the AI like a fast junior colleague, not an oracle.

**How prompts are formatted here:** each one has a short *Use it when* line, the *Prompt* (the part you copy), and a *Tip* for getting more out of it.

A note on tone: I've kept the prompts plain and direct. Swap in your own voice — if a prompt sounds too formal or too casual for your work, tell the AI your preferred tone in one sentence and it will adapt.

## 1. Planning & Strategy

### Prompt 1 — Turn a vague idea into a one-week plan
*Use it when:* you have an idea but no first step.

```
You are my operations partner for a one-person business.
Here is my idea: [DESCRIBE IDEA IN 2–3 SENTENCES].
My constraint: I can spend about [HOURS] hours on it this week and roughly
[BUDGET] in cash.
Give me a one-week plan with at most 5 concrete tasks, ordered so the
riskiest assumption gets tested first. For each task, state: the task, the
expected output, and how I'll know it worked. Skip anything that isn't
needed to learn whether this is worth continuing.
```

*Tip:* Add "Then ask me the one question that would most change this plan." It surfaces the thing you're avoiding.

### Prompt 2 — Pressure-test a decision before you commit
*Use it when:* you're about to spend money or time and want a second opinion.

```
I'm deciding whether to [DECISION]. My reasons for yes: [REASONS].
My worries: [WORRIES].
Act as a skeptical advisor who wants me to succeed. List the 3 strongest
arguments against doing this, the 1 condition under which it's clearly a
good idea, and a cheaper or smaller way to test the same bet first.
Be direct. Don't reassure me.
```

*Tip:* Run it twice — once as "skeptical advisor," once as "an experienced peer who's done this." Compare.

### Prompt 3 — Set a focus for the quarter without a planning ritual
*Use it when:* you want direction but hate formal goal-setting.

```
I run a one-person [TYPE OF BUSINESS]. Right now my situation is:
[2–3 SENTENCES ON REVENUE, CLIENTS, ENERGY, WHAT'S WORKING/NOT].
Help me pick ONE focus for the next 90 days that, if it goes well, makes the
other problems smaller. Give me 3 candidate focuses, your recommended one,
and the reason. Then give me a single weekly check-in question I can ask
myself to stay on track.
```

*Tip:* Save the weekly question somewhere you'll see it. The prompt's value is the one recurring question, not the plan.

### Prompt 4 — Break a stuck project into a next action
*Use it when:* a project has stalled and you don't know why.

```
This project is stuck: [PROJECT]. What's done: [DONE].
What's blocking me (or what I think is blocking me): [BLOCKER].
Diagnose the most likely real reason it's stuck (it may not be the one I
named). Then give me the single smallest next action I could finish in 25
minutes that moves it forward. One action only.
```

*Tip:* If the AI gives several actions, reply "Pick one." Stuck projects usually need a decision, not a list.

### Prompt 5 — Design a simple offer
*Use it when:* you can do the work but haven't packaged it as something to sell.

```
I can do this for clients: [SKILL / SERVICE]. My ideal client is roughly:
[WHO]. They currently solve this problem by: [STATUS QUO].
Help me shape one clear offer: a name, what's included, what's explicitly
NOT included, who it's wrong for, and a one-sentence description a stranger
would understand. Keep the scope small enough that I can deliver it reliably
alone. Don't suggest tiers yet.
```

*Tip:* "Don't suggest tiers yet" matters — solo operators over-complicate offers early. Ship one before you branch.

## 2. Research & Validation

### Prompt 6 — Map a market you're new to
*Use it when:* you're entering a space and want the lay of the land.

```
I'm exploring [MARKET / NICHE] as a solo operator.
Give me a plain-language map: who the typical buyers are, what they're
already paying for, the 3–4 common ways people serve this market, and where
a one-person operation has an edge vs. where it gets crushed by bigger players.
Flag anything you're unsure about so I can verify it myself.
```

*Tip:* AI can be confidently wrong about market facts. Treat this as a hypothesis to check, not a source. The "flag anything you're unsure about" line is doing real work — keep it.

### Prompt 7 — Write questions for a customer conversation
*Use it when:* you can talk to a potential customer and don't want to waste it.

```
I'm going to talk to a potential customer for [OFFER]. I want to learn
whether this problem is real and worth paying to solve — NOT to pitch.
Write me 7 open questions that get them describing their actual current
behavior and past spending, not their opinions or hypotheticals. Order them
from easy to sensitive. Add one question that reveals whether they'd
realistically pay.
```

*Tip:* The rule "behavior and past spending, not opinions" is the whole game in customer research. People are polite about ideas and honest about what they already do.

### Prompt 8 — Summarize messy research into a decision
*Use it when:* you've gathered notes/links and need a conclusion.

```
Here are my raw notes from researching [TOPIC]:
[PASTE NOTES].
Summarize what I actually found in 5 bullets. Then tell me: what does this
suggest I should do, what's still unknown, and what's the cheapest way to
resolve the biggest unknown. Separate what the notes support from your own
inference, and label which is which.
```

*Tip:* "Label which is which" prevents the AI from blending your evidence with its guesses. Always ask for the split.

### Prompt 9 — Sanity-check a claim before you repeat it
*Use it when:* you're about to publish or pitch a "fact."

```
I want to state this publicly: "[CLAIM]".
Play fact-checker. Is this likely true, partly true, or shaky? What would
I need to verify it, and what's a more careful way to phrase it that I
could defend if challenged? If it can't be supported, say so plainly.
```

*Tip:* Use this on your own marketing copy. It quietly removes the exaggerations that cost trust later.

## 3. Writing & Content

### Prompt 10 — First draft of anything, in your voice
*Use it when:* the blank page is the problem.

```
Write a first draft of [WHAT: email / post / page / description].
Audience: [WHO]. Goal: [WHAT THEY SHOULD UNDERSTAND OR DO].
Length: [ROUGH LENGTH]. Tone: [e.g. plain, warm, direct — no hype].
Here's the raw substance, unedited: [YOUR BULLET POINTS / BRAIN DUMP].
Keep my facts. Don't invent details I didn't give you. If something's
missing, leave a clearly marked [GAP] instead of making it up.
```

*Tip:* The "[GAP] instead of making it up" instruction is the single most useful line for keeping AI writing honest. Reuse it everywhere.

### Prompt 11 — Tighten writing that's almost there
*Use it when:* you have a draft that feels bloated.

```
Tighten this without changing the meaning or my voice:
[PASTE DRAFT].
Cut filler, fix weak sentences, and remove any claim that sounds bigger than
the facts support. Don't add new ideas. Return the edited version, then a
2-line note on what you changed and why.
```

*Tip:* Ask for the "what changed" note — you'll learn your own writing tics and need the AI less over time.

### Prompt 12 — Rewrite for a different reader
*Use it when:* you wrote something for yourself and now need it to land for someone else.

```
Rewrite this for [NEW AUDIENCE], who care about [WHAT THEY CARE ABOUT] and
already know [WHAT THEY KNOW]:
[PASTE TEXT].
Keep it the same length or shorter. Drop jargon they wouldn't use. Lead with
the part that matters most to them.
```

*Tip:* Run it for each audience separately. One "universal" version usually serves no one.

### Prompt 13 — Turn one piece into several formats
*Use it when:* you want more from work you already did.

```
Here's a piece I made: [PASTE OR DESCRIBE].
Repurpose it into: (a) a short post, (b) a 5-point summary, and (c) a single
strong opening line I could use to introduce it.
Keep each one standalone and don't pad. If the source is too thin to
support a format, tell me instead of stretching it.
```

*Tip:* Don't auto-publish all three. Pick the one that fits where you actually have an audience.

### Prompt 14 — Write a product or service description with no hype
*Use it when:* you need sales copy that doesn't make you cringe.

```
Write a description for [PRODUCT / SERVICE].
What it is: [PLAIN DESCRIPTION]. Who it helps and how: [BENEFIT].
What it does NOT do: [LIMITS].
Rules: no superlatives, no "revolutionary/ultimate/guaranteed," no fake
urgency. Describe the real value plainly enough that the right person
recognizes themselves and the wrong person walks away. Include the limits.
```

*Tip:* Including the limits ("what it does NOT do") increases trust and reduces refunds. Counterintuitive, but it works.

### Prompt 15 — Reply to feedback or a review thoughtfully
*Use it when:* you got criticism and want to respond well, not defensively.

```
I received this feedback: [PASTE].
Help me respond. First tell me, honestly, what's fair in it. Then draft a
short reply that acknowledges the fair part, stays calm, and either fixes
something or explains my reasoning without being defensive. Don't grovel
and don't argue.
```

*Tip:* Read the "what's fair" part before the draft. Often the reply matters less than seeing the point clearly.

## 4. Admin & Operations groundwork

> A note on scope: AI shouldn't be your accountant, lawyer, or system of record. These prompts help you *prepare* admin work — draft, structure, and think it through — so the final filing/decision is faster and cleaner. Verify anything with legal or financial consequences.

### Prompt 16 — Draft a simple process you keep redoing
*Use it when:* a task recurs and you want it written down once.

```
I keep doing this task and want a repeatable checklist: [TASK].
Here's how I currently do it, messily: [STEPS / BRAIN DUMP].
Turn it into a clean, numbered checklist a slightly-distracted version of me
could follow without thinking. Mark any step where I tend to make mistakes.
Keep it short — if it's longer than the task, it's wrong.
```

*Tip:* Save these checklists in one place. After ~5, you have an operations manual you never sat down to write.

### Prompt 17 — Structure your expense and income categories
*Use it when:* your records are a mess and you want a usable structure (not tax advice).

```
I run a one-person [BUSINESS TYPE]. Help me set up a simple, consistent set
of categories for tracking income and expenses month to month.
Keep it minimal — the fewest categories that still tell me where money goes.
For each category, give a one-line definition and one example so I categorize
consistently. This is for my own bookkeeping clarity; I'll confirm tax
treatment with a professional.
```

*Tip:* Consistency beats granularity. Ten clear categories you'll actually use beat forty you won't.

### Prompt 18 — Draft a quote or estimate structure
*Use it when:* a client asks "how much?" and you freeze.

```
A client wants [WORK]. Help me structure a clear quote.
Here's what's involved: [SCOPE]. My rough time estimate: [TIME].
Lay out the quote with: what's included, assumptions I'm making, what would
change the price, and what's out of scope. Leave the actual numbers as
[PLACEHOLDERS] for me to fill — just give me the structure and the
"out of scope" lines I tend to forget.
```

*Tip:* The "out of scope" and "what would change the price" lines prevent the unpaid scope creep that quietly kills solo margins.

### Prompt 19 — Prepare for a recurring admin task you dread
*Use it when:* something boring is overdue.

```
I've been avoiding this admin task: [TASK]. It's due [WHEN].
Break it into the smallest possible steps, starting with one I can do in
under 5 minutes right now. Tell me what I need gathered before I start so I
don't stall halfway. Keep it unintimidating.
```

*Tip:* The "gather first" step is why admin stalls — you start, hit a missing document, and quit. Front-load the gathering.

### Prompt 20 — Draft a policy in plain language
*Use it when:* you need a refund/cancellation/booking policy and don't want to copy a scary template.

```
Help me write a plain-language [refund / cancellation / booking] policy for
my one-person [BUSINESS].
How I actually want to handle it: [YOUR INTENT IN PLAIN WORDS].
Write it so a customer feels treated fairly and I'm protected from the
obvious problems. Keep it human, not legalistic. Flag anything I should run
past a professional before publishing.
```

*Tip:* This drafts the *intent* clearly; have a professional check anything binding. AI is good at clarity, not at being your lawyer.

## 5. Customer & Sales communication

### Prompt 21 — Write a first reply to an inquiry
*Use it when:* someone reached out and you want to respond well, fast.

```
Someone sent this inquiry: [PASTE].
Draft a warm, brief reply that answers what they asked, shows I understood
their situation, and proposes a clear next step. Don't over-pitch. If their
message is missing info I need to help them, ask for it in one simple
question at the end.
```

*Tip:* "One simple question at the end" keeps the conversation moving without overwhelming them. Multiple questions stall replies.

### Prompt 22 — Handle a price objection without discounting on reflex
*Use it when:* a prospect says it's too expensive.

```
A prospect said my price for [OFFER] is too high. Context: [WHAT THEY SAID].
Help me respond. First, tell me whether this is likely a real budget issue,
a value-perception issue, or a negotiating move. Then draft a reply for the
most likely case that holds my price by clarifying value or adjusting scope —
not by cutting the price reflexively. Give me one fallback if they truly
can't afford it.
```

*Tip:* Diagnosing the *type* of objection first is what stops you from discounting out of nervousness. Most "too expensive" is value, not budget.

### Prompt 23 — Say no, or set a boundary, kindly
*Use it when:* you need to decline work, an extra request, or a bad-fit client.

```
I need to decline / push back on this: [SITUATION].
Draft a short, kind, firm message that says no clearly, gives a brief honest
reason (not an over-explanation), and where possible offers an alternative
or referral. Don't leave a door open that I want closed.
```

*Tip:* Over-explaining invites negotiation. The AI tends to over-soften — tell it "firmer" if the draft wobbles.

### Prompt 24 — Follow up without being annoying
*Use it when:* someone went quiet and you don't want to nag.

```
I'm following up with [WHO] about [WHAT]. Last contact was [WHEN] and the
status is [STATUS].
Write a short, low-pressure follow-up that makes it easy for them to reply
even if the answer is no. Give them an easy out. One message, not a guilt trip.
```

*Tip:* "Give them an easy out" gets more replies than pressure does — including the honest no's that free up your pipeline.

### Prompt 25 — Turn a finished project into a referral or testimonial ask
*Use it when:* you delivered good work and want it to compound.

```
I just finished [WORK] for a happy client. Help me ask for either a short
testimonial or an introduction to someone similar.
Draft a brief, genuine message that's easy to say yes to — ideally one where
I make it almost effortless for them (e.g. by drafting something they can
edit). Don't be pushy and give them a graceful way to decline.
```

*Tip:* Offering to draft the testimonial *for* them (which they then edit) dramatically raises the yes rate. Make saying yes a 30-second task.

## 6. Personal productivity & decision-making

### Prompt 26 — Plan a realistic day, not a fantasy one
*Use it when:* your to-do list is longer than your day.

```
Here's everything on my plate today: [DUMP IT ALL].
I realistically have [HOURS] focused hours. Help me pick what actually gets
done today vs. what I'm pretending I'll do. Order the real list by what
matters most, protect one block for the single highest-leverage thing, and
tell me plainly what to drop or defer. Don't fit it all in — cut.
```

*Tip:* The value is in the cutting. If the AI fits everything, say "No — what do I drop?"

### Prompt 27 — Think through a problem out loud
*Use it when:* you're going in circles in your own head.

```
I'm stuck thinking about [PROBLEM]. Here's the mess in my head: [BRAIN DUMP].
Reflect it back to me: what's the actual question underneath this, what am I
treating as fixed that might not be, and what are 2–3 genuinely different
paths forward? Ask me one clarifying question before you answer if it would
change your response.
```

*Tip:* Letting it ask *you* a question first turns the AI from an answer machine into a thinking partner. Use this for the foggy problems.

### Prompt 28 — End-of-week honest review
*Use it when:* you want to learn from the week without spiraling.

```
Help me review my week as a solo operator. Here's what happened: [NOTES —
wins, misses, how I felt, what I avoided].
Give me: one thing that worked I should keep, one honest pattern worth
noticing (without being harsh), and one small change for next week. Keep it
constructive and brief. I want clarity, not a lecture.
```

*Tip:* Do this in 5 minutes, weekly. The compounding insight beats any productivity system. The "not a lecture" instruction keeps it usable on hard weeks.

## Appendix

### A. The reusable context block

Paste this at the top of a conversation once, and most prompts above get sharper because the AI knows your situation:

```
Context about me (reuse this): I run a one-person [TYPE] business.
My customers are [WHO]. I sell [WHAT]. My main constraints are time and
[OTHER]. My style preference: [plain / warm / formal / etc.], no hype.
When you help me, be concrete, flag uncertainty, and don't invent facts I
didn't give you. If a task needs info I haven't provided, ask for it.
```

*Why this works:* every prompt assumes context. Providing it once removes the generic-advice problem that makes AI feel useless.

### B. The self-review prompt (use on AI's own output)

Before you ship anything an AI helped write:

```
Review the text below as a skeptical reader. Flag: anything that overclaims
or sounds like hype, anything that reads as generic or could've been written
about any business, and any fact that I should double-check before publishing.
List the issues — don't rewrite it yet.
[PASTE THE OUTPUT]
```

*Why this works:* AI's first drafts drift toward generic and overconfident. This catches both before they reach a customer.

### C. What AI should *not* do for you (an honest list)

Tools like this oversell themselves. So here's where to keep a human in charge:

- **Final legal, tax, and financial decisions.** AI drafts and structures; a professional confirms anything binding or filed.
- **Facts you'll state publicly.** AI can be confidently wrong. Verify claims before you publish or pitch them (Prompt 9 helps).
- **Your actual customer relationships.** AI drafts the message; *you* read the room. Don't send replies you haven't read.
- **Judgment calls about your own life and risk.** AI is a thinking partner, not a decision-maker. The call is yours.
- **Anything you can't check.** If you can't evaluate whether the output is right, don't ship it on trust.

Used this way — as a fast, tireless junior colleague you supervise — AI genuinely changes what one person can run. Used as an oracle you obey, it quietly produces generic, sometimes-wrong work with your name on it. The prompts above are built for the first way.
