### Reddit投稿②（→ EN-K2 The Quiet Productivity Edge / EN-K1 補足）
> **想定サブ**：r/productivity（または r/Entrepreneur の宣伝許可曜日）。**多くの生産性系サブは直リンク販売に厳しい**ため、
> **本文は完全に価値提供で完結させ、リンクは"I wrote a book on this"の1行＋末尾のみ**。リンク不可のサブなら**リンクを外して投稿**（コメントで聞かれたら案内）。

**Title:**
```
The single rule that made AI actually save me time at work (instead of creating more cleanup)
```

**Body:**
```
I wasted my first month with AI tools because I used them backwards — asking for finished work, then spending
longer fixing the confident-but-wrong parts than if I'd written it myself.

The fix was one rule: the AI drafts, I stay the editor. I never ask it to *decide* — only to give me a first
pass I can cut down. A few habits that came out of that:

- Always give it a real example of "good" before asking for output. Vague prompts get vague drafts.
- Ask for a draft I can delete from, not a final I have to trust. Editing is faster than verifying.
- Keep anything sensitive (client names, numbers, contracts) out of the prompt — mask it first.
- Treat every fact, name, and figure as unverified until I've checked it. AI is fluent, not reliable.
- Decide up front what I will *never* delegate: judgment, the send button, and accountability.

This works across email, documents, slides, research, meetings, and translation — the pattern is the same.
The time savings are real, but they come from the discipline, not the tool.

(Disclosure: I expanded this into a short, practical book — no hype, no "AI does your job" promises, $5.99 on
Kindle. Sharing the rules above because they stand on their own; the book is just the longer version.)
→【Kindle:EN-Productivity】
```
